# V2K Evidence Matrix

## Executive summary

“Voice-to-skull” is not one claim. It is a bundle of claims that range from well-supported biophysics to unproven operational allegations. The microwave auditory effect, also called RF hearing or the Frey effect, is real: brief, intense RF or microwave pulses absorbed in the head can produce perceived sounds through thermoacoustically induced acoustic waves that reach the cochlea ([Frontiers in Public Health](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/)). James Lin’s review describes the effect as a recognized biological phenomenon in which a single microwave pulse can be perceived as a zip, click, or knock, while pulse trains may be perceived as buzzes, chirps, or tunes ([IEEE Journal of Electromagnetics, RF and Microwaves in Medicine and Biology](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9366412/)).

A newly reviewed DOE/Battelle Pacific Northwest Laboratory report, “Compilation and assessment of microwave bioeffects,” further strengthens the mechanism-and-dosimetry tier: it treats RF hearing/Frey effect as a well-confirmed datum, describes a single 10-microsecond microwave pulse absorbed in the head as capable of producing an acoustic response, and emphasizes that pulsed fields can matter differently from continuous-wave fields when assessing biological effects ([OSTI](https://www.osti.gov/biblio/6226719), [official OSTI PDF](https://www.osti.gov/servlets/purl/1069245)).

The public evidence also contains patents and concept records that explicitly claim or define RF-based speech or sound delivery to a person’s head. Brunkan’s “Hearing system” patent claims audio-modulated microwave bursts that can create perceived hearing in a person whose head is irradiated ([US4877027A](https://patents.google.com/patent/US4877027A/en)). An Air Force-assigned patent claims an apparatus for audibly communicating speech using the “radio frequency hearing effect” with preprocessing and modulation intended to produce intelligible subjective sound ([US6587729B2](https://patents.google.com/patent/US6587729B2/en)). A Federation of American Scientists mirror of a Center for Army Lessons Learned thesaurus entry defines “voice to skull” as a nonlethal-weapon concept involving microwave transmission of sound into the skull or a silent-sound device, but it is a definition entry, not deployment proof ([FAS / CALL thesaurus mirror](https://sgp.fas.org/othergov/dod/vts.html)).

The strongest negative finding is that open evidence reviewed here does not establish a reliable, covert, operational system that can deliver clear conversational speech to selected individuals in real-world conditions. The main weaponization review concludes that microwave systems can induce unexpected or frightening auditory sensations, but weaponization appears unlikely for effect-size and practicality reasons, and available public information does not establish covert deployment or intelligible speech performance ([Frontiers in Public Health](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/)). MEDUSA is a real Navy SBIR Phase I record for a WaveBand microwave-auditory nonlethal concept, but it supports program/prototype intent rather than Phase II continuation, fielding, or operational voice-to-skull use ([archived Navy SBIR record](https://web.archive.org/web/20080409063721/http://www.navysbirprogram.com/NavySearch/Summary/summary.aspx?pk=F5B07D68-1B19-4235-B140-950CE2E19D08)).

## Claim tiers

| Tier | Claim being tested | Current status | Evidence standard needed |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| T1: RF hearing mechanism | Pulsed RF/microwave energy can create perceived sound without ordinary airborne sound. | Strongly supported. | Controlled psychophysical and biophysical evidence. |
| T2: intelligible semantic speech | RF hearing can reliably transmit words or sentences with measurable intelligibility. | Strengthened as a historical claim: Justesen 1975 reports Sharp/Grove nine-word receiverless speech communication, but protocol-grade evidence is still not established in the open record reviewed here. | Word/sentence tests, sham controls, subject counts, error rates, replication, exposure parameters. |
| T3: patents and programs | Government, inventors, or contractors explored systems intended to transmit sound/speech or nonlethal effects. | Supported. | Patents, SBIR records, concept documents, procurement records. |
| T4: operational deployment | A fielded system was used against real targets as alleged. | Not established in the open record reviewed here. | System identity, deployment records, operational tests, chain of custody, independent corroboration. |
| T5: symptom/legal/media context | Reported experiences, pleadings, journalism, and policy concern exist. | Context only. | Useful for leads, not proof of mechanism or attribution by itself. |

The central mistake in weak V2K arguments is tier collapse: evidence for T1 or T3 is treated as if it proves T2 or T4. A patent can prove that an inventor claimed an apparatus and that a patent office granted claims, but it does not prove a robust device was built, tested, deployed, or used against a specific person. A concept entry can prove that terminology existed inside nonlethal-weapons vocabulary, but it does not prove operational use.

## Speech-evidence trace

The microwave auditory effect is a plausible pathway for perceived internal sound because RF pulses can cause rapid thermoelastic expansion in tissue, launching acoustic transients that reach the cochlea ([Frontiers in Public Health](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/)). That mechanism explains why clicks, knocks, buzzes, chirps, or tone-like perceptions can occur under the right pulse parameters ([IEEE](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9366412/)).

Speech is a harder claim. A click or chirp is a low-information perceptual endpoint; intelligible speech requires controlled modulation that preserves enough spectral-temporal structure for phonemes, syllables, words, and sentences to be decoded by the listener. The Air Force and Brunkan patents claim ways to encode audio or speech using RF hearing effects, but a patent claim is not equivalent to a blinded study showing word-error rates, subject-to-subject robustness, safety margins, and range constraints ([US4877027A](https://patents.google.com/patent/US4877027A/en), [US6587729B2](https://patents.google.com/patent/US6587729B2/en)).

The Sharp/Grove/Gandhi primary paper located in this pass is a mechanism paper titled “Generation of Acoustic Signals by Pulsed Microwave Energy,” not a protocol-grade speech-intelligibility study in the accessible record ([IEEE](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1128293/)). The full Justesen 1975 article materially strengthens the speech trail because it confirms the famous passage in a mainstream, peer-reviewed psychology journal: Justesen reports that Sharp and Grove recorded spoken digit words, used the negative zero-crossings of the voice analog to trigger brief microwave pulses, and could hear, identify, and distinguish nine words from the resulting “voice-modulated” microwaves ([APA PsycNet DOI record](https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.30.3.391)). It also reports that longer messages were not attempted because the averaged energy density would approach the 10 mW/cm² safe-exposure limit ([APA PsycNet DOI record](https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.30.3.391)).

That upgrade still does not close T2 at a modern evidentiary standard. Justesen’s article cites the Sharp/Grove speech demonstration as “Personal communication, September 28, 1973,” which means the full-text article is a published secondary report of the experiment, not the underlying Walter Reed/ARPA technical report. It provides the broad encoding concept and the outcome claim, but not a full protocol with RF frequency, pulse width, power density, subject count, sham controls, blinding, scoring method, error distribution, safety measurements, or independent replication.

A targeted hunt for the underlying Walter Reed / ARPA technical report was conducted on 2026-04-29 across the DTIC public catalog, Lin’s 1978 monograph bibliography, Lin’s 2022 IEEE review, the Foster/Garrett/Ziskin 2021 weaponization review, and the Naval Medical Research Institute Glaser 1976 RF/microwave bioeffects bibliography. **No separately-authored Sharp/Grove speech-protocol technical report was located in any of those sources** ([SHARP_GROVE_PRIMARY_REPORT_HUNT.md](findings/investigation-dig/SHARP_GROVE_PRIMARY_REPORT_HUNT.md)). The only Sharp+Grove primary publication indexed in the open record is the 1974 IEEE T-MTT mechanism paper “Generation of acoustic signals by pulsed microwave energy” (Vol. 22, pp. 583-584). Lin 1978 himself, writing three years after Justesen 1975 with direct contact with the field, cites the speech demonstration only via “(see Justesen, 1975)” and lists no separate primary report in his bibliography. The most parsimonious reading of the open-record evidence is that the speech demonstration was communicated to Justesen as a personal communication on 28 September 1973, that Justesen 1975 is the principal published anchor, and that any separate primary technical report — if one was written — is either classified (consistent with the Bizarre special-access compartment attached to Pandora’s most sensitive behavioral results), an internal WRAIR / ARPA deliverable not deposited with DTIC under Sharp/Grove joint authorship, or simply was never produced as a standalone technical report. This is a strong negative finding on the closure target while preserving the Justesen 1975 published account intact.

The AD-A090426 trail is similar. Secondary pages describe a 1980 Army Mobility Equipment Command/NASA Technical Reports record titled “Effects of low power microwaves on the local cerebral blood flow of conscious rats,” and quote an abstract saying that decoy/deception applications considered low-power pulsed microwaves and that “by proper choice of pulse characteristics, intelligible speech may be created” ([omega.twoday.net mirror](https://omega.twoday.net/stories/299953)). The official NTRS page and direct PDF were not found in this pass, so the quote should be treated as an archival target to verify, not as a confirmed primary-source conclusion.

The ARPA/DARPA Speech Understanding Research line should be treated as a negative control, not a hidden V2K proof chain. The uploaded SUR, Hearsay, Klatt/JASA, and CACM materials describe ordinary automatic speech recognition: speech enters through microphones, is digitized into acoustic features, and is decoded with grammars, search algorithms, hidden Markov models, and later neural-network methods ([Communications of the ACM](https://cacm.acm.org/research/a-historical-perspective-of-speech-recognition/)). Raj Reddy’s ACM biography likewise frames Hearsay, Harpy, and Dragon as systems for recognizing continuous speech and as ancestors of modern commercial speech-recognition technology, with no microwave auditory or directed-energy component ([ACM Turing Award biography](https://amturing.acm.org/award_winners/reddy_6247682.cfm)). These documents are useful because they prevent a false merge between “speech understanding” and “voice-to-skull”: they show that the ARPA speech program was about computers understanding spoken audio, not RF-induced perception inside a listener’s head.

The current speech-trace assessment is therefore stronger than before but still narrower than many V2K summaries claim. The mechanism is strong, the Justesen article confirms a published historical claim of nine-word receiverless microwave speech communication, and patents/program records show serious interest; however, no open primary source located here provides a modern closure-grade demonstration of reliable conversational speech with documented subjects, controls, error rates, exposure parameters, safety margins, and independent replication.

## Safety and dosimetry context

The 1978 DOE/Battelle review is important because it puts RF hearing into a standards-and-threshold framework rather than treating it as folklore. The report states that a single acoustically detectable microwave pulse can be a limiting case for a biological effect and argues that a strict “no-effect” exposure limit would be impractical because RF hearing can occur at extremely small averaged exposure when a brief pulse is averaged over an eight-hour workday ([OSTI](https://www.osti.gov/biblio/6226719), [official OSTI PDF](https://www.osti.gov/servlets/purl/1069245)).

The report also reinforces a key interpretive rule for this investigation: averaged power density alone is not enough. It distinguishes continuous-wave, sine-modulated, and pulse-modulated fields; notes that rectangularly pulsed microwave radiation has a perception threshold near 10 microjoules per gram per pulse; and says the perceptual threshold for continuous-wave and sinusoidally modulated radiations is considerably higher because they lack the sharp “attack” and high peak density required for the presumed thermoelastic pressure wave behind RF hearing ([official OSTI PDF](https://www.osti.gov/servlets/purl/1069245)).

That does not move the speech claim to closure. The DOE/Battelle report cites Justesen 1975 and RF-hearing literature, but it does not reproduce the Sharp/Grove speech experiment, does not report word-recognition scores, and does not supply the missing Walter Reed/ARPA protocol. Its proper evidentiary role is to strengthen T1 and the safety/dosimetry frame, not to prove T2 conversational speech or T4 operational use ([OSTI](https://www.osti.gov/biblio/6226719), [official OSTI PDF](https://www.osti.gov/servlets/purl/1069245)).

## Program evidence

MEDUSA is real in the limited, documentable sense that a Navy SBIR Phase I record exists. The archived record names WaveBand Corporation, contract `M67854-04-C-1012`, topic `N03-163`, Phase I, and describes a breadboard prototype concept for “Mob Excess Deterrent Using Silent Audio” based on the microwave auditory effect ([archived Navy SBIR record](https://web.archive.org/web/20080409063721/http://www.navysbirprogram.com/NavySearch/Summary/summary.aspx?pk=F5B07D68-1B19-4235-B140-950CE2E19D08)). The record reports hardware design/build activity and observed experimental evidence of MAE, but it does not show a fielded voice-to-skull system or semantic speech capability ([archived Navy SBIR record](https://web.archive.org/web/20080409063721/http://www.navysbirprogram.com/NavySearch/Summary/summary.aspx?pk=F5B07D68-1B19-4235-B140-950CE2E19D08)).

EPIC is related but distinct. SBIR.gov records show Invocon’s EPIC Phase I under contract `M67854-04-C-1013` and EPIC Phase II under contract `M67854-05-C-6509`, with the Phase II record tied to topic `N03-163` and a 2005-2007 performance period ([SBIR EPIC Phase I](https://www.sbir.gov/awards/62073), [SBIR EPIC Phase II](https://www.sbir.gov/awards/62074)). The EPIC patent names Invocon inventors and a government-support clause for `M67854-04-C-1013`, but it does not mention MEDUSA, WaveBand, microwave auditory effect, Sadovnik, or Manasson ([EPIC patent](https://patents.justia.com/patent/7841989)).

The best reading is that MEDUSA supports program-intent evidence for an MAE-based nonlethal concept, while EPIC supports a related RF nonlethal personnel-interdiction branch with clearer public Phase II continuation. Neither source, by itself, proves covert semantic V2K deployment.

## AHI evidence

Anomalous Health Incident evidence is relevant but must not be overread. The National Academies concluded that many distinctive acute signs and symptoms reported by some Department of State personnel were consistent with directed pulsed RF energy, while emphasizing limited access to information, heterogeneous cases, and no ability to assess a source, scenario, actor, or device ([National Academies](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/read/25889/chapter/1)). That supports RF bioeffects as a serious hypothesis in a specific medical-policy context, not V2K speech delivery.

The ODNI’s December 2024 update is even more cautious on attribution. Most intelligence components assessed it was very unlikely that a foreign adversary was responsible for reported possible AHIs, while a minority retained low-confidence possibilities involving RF antipersonnel capability or a small undetermined subset of cases ([ODNI](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf)). The ODNI assessment does not establish a voice-to-skull system or operational V2K use ([ODNI](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf)).

## What would prove the stronger claim

For T2, the stronger “intelligible speech V2K” claim would require a primary source with all of the following: full methods, exposure parameters, modulation scheme, subject count, sham controls, blinding, word or sentence intelligibility scoring, confusion/error rates, safety data, and independent replication. A declassified technical report could also matter if it contains comparable rigor and enough experimental detail to evaluate performance. Because the full Justesen 1975 article has now been located and cites the speech demonstration to Sharp and Grove personal communication, the highest-value current targets are the underlying Walter Reed/ARPA Sharp-Grove report and the primary AD-A090426/NASA/Army record rather than later internet excerpts ([APA PsycNet DOI record](https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.30.3.391), [IEEE](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1128293/), [omega.twoday.net mirror](https://omega.twoday.net/stories/299953)).

For T4, the stronger “operational deployment” claim would require a specific system identity, procurement or fielding record, mission-representative test results, dated use documentation, a chain linking tested system to deployed system to alleged event, and independent corroboration. Without those gates, the correct classification is “mechanism support,” “program interest,” or “context,” not proof of deployment.

For case attribution, the evidentiary bar is even higher. A specific actor-specific person claim would require general causation, specific exposure evidence, a spatiotemporal nexus, device/system access by the alleged actor, chain-of-custody for measurements, and exclusion of more likely alternatives. Personal reports can be important leads, but they do not close attribution without instrumented and independently corroborated evidence.

## Practical next steps

The highest-value next research target is now the underlying Walter Reed or ARPA Sharp/Grove technical report, lab record, or correspondence file. The key questions are whether the experiment had controls, how many subjects participated, what the exact word list and scoring method were, what RF parameters were used, and whether the effect was independently replicated ([APA PsycNet DOI record](https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.30.3.391), [IEEE](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1128293/)).

The second target is AD-A090426. The official NTRS/NTIS/CASI/DTIC record should be recovered to verify the abstract, distinguish “rats and cerebral blood flow” from “human intelligible speech,” and determine whether the speech line was a background motivation, a speculative application, or an experimentally demonstrated result ([omega.twoday.net mirror](https://omega.twoday.net/stories/299953)).

The third target is a patent-to-experiment audit. The Brunkan and Air Force patents should be mapped against open experimental literature to determine whether their claimed speech systems were ever built, tested with human subjects, or cited in government technical reports ([US4877027A](https://patents.google.com/patent/US4877027A/en), [US6587729B2](https://patents.google.com/patent/US6587729B2/en)).

The fourth target is MEDUSA documentation. A FOIA or archival search for the WaveBand Phase I final report, technical evaluation, closeout package, and any Phase II evaluation or denial memorandum would materially clarify whether MEDUSA remained a concept, failed for technical/safety reasons, or moved into a nonpublic path ([archived Navy SBIR record](https://web.archive.org/web/20080409063721/http://www.navysbirprogram.com/NavySearch/Summary/summary.aspx?pk=F5B07D68-1B19-4235-B140-950CE2E19D08)).

## Bottom line

V2K is real if the term means “RF or microwave-induced auditory perception.” It is supported by scientific literature, patents, nonlethal-weapons terminology, and now an official DOE/Battelle bioeffects review that treats RF hearing as a well-confirmed threshold phenomenon. V2K has stronger historical support for limited word-level speech than the previous pass could confirm because Justesen 1975 reports the Sharp/Grove nine-word “voice-modulated” microwave demonstration in a published article. V2K is still not proven in the public record reviewed here if the term means “a reliable covert system that transmits conversational speech into targeted people’s heads and has been operationally deployed.” The Sharp/Grove/Justesen and AD-A090426 trails remain important, but they are not yet closure-grade primary proof. The investigation should therefore proceed with a tiered standard: accept the mechanism, treat Justesen as published historical support for limited word-level speech, track patents and programs as intent/context, and reserve judgment on conversational reliability and deployment until primary protocol-grade evidence appears.
