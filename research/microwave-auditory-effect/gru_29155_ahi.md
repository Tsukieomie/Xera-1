# GRU Unit 29155 and Anomalous Health Incidents (Havana Syndrome)

*Research note: This document distinguishes between what was **reported** by journalists, what was **stated** by named officials, and what has been **independently confirmed** by governments or courts. Much of the evidentiary chain remains classified or relies on intercepted communications and commercial travel databases whose provenance is not fully public.*

---

## 1. What Is GRU Unit 29155?

GRU Unit 29155 — formally the 161st Specialist Training Center — is Russia's most operationally aggressive foreign clandestine unit, dedicated exclusively to kinetic "active measures": assassinations, political sabotage, and destabilization campaigns abroad. [European intelligence services believe](https://www.bbc.com/news/uk-56790053) the unit numbers roughly 200 personnel, with around 20 conducting overseas operations and the rest serving in support roles. It is thought to have been reorganized and expanded in its current form around 2007–2008 under its founding commander, Maj. Gen. Andrey Averyanov, who by 2025 had risen to the role of a senior GRU deputy director. [Grey Dynamics analysis](https://greydynamics.com/gru-unit-29155-russias-foreign-covert-action-machine/) places the unit within the GRU's Department for Special Tasks (SSD), which reports directly to GRU leadership and, through it, to the Kremlin.

The unit first entered public consciousness in 2018 through investigative work by Bellingcat and [The Insider](https://theins.press/en/politics/270425), which identified two of the three GRU officers responsible for the Novichok poisoning of former double agent Sergei Skripal and his daughter Yulia in Salisbury, England, in March 2018. The two men — operating under the cover aliases "Ruslan Boshirov" and "Alexander Petrov" — were subsequently identified as Col. Anatoly Chepiga and Dr. Alexander Mishkin. A third officer, Maj. Gen. Denis Sergeev (alias "Sergei Fedotov"), was later identified as the operational commander of the mission. [BBC reporting](https://www.bbc.com/news/uk-56790053) confirmed these identifications alongside Czech government findings.

A consistent pattern of attributed operations includes:

- **Skripal poisoning (2018):** Novichok nerve agent applied to the door handle of Skripal's Salisbury home. Both Skripals survived; a civilian, Dawn Sturgess, died later from secondary exposure. Czech, British, and EU governments formally attributed the attack to Unit 29155. ([Bellingcat](https://www.bellingcat.com/news/2021/04/20/senior-gru-leader-directly-involved-with-czech-arms-depot-explosion/))
- **Bulgarian arms dealer poisoning (2015):** Denis Sergeev and other Unit 29155 members made two separate trips to Sofia to poison Emilian Gebrev, a Bulgarian manufacturer whose company was selling ammunition to Georgia and Ukraine. CCTV placed Sergeev in the parking garage of Gebrev's office building. Gebrev survived.
- **Czech ammunition depot explosions (2014):** Czech authorities announced in April 2021 that Unit 29155 operatives — including the same "Petrov" and "Boshirov" personas later connected to Salisbury — planted explosives that destroyed the Vrbětice munitions depot in October 2014, killing two Czech civilians and destroying warehoused ammunition believed destined for Ukraine. [Bellingcat/Insider/Der Spiegel reporting](https://www.bellingcat.com/news/2021/04/20/senior-gru-leader-directly-involved-with-czech-arms-depot-explosion/) established that commander Averyanov was in Vienna at the time of the blast. Czech police formally completed their investigation in April 2024, and a European Arrest Warrant was issued for Averyanov in May 2024.
- **Montenegro coup attempt (2016):** Unit operatives Eduard Shishmakov and Vladimir Popov allegedly provided weapons, funding, and coordination to local proxies in an attempt to overthrow the Montenegrin government on the night of NATO accession. The plot was foiled after a tip to Western intelligence.
- **Moldova destabilization and other operations:** The unit has been linked to influence operations across former Soviet space, as well as to a series of arms-depot explosions in Bulgaria starting in 2011.

## 2. The March/April 2024 Joint Investigation

The principal investigative report in this domain was published on **March 31, 2024**, not March 2023. The investigation was a joint product of [The Insider](https://theins.press/en/politics/270425) (reporters Christo Grozev and Roman Dobrokhotov), [Der Spiegel](https://www.dw.com/en/havana-syndrome-linked-to-russian-intelligence-report/a-68714812), and CBS's [60 Minutes](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/havana-syndrome-culprit-investigation-new-evidence-60-minutes-transcript/) (correspondent Scott Pelley), following five years of reporting on AHIs. The broadcast aired on 60 Minutes on **March 31, 2024**, and drew over **10 million viewers**, making it the [most-watched program of that week](https://www.forbes.com/sites/maryroeloffs/2024/04/05/60-minutes-episode-on-havana-syndrome-was-last-weeks-most-watched-program-beating-unc-and-houston-ncaa-games/), surpassing NCAA tournament viewership. The investigation later [won an Emmy Award for Outstanding Investigative News Coverage](https://theins.press/en/news/282528) in June 2025.

The investigators described the work as "a yearlong investigation" that produced:

1. **Travel records and phone metadata** placing Unit 29155 operatives in the same cities as reported AHI incidents.
2. **A Russian intelligence accounting document** obtained by Grozev showing that a Unit 29155 officer received a bonus for work on the "potential capabilities of non-lethal acoustic weapons in combat activities in urban settings." Grozev called it "the closest to a receipt you can have for this."
3. **Eyewitness identification** of Albert Averyanov — the son of the unit's commander, and himself an active-duty Unit 29155 member — near the home of an AHI victim in Tbilisi, Georgia in October 2021.
4. **Email access logs** placing an account holder in Tbilisi during the incident window, despite the phone being officially switched off.

### Key Locations Documented

- **Frankfurt, Germany (2014):** The investigation reported that at least four Americans experienced AHI symptoms there — the earliest documented incidents, predating the 2016 Havana cases. This coincided with Unit 29155 operatives' preparatory travel patterns for the Czech depot bombing operation.
- **Guangzhou, China (2017):** State Department official Mark Lenzi and Commerce Department official Catherine Werner both reported AHIs. Multiple Unit 29155 members, including Sergey Avdeenko (described as posing as Silk Way Rally staff), were present in China during the same period, according to The Insider's reporting.
- **Tbilisi, Georgia (October 7, 2021):** A U.S. Embassy official's wife, identified only as "Joy," reported a sudden piercing sound entering her left ear while doing laundry. She later identified a photograph of Albert Averyanov as the tall, blond, well-dressed man she saw standing outside her home moments before the incident. Two U.S. government officials separately confirmed to 60 Minutes that Averyanov was in Tbilisi around this time. Sources additionally reported an intercepted phone call in Russian near the incident site: a man asked, "Is it supposed to have blinking green lights?" and "Should I leave it on all night?" — with AHI incidents beginning the following day.
- **Hanoi, Vietnam (August 2021):** The incoming CIA chief of station was reportedly struck while in temporary housing at the Oakwood Residence Suites hotel. The investigation linked this case to a CIA officer previously stationed in Kyiv who had worked closely with Ukrainian intelligence.
- **Vilnius, Lithuania (2023 NATO Summit):** Multiple sources told 60 Minutes that a senior Department of Defense official experienced AHI symptoms during the summit, which was attended by President Biden and focused heavily on Russia's Ukraine invasion.
- **Vienna, Austria:** The investigation documented multiple reported incidents; a classified security camera video from a stairwell in the U.S. Embassy in Vienna allegedly shows two people collapsing, though 60 Minutes noted it had not independently seen this footage.

### The Tbilisi Incident and Albert Averyanov

Albert Averyanov, born around 1998, is the son of Unit 29155's founding commander. [The Insider's reporting](https://theins.press/en/politics/270425) describes him as having been groomed for unit membership since age 16, conducting a 2019 visit to Geneva's rezidentura under the cover of English language studies, and subsequently appearing on travel manifests and in phone metadata alongside known 29155 officers. Grozev described this as making Averyanov "clearly more than just the son of the boss." The identification by "Joy" was corroborated, according to 60 Minutes, by two U.S. government officials who confirmed his presence in Tbilisi in the relevant period — though neither was named and neither said they had personally witnessed any device use.

## 3. The Albany Incident and Vitalii Kovalev

A domestic AHI case tied to Russia through circumstantial evidence involved **Vitalii Kovalev**, a Russian national who had worked as executive chef at prominent Russian restaurants in New York and Washington, D.C. (including the Mari Vanna restaurants).

[The Insider's detailed profile](https://theins.press/en/politics/270450) established that:

- Kovalev was born in St. Petersburg in 1985 and studied at St. Petersburg State Marine Technical University — Russia's leading naval engineering institute — in a military engineering department. His coursework included "electroacoustic transducers" and "digital signal processing techniques." His graduate thesis was "Efficiency of Helicopter Noise Detection Channels."
- While studying, he worked as an engineer at Elektroavtomatika, a subsidiary of MiG, the Ministry of Defense's military aircraft manufacturer.
- Kovalev held a military-secrets clearance rated X3, meaning he could not have left Russia in 2012 to work abroad without FSB authorization. A former senior U.S. counterintelligence official subsequently confirmed to 60 Minutes that Kovalev was known to the U.S. government as both a technical officer for the GRU and a member of the FSB's 16th Directorate (Center 16), a cyber unit whose other members have been indicted for hacking U.S. energy infrastructure.

On June 16, 2020, Kovalev was arrested after a high-speed car chase at 110 mph in the Florida Keys. Deputies found in his bag: a soot-covered paper-burning bowl, notes on multiple bank accounts (including a $75,000 Citibank entry), a Russian passport, and a commercially available device capable of reading and erasing a vehicle's onboard computer data, including GPS records. He had not attempted to flee Russia before the arrest despite his financial resources.

An FBI counterintelligence agent identified only as "Carrie" — described as experienced in Russian operations — spent approximately 80 hours over six months interviewing Kovalev. Kovalev declined to sign a confession to working as a Russian illegal agent and returned to jail. A few months after the interviews concluded, Carrie experienced severe AHI symptoms at her Key West home: a sudden high-pitched metallic drilling sound entering her right ear, accompanied by pressure and pain coursing through her jaw and neck. She experienced a second incident at her subsequent posting in California. She told 60 Minutes: "I assess that I was targeted — because I pissed off the wrong people for doing what I was doing."

Kovalev returned to Russia in September 2022 following his release after 26 months, despite warnings from U.S. officials. He crossed into Ukraine near Luhansk on New Year's Eve 2022. A death certificate was issued on February 8, 2023, citing wounds from "unclarified military actions." His family announced his death on social media on March 1, 2023. Grozev observed that no military investigation was conducted, which is unusual under Russian wartime protocol, and speculated Kovalev may have been sent to the front to be "disposed of." *(Note: The "Albany incident" as a formal category was not established in the published reporting; the Kovalev-related incidents occurred in Key West and California.)*

## 4. The Terentiev Document and the Acoustic Weapons Trail

A central piece of documentary evidence reported by the investigation concerns **Col. Ivan Terentiev**, who served for a decade as deputy commander of Unit 29155. [The Insider obtained](https://theins.press/en/politics/270425) from the email account of Terentiev's aide, Lt. Col. Nikolay Ezhov, an addendum from a financial vetting process showing that Terentiev's government bonus was linked to his research on "the potential capabilities of non-lethal acoustic weapons in combat activities in urban settings." The vetting was connected to Terentiev's appointment as Putin's federal inspector for the Far Eastern Sakhalin region — an unusual elevation for a covert operative, which the investigation suggested reflected recognition for successful operational achievements.

Terentiev co-wrote dozens of military-scientific papers, including one on "the effectiveness of underwater shooting" and separate research exploring acoustic weapons. The Bulgarian government issued a European Arrest Warrant for Terentiev in January 2024 for his personal involvement in Bulgarian arms depot explosions beginning in 2011.

Additionally, Mishkin — one of the Skripal poisoners — is identified as a former student of Sergei Chepur, the head of the Russian Defense Ministry's Research Testing Institute of Military Medicine, which the investigation describes as the GRU's entity for testing the effects of toxic substances on the human body. Chepur is described as a consultant to Unit 29155.

## 5. Russian RF/Directed Energy Research Background

The investigation references Soviet and Russian research on radiofrequency (RF) and acoustic effects on the human body, providing a technical motive argument. Key elements:

- **The Frey Effect:** Since the 1970s, Western scientists — including Prof. James Lin of the University of Illinois — have documented that pulsed microwave energy can create acoustic sensations inside the skull (the microwave auditory effect). This is well-established physics.
- **Soviet/Russian research programs:** Declassified CIA documents reference Soviet research into the antipersonnel uses of microwaves, including potential effects on the blood-brain barrier. Dr. David Relman — a Stanford professor who led two government panels on AHI — has stated publicly that "Russian scientists had been perfecting the concept of a pulsed microwave weapon for decades" and that the key Russian finding was that **pulsing** energy, rather than continuous exposure, produces biological effects by electrically stimulating brain tissue. Research at the Tomsk Institute of High-Current Electronics on pulsed microwaves' effects on animals was specifically cited by the investigation.
- **The Terentiev acoustic weapons document** (described above) is the most direct operational link in the reporting chain.
- **A 2024 classified acquisition:** In a [March 2026 60 Minutes follow-up report](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/havana-syndrome-culprit-investigation-new-evidence-60-minutes-transcript/), three independent government sources described a 2024 classified mission in which undercover Department of Homeland Security agents purchased a miniaturized pulsed microwave device from a Russian criminal network at a cost of approximately $15 million (Pentagon-funded). Sources described the device as portable, concealable, silent, capable of operating by remote control, with a range of several hundred feet that can penetrate windows and drywall. The vital components were described as manufactured in Russia. Tests on rats and sheep at a U.S. military laboratory reportedly produced injuries consistent with AHI symptoms. This device was **not linked to any specific AHI attack** in the reporting; it demonstrated technical plausibility, not attribution.

*Note: The specific devices referred to as "Kropuk," "Krap," or "Slipper" — and the "Tunkin-Vilenskaya" research — do not appear in the published open-source reporting reviewed for this document. These names may reference classified materials, internal government designations, or unverified claims not yet surfaced in open reporting.*

## 6. US Government Responses

### The March 2023 IC Assessment

On March 1, 2023, the ODNI released a joint Intelligence Community Assessment prepared by seven agencies, concluding that it was **"very unlikely"** a foreign adversary was responsible for the reported AHIs. The assessment found no credible evidence that a foreign adversary had a device or weapon causing the symptoms, and attributed reported cases most likely to "preexisting conditions, conventional illnesses, and environmental factors." [PBS NewsHour reported](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/very-unlikely-foreign-adversary-caused-havana-syndrome-u-s-intelligence-says) that intelligence officials told reporters they had found evidence that "adversaries including China and Russia expressed some confusion" when U.S. Havana Syndrome reports emerged — suggesting adversaries thought the U.S. was framing them for something. Key caveats: not all agencies participated; confidence levels varied from "low" to "moderate" at some agencies.

### The December 2024 ODNI Updated Assessment

A [December 2024 ODNI update](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf) maintained the "very unlikely" conclusion for most agencies on the question of foreign adversary responsibility. However, it noted a significant internal division: **one IC component** now assessed it was "likely" a foreign actor has an RF antipersonnel capability that could cause biological effects consistent with some AHI symptoms; **another component** assessed there was a "roughly even chance" such a capability exists. These were capability assessments, not attribution assessments — agencies were splitting on whether Russia had developed such a weapon, not on whether it had deployed one.

### The DIA Investigation (Greg Edgreen)

The Defense Intelligence Agency ran its own investigation from 2021 to 2023, overseen by Lt. Col. Greg Edgreen. Edgreen retired from the Army and spoke publicly to 60 Minutes on March 31, 2024 — his first public statements — saying: "One of the things I started to notice was the caliber of our officer that was being impacted. This wasn't happening to our worst or our middle-range officers. This was happening to our top 5%, 10% performing officers across the Defense Intelligence Agency. And consistently there was a Russia nexus." When asked directly whether America was being attacked, Edgreen replied: "My personal opinion, yes." When asked by whom: "Russia." He added: "If I'm wrong about Russia being behind anomalous health incidents, I will come onto your show and eat my tie." Edgreen's statements represent his personal assessment; he stated he could not disclose classified specifics.

### The House Intelligence Committee (December 2024)

In December 2024, the [House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence's CIA Subcommittee](https://intelligence.house.gov/2024/12/05/crawford-cia-subcommittee-release-interim-report-on-havana-syndrome/), led by Chairman Rick Crawford (R-AR), released an unclassified interim report concluding that "it is increasingly likely a foreign adversary is responsible for some portion of reported AHIs." The report stated the 2023 IC Assessment "lacked analytic integrity and was highly irregular in its formulation," and that the Intelligence Community had "actively attempted to impede" the Subcommittee's investigation. Full Committee Chairman Mike Turner (R-OH) endorsed the findings. A separate classified report was described as containing "very sensitive information gathered over the past two years that was previously unknown to the Committee." The ODNI publicly disagreed, calling the assertion that it withheld contradictory information "unfounded."

### The Senate Intelligence Committee (December 2024)

A [December 2024 bipartisan Senate Intelligence Committee report](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/senate-intelligence-panel-criticizes-cia-response-havana-syndrome-2024-12-27/) did not make new findings on cause or attribution, but sharply criticized the CIA's handling of AHI cases — finding that many affected officers did not receive adequate or timely medical care, and that the CIA's shifting organizational approach and lack of a clear AHI definition had "significantly hindered" consistent treatment and compensation.

### FBI Response

The FBI authorized agent "Carrie" to speak to her personal experience with 60 Minutes but did not allow her to discuss case work. No public FBI attribution statement regarding Unit 29155 and AHIs has been issued. Carrie's case is presented in the reporting as circumstantial evidence (proximity to a suspected GRU technical officer, followed by AHI onset) rather than confirmed attribution.

## 7. The 2023–2024 IC Assessment vs. the Journalist Evidence

A central analytical tension in this story: the journalists' 2024 findings — travel/phone colocation, the Terentiev document, the Kovalev background — were presented as newly surfaced evidence that postdated or was not considered in the March 2023 IC Assessment. Grozev testified at a May 2024 [House Homeland Security Committee hearing](https://homeland.house.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/2024-05-08-CTI-HRG-Testimony.pdf) that the team had established at least 68 AHI incidents that "cannot be explained away with pre-existing conditions or psychosomatic symptoms," and had geolocated Unit 29155 members in proximity to confirmed AHI incidents in at least four cases: Frankfurt (2014), China (2016–2017), and Tbilisi (2021), with further overlaps at Belgrade and Hanoi.

The December 2024 ODNI update made no specific reference to the journalists' findings. ODNI's spokesperson stated that "years of IC collection, targeting, and analytic efforts have not surfaced compelling intelligence reporting that ties a foreign actor to any specific event reported as a possible AHI." Whether the journalists' open-source evidence chain was formally evaluated by IC analysts — and what conclusion they reached — is not publicly known.

## 8. Counter-Arguments and Evidentiary Limitations

The reporting on Unit 29155 and AHIs is significant but not without serious evidentiary gaps. Analysts and skeptics raise the following:

1. **Surveillance presence ≠ attack.** Unit 29155 operatives conduct wide-ranging surveillance and counterintelligence targeting of U.S. government personnel globally. Their presence in the same city — or even same neighborhood — as an AHI victim is consistent with routine espionage coverage and does not independently establish use of a weapon.

2. **Correlation without causation.** The investigation identified 4 "confirmed" location overlaps and noted "further overlaps" from among approximately 68 curated cases. Given the global footprint of Unit 29155 operations and the breadth of reported AHI locations, statistical probability of coincidental overlap has not been formally estimated in open-source reporting.

3. **Weak chain of identification (Tbilisi).** The identification of Albert Averyanov by "Joy" was made from a photograph shown to her approximately three years after the alleged incident. Two anonymous U.S. government sources corroborated his presence in Tbilisi, but no public official has made an on-record attribution. The intercepted phone call about "blinking green lights" has no established connection to any specific device.

4. **The Kovalev link is circumstantial.** No evidence has been published establishing that Kovalev was physically present at either of the locations where "Carrie" experienced AHI symptoms. The connection is: Carrie investigated Kovalev → Kovalev appears to be a GRU technical officer → Carrie later experienced AHI symptoms. This is suggestive but not probative.

5. **Technical feasibility remains contested.** Independent physicists and microwave engineers, including those reviewed by the Russian science outlet [T-invariant](https://t-invariant.org/2026/03/havana-syndrome-forever-the-psychogenic-roots-microwave-mirage-laser-optoacoustic-impact-what-s-going-on/), have argued that building a miniaturized pulsed microwave weapon capable of causing traumatic brain injury at range without disrupting surrounding electronics remains technically extremely difficult or impossible under known physics. The CIA itself reportedly argued for years that such a weapon would need to be the size of a truck — though the 2024 classified acquisition described by 60 Minutes, if accurate, challenges that assumption.

6. **The "very unlikely" IC consensus has not moved on attribution.** As of the December 2024 update, five of the seven participating IC agencies continued to assess it "very unlikely" that a foreign adversary had deployed a weapon against U.S. personnel — even as two agencies moved toward "roughly even chance" on the existence of a Russian capability. The agencies with the most direct collection against Russia (CIA, NSA) have not publicly moved from the "very unlikely" assessment.

7. **Massive case inflation.** The total pool of approximately 1,500 reported AHI cases worldwide has been widely noted — even by AHI advocates — to include many cases almost certainly attributable to conventional illness, anxiety, or environmental factors. The journalists focused on a curated subset of approximately 68 cases meeting stricter criteria, but the broader pool has been used politically in ways that conflate all reported cases with deliberate attacks.

8. **Source limitations.** Key elements of the investigation rely on intercepted Russian intelligence documents, leaked databases, and anonymous U.S. official sources whose classification status prevents independent verification. The Insider operates from exile following Russian government pressure and has strong institutional priors about Russian responsibility.

## 9. Summary Assessment

The joint Insider/Der Spiegel/60 Minutes investigation constitutes the most substantive public evidentiary case to date suggesting Russian involvement in a subset of AHI cases — specifically linking Unit 29155's travel patterns and internal documentation to a directed energy weapons program. The evidence is most compelling in the Tbilisi, Frankfurt, and China cases, where geolocation overlaps are most specific. The Terentiev bonus document represents the strongest single piece of evidence of Unit 29155 internal engagement with acoustic/directed energy weapon development.

However, none of this evidence has been independently confirmed by named U.S. government officials in an on-record attribution. The IC's public position — "very unlikely" — has not changed despite the investigation's publication. The House Intelligence Committee's December 2024 interim report constitutes the most significant official endorsement of a foreign adversary hypothesis, but it is an oversight report, not an intelligence finding, and relies substantially on classified materials not available for independent review.

The December 2024 IC update's acknowledgment that one agency now assesses it "likely" Russia has an RF antipersonnel capability is a meaningful shift — but capability and deployment are separate questions, and the IC has not formally concluded deployment occurred.

The honest characterization of the current state: the journalist evidence presents a **plausible operational theory** with four documented location overlaps, one documentary link to acoustic weapons research, and circumstantial human intelligence. It has not been confirmed or refuted by the classified intelligence picture. Whether the IC's "very unlikely" conclusion reflects genuine absence of evidence, insufficient collection, or — as Attorney Mark Zaid and former CIA officers have alleged — analytical pressure to avoid an uncomfortable conclusion, remains unresolved.
