## 1. Background: Havana Syndrome and the Anomalous Health Incidents File {#background}

Starting in late 2016, American diplomatic and intelligence personnel stationed at the U.S. Embassy in Havana, Cuba began reporting sudden, localized sensory phenomena — intense aural pressure, high-pitched sounds, vertigo — followed by persistent symptoms including traumatic brain injury, cognitive impairment, tinnitus, and gait disturbances. The phenomenon spread far beyond Havana: subsequent incidents were reported in China (Guangzhou, 2017–2018), Russia, Austria, Germany, Georgia, Poland, Vietnam, India, Colombia, and domestically within the United States, including near the White House and in the Washington suburbs.

The U.S. government officially designated these events "Anomalous Health Incidents" (AHIs), a deliberately non-attributive term that sidestepped the question of cause and origin. Colloquially they became known as "Havana Syndrome." As of early 2024, at least 334 U.S. government personnel and family members — across the CIA, FBI, State Department, National Security Council, and military — had qualified for care in the Military Health System, according to [HPSCI reporting](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/havana-syndrome-intelligence-report-questioned-house-committee-60-minutes/). More than 1,500 reports had been made across the government.

The Trump administration's first term initially characterized the incidents as "attacks." By the Biden administration, IC leadership shifted to characterizing the syndrome's causes as ambiguous and most likely non-adversarial — a pivot that became crystallized in the March 2023 ICA.

## 2. The March 2023 ICA: Content, Methodology, and Authorship {#2023-ica}

### Document identity

The March 2023 assessment is formally titled **"Updated Assessment of Anomalous Health Incidents,"** designated [ICA 2023-02286-B](https://www.dni.gov/index.php/newsroom/reports-publications/reports-publications-2023/3673-dni-statement-on-the-intelligence-community-assessment-on-ahis), issued March 1, 2023. It updates an earlier January 2022 interim assessment. Seven U.S. intelligence agencies participated. The document was produced under the auspices of the **National Intelligence Council (NIC)** — the senior analytic body within ODNI — and coordinated across the participating IC components.

### The authoring structure

The ICA was drafted by the NIC. The scope note of the January 2025 follow-up ICA ([ICA 2025-00008-B, released January 10, 2025](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf)) specifies it was "prepared under the auspices of the National Intelligence Officer (NIO) for Counterintelligence." This indicates the 2023 ICA was principally driven by the NIO for Counterintelligence, a senior NIC position. Congressional critics and whistleblowers have alleged — but not publicly specified — that specific CIA analytic units, including the Counterintelligence Mission Center (CIMC) and what has been referenced in classified congressional documents as the Center for Heuristic Intelligence Analysis (or analogous compartmented unit), shaped the product's analytic framing. The precise organizational units within CIA responsible for the 2023 ICA's analytic conclusions have not been publicly confirmed, as the CIA has not voluntarily disclosed them.

### Key judgments

The [unclassified key judgments](https://www.hstoday.us/subject-matter-areas/intelligence/intelligence-community-agencies-find-it-unlikely-that-foreign-adversary-caused-havana-syndrome-cases/) of the 2023 ICA are as follows:

**Line of Inquiry 1 — Foreign Actor Involvement:**
- Five agencies: "**very unlikely**" a foreign adversary is responsible, with moderate-to-high confidence (two agencies) or moderate confidence (three agencies).
- One agency: "**unlikely**" a foreign adversary played a role, with low confidence, citing "collection gaps" and a view that "the IC has failed to detect some adversaries' activities."
- One agency: **abstained**.

**Line of Inquiry 2 — Deliberate Causal Mechanism (e.g., pulsed RF weapon):**
- Most agencies: "**very unlikely**" that deliberate causal mechanisms produced the sensory phenomena. Two agencies had high confidence, three had moderate confidence.
- Two agencies: "**unlikely**" deliberate causal mechanisms were responsible, with **low confidence**, because they judged "radiofrequency (RF) energy is a plausible cause for AHIs, based in part on the findings of the IC Expert Panel."

**Line of Inquiry 3 — Medical Analysis:**
- The IC assessed that U.S. personnel did not present a consistent set of physical injuries. This relied significantly on preliminary NIH study data — a reliance later heavily criticized.

**Bottom line:**
> "In light of this and the evidence that points away from a foreign adversary, causal mechanism, or unique syndrome linked to AHIs, IC agencies assess that symptoms reported by US personnel were probably the result of factors that did not involve a foreign adversary, such as preexisting conditions, conventional illnesses, and environmental factors." — [ODNI DNI Statement, March 1, 2023](https://www.dni.gov/index.php/newsroom/press-releases/press-releases-2023/3674-dni-statement-on-the-intelligence-community-assessment-on-ahis-1692377389)

### Methodology

The ICA employed three lines of inquiry: (1) intelligence collection and targeting data to determine whether foreign adversaries were linked to specific incidents; (2) scientific and technical analysis of whether a foreign adversary possesses a device capable of causing the reported symptoms; and (3) medical evidence for whether victims share a consistent clinical profile. All seven participating agencies reviewed the same underlying intelligence base, with varying confidence levels reflecting differing assessments of collection gaps.

The ICA explicitly committed to revisiting its conclusions "if new medical analysis identified a syndrome linked to affected personnel or the identification of a specific device that both caused the harmful effects described in AHI reports and was fielded by an adversary during the timeframe of the incidents."

### What the IC Expert Panel found — and why its exclusion matters

Separately from the ICA, in February 2022 ODNI released the declassified summary of the **IC Expert Panel** — a group of scientists and specialists with no prior AHI analytic involvement. That panel found that "pulsed electromagnetic energy, particularly in the radiofrequency range, plausibly explains the core characteristics" of a subset of AHI cases and that "known medical and environmental conditions, including psychosocial factors, could not explain some AHI cases with abrupt-onset, location-dependent, audio-vestibular phenomena." The panel's findings diverged markedly from the 2023 ICA's overall framing — a discrepancy that HPSCI's Crawford investigation later cited as a central problem.

### Disputed reliance on the NIH study

The 2023 ICA cited, as a "key factor underpinning its analytic judgment," preliminary results from a major NIH study finding no consistent MRI-detectable brain injury among AHI patients. That study was subsequently shut down in August 2024, after [NIH notified participants](https://treatnow.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/salon.com-Active-suppression-of-witnesses-CIA-lied-about-Havana-Syndrome-whistleblower-documents-reveal.pdf) that "due to concerns about this study raised by some participants," the NIH was terminating it "out of an abundance of caution." Attorney Mark Zaid confirmed that part of the concern was that CIA personnel were being required to participate in the NIH study as a **condition for receiving health care treatment**, which is considered ethically prohibited. The HPSCI investigation later found that the IC "utilized the NIH study's invalid results to prop up the highly flawed 2023 intelligence community assessment."

## 3. Scientific and Expert Context Prior to the ICA {#scientific-context}

The 2023 ICA did not arise in a scientific vacuum. Two landmark prior assessments shaped the contested analytical landscape:

**National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (December 2020):** A blue-ribbon panel, tasked by the State Department, concluded that the symptoms and circumstances of the Havana cluster were "consistent with the effects of directed, pulsed, radiofrequency energy" — the first institutional body to name directed RF energy as the "most plausible" mechanism for a subset of cases. This report became a persistent reference point for critics of the 2023 ICA.

**JASON scientific advisory group (2018, partially declassified):** The JASON group, advising the State Department, reached more cautious conclusions, noting that available data did not support strong mechanistic claims and many cases might have mundane causes. This report was cited by ODNI as underpinning its skepticism of the directed-energy hypothesis.

The [IC Expert Panel (2022)](https://www.dni.gov/index.php/newsroom/press-releases/press-releases-2023/3674-dni-statement-on-the-intelligence-community-assessment-on-ahis-1692377389), as noted above, sided closer to the National Academies position — finding pulsed RF energy a plausible explanation for the "core characteristics" of a subset of cases. That panel's findings, while technically published within the IC process, were not prominently integrated into the March 2023 ICA's overall framing.

## 4. HPSCI CIA Subcommittee Findings — December 2024 {#hpsci-2024}

### Investigation genesis

On February 8, 2024, HPSCI Chairman **Mike Turner** (R-OH) elevated an existing oversight initiative to a formal committee investigation and designated **CIA Subcommittee Chairman Rick Crawford** (R-AR, AR-01) as investigative lead. In the same letter to then-DNI **Avril Haines**, Crawford specified the investigation would focus on: (1) the analytic integrity of intelligence reporting on AHIs; (2) allegations of improper suppression of AHI information within and between executive branch agencies and Congress; and (3) assessed risks to the IC workforce.

By December 2024, the subcommittee had:
- Conducted 48 interviews, including 11 transcribed interviews, of CIA, FBI, ODNI, DIA, and military personnel, plus non-government medical and academic experts;
- Reviewed over 5,000 pages of records produced by CIA, NSA, DIA, and ODNI, plus over 3,400 pages of records provided by whistleblowers;
- Issued a subpoena to NSA in May 2024 after NSA's document production was deemed inadequate.

### The December 5, 2024 unclassified interim report

On December 5, 2024, [Crawford released an unclassified interim report](https://intelligence.house.gov/2024/12/05/crawford-cia-subcommittee-release-interim-report-on-havana-syndrome/) titled *"Investigating the Intelligence Community's Conclusions on Anomalous Health Incidents: Is the Intelligence Community Hiding the Real Reason for This Phenomenon?"* The [full unclassified report is available here](https://intelligence.house.gov/uploadedfiles/unclassified_ahi_report.pdf).

**Key findings:**

**Finding 1:** "The ICA on AHIs lacked analytic integrity and was highly irregular, hindering the Subcommittee's trust in the IC's process and conclusions."

The Subcommittee found evidence of "problems with the ICA's creation, review, and release." Specifically:
> "Some of these problems may include a rush to convey a consensus amongst elements of the IC in an effort to control the narrative with the American public, policymakers, foreign partners and adversaries, and IC employees."

In his *Washington Examiner* op-ed of December 9, 2024, Crawford was more explicit, stating the subcommittee had found that ["there had been overt attempts by management to basically drive a narrative through that analysis — that is to say, here's our statement, now go forth and support our statement. It wasn't about asking those analysts to give us the ground truth."](https://intelligence.house.gov/2024/12/09/icymi-house-intelligence-committee-cia-subcommittee-chairman-rick-crawfords-op-ed-in-the-washington-examiner-we-have-been-misled-on-havana-syndrome/)

**Finding 2:** "The IC's response to AHIs has likely impeded collection." The investigation concluded that the IC's determination that AHIs were not foreign-caused discouraged further collection against the potential threat, creating a self-fulfilling analytic blind spot.

**Finding 3:** "Congress's oversight and investigation must continue with support from the Trump Administration." The subcommittee accused IC leadership under the Biden administration of "stonewalling, slow-walking, and cherry-picking of information" and said "the Biden Administration and IC leadership has sought to hinder the Subcommittee's investigation into AHIs to keep the truth about AHIs from Congress and, by extension, the American public."

### The classified annex

Alongside the public report, Crawford disclosed that he was "finalizing a substantial and highly classified report" involving "very sensitive information gathered over the past two years that was previously unknown to the Committee." This classified interim report was directed at the formal IC channels and has not been publicly released. The unclassified report explicitly stated that without this classified information, a final unclassified report could not yet be produced.

### ODNI's rebuttal

The [ODNI under Haines disputed the subcommittee's findings](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/havana-syndrome-intelligence-report-questioned-house-committee-60-minutes/): "Most IC agencies assess that it is very unlikely a foreign adversary is responsible for the reported AHIs, and the assertion that we are withholding information that contradicts this analysis or would otherwise illuminate this complex subject is unfounded."

### Senate Intelligence Committee parallel report

On December 27, 2024, the Senate Intelligence Committee released a bipartisan report on [the CIA's handling of AHI victim care](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2024/12/27/senate-cia-havana-syndrome/). The SSCI report did not address the attribution question directly, but found that the CIA's "evolving organizational position" had seriously impaired its ability to provide medical care, workers' compensation, and institutional support to affected employees. The report noted that as of December 31, 2023, only 21% of CIA AHI applicants had workers' compensation claims approved, compared to 67% for AHI applicants from other federal agencies. A full [Senate Intelligence Committee report](https://www.intelligence.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/AP_Report_24_01_R.pdf) on CIA's medical response found the agency had discontinued its own clinical research on AHIs, including pre- and post-AHI baseline medical assessments.

## 5. The January 2025 ODNI Updated Assessment {#odni-2025}

On January 9–10, 2025 — eleven days before the end of the Biden administration — ODNI released a second updated ICA: [**"Updated Assessment of Anomalous Health Incidents, as of December 2024,"** ICA 2025-00008-B](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf). Approved for public release on January 9, dated January 10. This was issued under outgoing DNI **Avril Haines**, not under Tulsi Gabbard, who was not confirmed until February 12, 2025.

### Issuing authority

A critical chronological point: the January 2025 ICA was produced and approved entirely under the Biden administration, by the outgoing NIC and ODNI. The ICA was drafted under the National Intelligence Officer for Counterintelligence — the same auspices as the 2023 ICA. This means two sequential administrations' IC assessments now carry the "very unlikely" overall judgment, though the January 2025 update introduced notable shifts.

### What changed and what did not

The overall judgment remained: **five IC components assess it is "very unlikely" a foreign actor caused any of the reported AHI events**; one component assesses it is "unlikely"; one abstains. This is structurally identical to 2023.

However, two components shifted their assessments on the **capability** question:

> "One component assesses it is **'likely' a foreign actor has an RF antipersonnel capability** and that this capability can cause biological effects consistent with some of the symptoms reported as possible AHIs."

> "The other component assesses there is a **'roughly even chance' that a foreign actor has a capability** that could have caused some of the experiences reported as possible AHIs."

Both assessments carry **low confidence**. Notably, even the "likely" capability component did not assess that such a weapon had been used in any specific AHI event; it assessed only that the adversary *possesses* the capability.

Two other IC components — identified by [CNN and the *Washington Examiner*](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/opinion/beltway-confidential/4490516/congress-must-question-intelligence-community-havana-syndrome-cover-up/) as the **NSA and the U.S. Army's Intelligence Command** — were described as dissenting from or modifying their 2023 views based on new reporting about foreign directed-energy research programs. The January 2025 ICA does not publicly name the dissenting agencies; the NSA identification comes from multiple press reports cited by the *Washington Examiner* (March 16, 2026).

### Congressional reaction to the January 2025 update

HPSCI Chairman Turner and CIA Subcommittee Chairman Crawford released a joint statement calling the update ["the Biden Administration's doubled-down cover-up"](https://intelligence.house.gov/2025/01/10/turner-and-crawford-respond-to-biden-administration-covering-up-truth-about-havana-syndrome/):

> "This new intelligence, I believe, should completely change the assessment of our adversaries' capabilities and the risks to our personnel. This will not age well for the Biden Administration."

ODNI, by contrast, characterized the shift as "subtle" — a move from "unlikely with low confidence" to "roughly even chance with low confidence" — and emphasized that no specific incident had been linked to a foreign actor.

## 6. John Ratcliffe at the CIA {#ratcliffe-cia}

### Confirmation

[The Senate confirmed John Ratcliffe as CIA Director on January 23, 2025](https://www.senate.gov/legislative/LIS/roll_call_votes/vote1191/vote_119_1_00013.htm), by a vote of 74–25, with 20 Democrats and independent Angus King voting in favor. It was the second confirmation of the second Trump administration. Ratcliffe had previously served as **Director of National Intelligence from May 2020 to January 2021** — confirmed 49–44 in Trump's first term following a withdrawn prior nomination in 2019.

### Background and prior DNI experience

Ratcliffe represented Texas's 4th Congressional District (2015–2020) and served on the House Intelligence Committee. As first-term DNI, he aggressively declassified materials related to the Russia investigation, the Steele dossier origins, and the Flynn unmasking matter. He has been a persistent critic of what he characterizes as the politicization of IC analysis under Obama-era and Biden-era leadership.

### January 2025 confirmation hearing on AHI

During his [January 15, 2025 confirmation hearing before SSCI](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2025/jan/16/john-ratcliffe-review-cia-analysis-havana-syndrome/), Ratcliffe was pressed on AHIs by Senator Susan Collins (R-ME), one of the bipartisan HAVANA Act authors. He stated in written responses:

> "Should I be confirmed as CIA director, I will review all existing information and analysis and pursue all leads that provide insight into the cause and origin of [anomalous health incidents] and all medical incidents with a [counterintelligence] nexus. I will ensure that, under my leadership, CIA will pursue this issue aggressively, objectively, and with total analytic integrity."

Ratcliffe also noted: "I asked then CIA Director Haspel to look into the issue four years ago. Having stepped away, I share your frustration that four years later we're very much in the same place."

### Ratcliffe's public position on AHI after confirmation

At the [Senate Intelligence Committee annual worldwide threats hearing on March 18, 2026](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BW6qd4IVoIE), Ratcliffe told Sen. Mike Rounds (R-SD):

> "There are disparate opinions across the IC about what the cause of these AHIs may or may not be... and the question of whether or not they could be the result of a directed energy weapon from a foreign adversary. Director Gabbard and I had a conversation where she agreed that she would undertake a comprehensive review of that issue across the IC to make sure it is getting the attention it deserves. And I know that she has undertaken that and will be able to brief you in further detail in the classified session."

This statement is notable for two things: Ratcliffe explicitly acknowledged IC disagreement, and he deferred the leadership of the review to Gabbard's ODNI rather than asserting CIA primacy.

### The July 2025 "lessons learned" review of the 2016 Russia ICA

In July 2025, Ratcliffe [declassified and released a CIA tradecraft review](https://www.cia.gov/stories/story/cia-director-john-ratcliffe-declassifies-internal-tradecraft-review/) of the January 2017 ICA on Russian election interference. The review found that then-CIA Director Brennan had driven "an atypical analytic process" under "a politically charged environment," criticized the compressed timeline, excessive compartmentation, and the inclusion of the Steele dossier over analytic objections. [Ratcliffe stated](https://nypost.com/2025/07/02/us-news/obamas-trump-russia-collusion-report-was-corrupt-from-start-cia-review/): "Agency heads at the time created a politically charged environment that triggered an atypical analytic process." This precedent is directly relevant to the AHI ICA dispute: critics of the 2023 AHI assessment have used the same "predetermined conclusion" and "atypical process" language to characterize how it was produced. The tradecraft review of the 2016 ICA signals Ratcliffe's willingness to retroactively critique prior analytic products on process grounds.

### Internal CIA actions on AHI

No leaked personnel changes or internal CIA memos on the AHI analytic chain have been publicly reported as of May 2026. However, the CIA has been under sustained pressure: the HPSCI classified annex reportedly documents specific individuals involved in the 2023 ICA's production. Those names have not entered the public record. The DIA's Director, Lt. Gen. Jeffrey Kruse, was removed from his position in August 2025; Crawford told colleagues he had recommended Kruse's removal in part because of his "treatment of AHI victims." The CIA OIG's 2022 report on the agency's initial handling of AHI remains [classified and has not been released](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/world/americas/cuba/article312343950.html), though Marc Polymeropoulos and attorney Zaid have publicly called for its declassification.

## 7. Tulsi Gabbard at ODNI {#gabbard-odni}

### Confirmation

[The Senate confirmed Tulsi Gabbard as Director of National Intelligence on February 12, 2025](https://www.dni.gov/index.php/newsroom/press-releases/press-releases-2025/4050-pr-01-25), by a vote of 52–48 along partisan lines, with former Senate Republican Leader Mitch McConnell joining all Democrats in opposition. The Senate Intelligence Committee approved her nomination 9–8 on a party-line vote. [Gabbard took the oath of office the same day, February 12, 2025](https://www.axios.com/2025/02/12/tulsi-gabbard-confirmed-dni-senate), becoming the eighth Senate-confirmed and first female combat-veteran DNI.

Gabbard is a former four-term Democratic congresswoman (Hawaii) and 2020 presidential candidate who switched to the Republican Party in 2022. She is an Army Reserve officer. She has been historically skeptical of IC consensus assessments, particularly on Russia-related matters, which fueled both her nomination by Trump and significant Democratic opposition.

### Gabbard's pre-confirmation statements on AHI

At her Senate confirmation hearing (January 30, 2025), Gabbard told senators:

> "It has been deeply concerning throughout this period, from the first time this was discovered so long ago to where we are today, that the intelligence community still has failed to identify the source and the cause for Havana Syndrome... I look forward, if confirmed as director of national intelligence, to addressing this. I am committed to getting to the truth behind how and why this has occurred."

She also committed to "initiating a new investigation into Anomalous Health Incidents and making the findings public," a [statement publicly praised by attorney Mark Zaid](https://x.com/MarkSZaidEsq/status/1914419424880189699).

### The Director's Initiatives Group

In April 2025, Gabbard [established the "Director's Initiatives Group" (DIG)](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/04/08/us/politics/tulsi-gabbard-intelligence-task-force.html) — a transparency and accountability task force explicitly tasked with, among other topics, reviewing AHI-related documents for potential declassification. ODNI framed the DIG's mission as "streamlining outdated processes, reviewing documents for declassification, and leading ongoing efforts to root out abuses of power and politicization." AHI investigations were listed alongside COVID-19 origins and 2016 election interference as a specific declassification priority. The [AP reported in February 2026](https://apnews.com/article/gabbard-trump-intelligence-odni-directors-group-cia-0432d493e578565b5d57f70405f38b31) that Gabbard ended the DIG's formal work — though the AHI review continued under separate ODNI auspices.

### ODNI review status as of early 2026

[CBS News reported in January 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/device-havana-syndrome-obtained-by-u-s-government/) that Gabbard's ODNI review was "largely complete" but "has not yet been briefed to lawmakers or prepared for public release." ODNI's spokesperson said Gabbard "remains committed to sharing findings from her investigation into Anomalous Health Incidents with the American people" and that a team was continuing "relentless" work on completing the assessment but: "we are not going to rush to put out incomplete information."

By January 2026, [Crawford was publicly pressing for immediate recall of the 2023 ICA](https://nypost.com/2026/01/26/us-news/house-intel-chair-demands-recall-of-flawed-report-saying-us-foes-didnt-cause-havana-syndrome/), and Gabbard had condemned the government's prior handling of Havana Syndrome.

A [*Washington Examiner* report from March 2026](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/opinion/beltway-confidential/4498095/rick-crawford-drags-intelligence-community-toward-havana-syndrome-truth/) noted that "the Trump administration has thus far prevented Gabbard's report from being published," suggesting internal resistance within the administration to publicly releasing the review, despite Gabbard's stated commitment.

## 8. The March 2026 Congressional Testimony and the Retraction Commitment {#march-2026-testimony}

### The March 18–19, 2026 hearings

The pivotal public moment came at the annual worldwide threats hearings held March 18 (SSCI) and March 19, 2026 (HPSCI). IC heads testifying included: DNI Tulsi Gabbard; CIA Director John Ratcliffe; FBI Director Kash Patel; acting NSA Director Lt. Gen. William Hartman; and DIA Director Lt. Gen. James Adams.

**At the March 18 SSCI hearing**, Ratcliffe acknowledged "disparate opinions" within the IC on AHI causes and stated he had deferred to Gabbard for the comprehensive review. He noted that Gabbard "has undertaken that and will be able to brief you in further detail in the classified session."

**At the March 19 HPSCI hearing**, Crawford opened with a direct statement: ["Put simply, it's my clear opinion that individuals in the intelligence community were involved in a cover-up."](https://www.spytalk.co/p/spy-agency-bosses-pledge-new-look) He then posed a direct yes-or-no question to each witness: whether the 2025 ICA (the January 2025 update, which he treated as an extension of the 2023 ICA's conclusions) should be retracted.

According to [*Just the News* reporting from March 19, 2026](https://justthenews.com/government/security/spy-chiefs-all-agree-biden-era-intel-assessment-havana-syndrome-should-be):

> **DNI Tulsi Gabbard: "Yes."**  
> **FBI Director Kash Patel: "Yes."**  
> **Acting NSA Director Lt. Gen. William Hartman: "Yes."**  
> **DIA Director Lt. Gen. James Adams: "Yes."**  

Ratcliffe offered a more equivocal answer: "For the ICA, I understand that Director Gabbard is reviewing that, and I've deferred all AHI investigations to her review. So, if in her judgment that is something that should be retracted, then I would agree with that."

[Global News reported](https://globalnews.ca/news/11737905/havana-syndrome-foreign-actor-cover-up-canada/) that Crawford also stated the IC's assessments "as well as the 2024 U.S. National Institutes of Health studies that found no evidence of brain injury among those affected by Havana Syndrome, had created distrust among Americans in the intelligence community and 'caused real, serious harm to some of our nation's bravest.'"

Gabbard nodded in agreement that ODNI's review would be "comprehensive and complete" and that she was "committed to sharing her findings with the American people."

### What "yes" means — and what it does not mean

These affirmative answers constitute a **public congressional commitment by sitting IC principals to seek formal retraction**. They do not constitute, and should not be described as, an actual retraction of the ICA. As of late April / early May 2026:

- **No formal written retraction document has been issued by ODNI.**
- **No corrective ICA replacing the 2023 ICA has been published.**
- **No "no longer represents IC views" statement has been released on odni.gov.**

The [*War on the Rocks* assessment of March 10, 2026](https://warontherocks.com/we-were-right-about-havana-syndrome/) noted that "despite all the promises from Gabbard, CIA director John Ratcliffe, and the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence to reopen the investigation and recall previous flawed analysis, there is still no visible progress" in terms of formal published action. This tension — between the political/institutional agreement and the procedural inaction — defines the current state of the retraction process.

## 9. The DOJ Criminal Referral — September 2025 {#doj-referral}

In early September 2025, HPSCI formally transmitted **criminal referrals to the Department of Justice** related to the committee's AHI investigation. The referrals were confirmed by an HPSCI spokesperson to multiple news outlets.

**Who made the referral:** The House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence, under Crawford's chairmanship. As [*Miami Herald* reporting from October 2, 2025](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/world/americas/cuba/article312343950.html) confirmed, the referral was transmitted "in early September."

**What the referral covers:** HPSCI's spokesperson declined to identify the specific targets. However, the committee had been explicit about the broad nature of its concerns: potential illegal activities related to the IC's obstruction of the congressional investigation; possible false statements or misleading testimony to Congress; and alleged coercive use of the NIH study to deny victim care and manipulate the evidentiary base for the 2023 ICA. Crawford stated: "Among the many issues involved, the withholding of medical care to force participation in this human subject research study, as directed by personnel in the IC, betrayed those desperately seeking help."

**What HPSCI said it might also refer:** The spokesperson noted that "additional criminal referrals may be sent, as the facts determine. This is particularly the case in the Committee's review of the IC's obstruction of this investigation."

**DOJ response:** No public statement from DOJ on whether it is pursuing a criminal investigation has been reported as of May 2026. The referrals were made to the Trump DOJ, which is politically aligned with the investigation's direction. The absence of a public DOJ response is not unusual; DOJ routinely neither confirms nor denies the existence of investigations.

**The ICIG angle:** HPSCI's investigation found that the Intelligence Community Inspector General (ICIG) also participated in withholding key information from Congress, according to the committee's spokesperson, though no ICIG personnel have been publicly named as referral subjects.

## 10. Internal IC Dissent: Greg Edgreen and the Whistleblower Record {#ic-dissent}

### Greg Edgreen — the DIA's internal skeptic

**Retired Army Lt. Col. Greg Edgreen** ran the Defense Intelligence Agency's investigation into AHIs from approximately 2020 to 2023 — one of the key internal IC analytical efforts that ran in parallel with, and sometimes in tension with, the CIA-led NIC process producing the 2023 ICA.

Edgreen's first major public appearance was the March 31, 2024 joint 60 Minutes / Insider / Spiegel broadcast, where he stated on camera, in one of the most direct on-record statements by a former U.S. intelligence official:

> **"If I'm wrong about Russia being behind anomalous health incidents, I will come onto your show and eat my tie."** — [*The Insider* / 60 Minutes, March 31, 2024](https://theins.press/en/politics/270425)

Edgreen described noticing, through "signals intelligence, human intelligence, open-source reporting" and review of "travel records, financial records," that "this was happening to our top 5%, 10% performing officers across the Defense Intelligence Agency. And consistently there was a Russia nexus."

> "I believe the U.S. is being attacked by Russia, and that the official threshold to prove it was set impossibly high because the country doesn't want to face some very hard truths." — [CBS News / 60 Minutes, March 31, 2024](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/havana-syndrome-russia-evidence-60-minutes/)

He also stated he had focused on Moscow "at a very early stage" of his investigation, and that his access included classified intelligence compiled across the IC, not just DIA.

After the hearing, Edgreen appeared on [PBS NewsHour (April 1, 2024)](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/havana-syndrome-investigator-on-accusing-russia-of-targeting-u-s-officials), reiterating his confidence that Russia was responsible and describing the official bar of proof as "impossibly high."

By late 2025, the [*Washington Times* (December 19, 2025)](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2025/dec/19/house-intelligence-panel-steps-probe-havana-syndrome-incidents/) confirmed that Edgreen "headed a DIA investigation into anomalous health incidents" and "testified to the House in 2023 that he suspects Russia is behind" the incidents. His 2023 testimony to HPSCI, delivered in a classified format, was described by Crawford's investigation as among the information that led to the formal February 2024 investigation being opened.

### The whistleblower complaint to IC IG

A whistleblower — an intelligence community professional represented by attorney **Mark Zaid** — filed a complaint with the IC Inspector General that was deemed an "urgent concern" and forwarded to the congressional intelligence committees. The existence of this complaint was confirmed in [Zaid's congressional testimony and a FOIA-related lawsuit](https://wislawjournal.com/2024/05/06/cia-is-obstructing-justice-and-witness-tampering-in-major-havana-syndrome-cover-up-attorney-says/), via the **James Madison Project v. ODNI** litigation.

The whistleblower complaint, as described in documents obtained via FOIA, alleged:

> "The CIA, with apparent assistance of elements of the DNI — notably the National Intelligence Council and the National Counter Intelligence and Security Center — is engaged in the active suppression of witnesses, and actions which may constitute obstruction of justice and witness tampering as defined in federal statute." — documents cited in [Salon / TreatNow, September 2024](https://treatnow.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/salon.com-Active-suppression-of-witnesses-CIA-lied-about-Havana-Syndrome-whistleblower-documents-reveal.pdf)

Zaid stated: "One of the conclusions in this document is that the CIA is deliberately withholding information from sister agencies in order to prevent those agencies from conducting further investigations." He added that the CIA was "withholding significant AHI documents from DOD."

The HPSCI investigation reportedly originated in large part from this whistleblower complaint and related disclosures.

### Anonymous "two intelligence officials" and early 2024 reporting

Reporting in early 2024 by Adam Entous in *The New Yorker* (referenced in multiple secondary sources) indicated that two anonymous intelligence officials had disclosed to journalists that the IC's internal assessment was being challenged — a leak that preceded the formal HPSCI investigation and the March 2024 60 Minutes broadcast. The specific *New Yorker* article by Entous has not been publicly cited by name in HPSCI documents, though his broader Havana Syndrome reporting is widely acknowledged as having been central to sustaining public and congressional attention.

### The NSA's classified memorandum on directed energy

Separately, the NSA provided Zaid — representing pre-Havana AHI victim **Michael Beck**, a former NSA counterintelligence official who was forced to retire in 2016 after developing a rare form of Parkinson's disease — a memorandum dated **October 14, 2014**, stating that NSA "possessed intelligence information from 2012 associating the hostile country to which Mr. Beck traveled to in the late 1990s with a high-powered microwave system weapon." Beck's 2016 medical crisis predates the Cuba cluster; his case has been described as potentially among the earliest documented AHI victims. Beck died in January 2026, his death cited by Crawford when calling for the ICA's immediate recall.

## 11. The March 31, 2024 Joint Investigation: 60 Minutes, Der Spiegel, The Insider {#media-investigation}

The most significant journalistic intervention in the AHI debate came on March 31, 2024, when **CBS News' 60 Minutes** (correspondent **Scott Pelley**, producers Oriana Zill de Granados and Michael Rey), **Der Spiegel**, and **The Insider** (a Russia-focused investigative publication co-founded by Christo Grozev and edited by Michael Weiss) published a joint investigation: ["Unraveling Havana Syndrome: New evidence links the GRU's assassination Unit 29155 to mysterious attacks on U.S. officials and their families"](https://theins.press/en/politics/270425).

### Core findings

The investigation, which took approximately one year to produce, yielded:

**GRU Unit 29155 geolocated to AHI incident sites:** The team documented that members of Unit 29155 — a GRU black-ops unit previously linked to the Salisbury nerve agent attack, Bulgaria bombings, and other assassination campaigns — were physically present in multiple cities at times coinciding with reported AHI incidents. This included Tbilisi, Georgia (October 2021, where a U.S. Embassy employee's wife reported a sudden auditory attack); Frankfurt, Germany (2014, possibly the earliest documented AHI); and the Kyiv CIA station.

**Unit 29155 officers rewarded for directed energy work:** The Insider obtained documentary evidence — described as an internal GRU accounting document — showing that a Unit 29155 officer received a financial bonus for work on **"potential capabilities of non-lethal acoustic weapons."** Grozev called this "the closest to a receipt you can have."

**Albert Averyanov identified at Tbilisi AHI site:** The joint investigation identified Albert Averyanov, son of Unit 29155 commander Andrey Averyanov, as having been in Tbilisi around the time of the October 2021 AHI cluster. A U.S. Embassy employee's wife, "Joy," identified him from photographs as a man she saw outside her home immediately before her attack.

**Operation Reduktor:** The Insider published documents describing a classified Soviet-era program codenamed *Reduktor* ("Gearbox"), begun in 1984 at the Radio Technical Measurement Research Institute in Kharkiv, whose central task was studying "electromagnetic radiation to influence the behavior and reactions of biological objects, including people."

### Greg Edgreen on camera

This investigation featured Edgreen's first public television appearance, in which he stated: "I believe the U.S. is being attacked by Russia" and characterized the bar for proof as impossibly high because "the country doesn't want to face some very hard truths."

### Impact

Following the broadcast, a bipartisan group of lawmakers sent a letter to President Biden calling for a "renewed assessment" of AHIs. The 60 Minutes report, along with the HPSCI investigation's momentum, is widely credited with catalyzing the January 2025 ODNI update — even though that update ultimately maintained the "very unlikely" overall assessment.

## 12. The March 8, 2026 60 Minutes Follow-Up: The Acquired Device {#march-2026-60-minutes}

On March 8, 2026, [60 Minutes broadcast a fourth installment of its Havana Syndrome series](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/60-minutes-havana-syndrome-report-finds-u-s-government-tested-energy-weapon/), again with Pelley, Zill de Granados, and Rey. This report introduced a major new factual element:

### The covert acquisition of a directed-energy device

Multiple 60 Minutes sources, confirmed by [CNN reporting from January 13, 2026](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/13/politics/havana-syndrome-device-pentagon-hsi), disclosed that:

- U.S. agents investigating illicit arms dealers had learned that a **Russian criminal network** was selling a **microwave weapon** capable of producing pulsed radio waves.
- **Department of Homeland Security Investigations (HSI)** operatives purchased the device in an undercover operation in 2024.
- The operation cost approximately **$15 million**, financed by the Pentagon.
- The device is **backpack-portable** and contains **Russian components** (though "not entirely Russian in origin," per CNN).
- The device has been **tested on animals (rats and sheep) at a U.S. military laboratory for more than a year**, with results showing injuries "consistent with those seen in humans."

60 Minutes also quoted Dr. David Relman — the Stanford professor who led the 2020 and 2022 government expert panels — stating that federal officials had "downplayed," "dismissed," and in some cases "buried" his panels' findings. A "high-ranking CIA informant" was quoted describing the AHI cover-up as "the biggest coverup I've witnessed in my adult life."

The *Washington Examiner* noted that this disclosure "gutted the credibility" of the January 2025 ICA "at its birth," given the ICA's heavy reliance on the judgment that no credible evidence exists for a foreign-held directed-energy weapon capable of causing AHI-type injuries. ODNI's response to the 60 Minutes inquiry was that "a new review of the Havana Syndrome incidents will be 'comprehensive and complete'" and that ODNI "remains committed to delivering the truth."

## 13. Legislative Pressure: HAVANA Act, Warner, Turner, Crawford {#legislation}

### The HAVANA Act (2021)

The **Helping American Victims Afflicted by Neurological Attacks (HAVANA) Act of 2021**, [Public Law 117-46, signed October 8, 2021](https://www.warner.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/u-s-sen-mark-r-warner-s-bill-to-support-havana-syndrome-victims-signed-into-law/), was authored by SSCI Chairman Mark Warner (D-VA) with bipartisan co-sponsors Marco Rubio (R-FL), Susan Collins (R-ME), and Jeanne Shaheen (D-NH). The bill passed both chambers unanimously. It authorized CIA, State, and other agency heads to provide financial compensation for brain injuries sustained in connection with AHIs on or after January 1, 2016. Both CIA and State were required to issue implementing regulations and report to Congress on their use of the authority.

Implementation was slow and uneven. As of December 31, 2023, CIA's approval rate for AHI compensation claims was 21%, compared with 67% for other agencies, per [SSCI reporting](https://www.intelligence.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/AP_Report_24_01_R.pdf). [The Pentagon only began formally reviewing HAVANA Act payment applications in July 2025](https://defensescoop.com/2025/07/14/havana-syndrome-dod-military-payment-applications/), following a June 2025 memo from the acting undersecretary of defense for personnel and readiness.

The NDAA signed by President Trump in late 2025 included a provision requiring [DNI Gabbard and IC agency heads to produce standardized guidelines for reporting AHIs](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2025/dec/19/house-intelligence-panel-steps-probe-havana-syndrome-incidents/).

In February 2026, Senators Shaheen and Collins wrote to Defense Secretary Hegseth [warning against a Pentagon proposal to restructure the cross-functional AHI team](https://www.shaheen.senate.gov/news/press/standing-up-for-victims-of-havana-syndrome-senators-shaheen-and-collins-urge-pentagon-to-pause-relocation-proposal), urging him to pause the move and brief Congress.

### Mark Warner (SSCI)

Warner's March 1, 2023 statement on the original ICA release was cautious: ["The Senate Intelligence Committee received this morning the Intelligence Community's Assessment on Anomalous Health Incidents and is currently reviewing it... We will continue to hold the Intelligence Community accountable for ensuring that those officers who have reported being affected by AHIs are being treated with the respect, dignity, and care they deserve."](https://www.warner.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/statement-of-senate-intel-chair-mark-r-warner-on-the-intelligence-community-assessment-on-anomalous-health-incidents/) In October 2021, Warner had called the lack of IC intelligence on AHI causes ["very, very problematic"](https://www.politico.com/news/2021/10/27/mark-warner-havana-syndrome-intel-517331) and noted that the Trump administration had "whiffed" on early reporting. By 2024–2025, as the minority member on SSCI under Trump, Warner's public posture shifted to more general oversight pressure.

### Mike Turner (HPSCI, 118th Congress)

HPSCI Chairman Turner authorized the formal investigation in February 2024. At the January 10, 2025 ODNI update release, Turner said the update ["continues the Biden Administration's cover-up"](https://intelligence.house.gov/2025/01/10/turner-and-crawford-respond-to-biden-administration-covering-up-truth-about-havana-syndrome/) and that the new intelligence "should completely change the assessment of our adversaries' capabilities and the risks to our personnel."

### Rick Crawford (HPSCI, 119th Congress)

Crawford became HPSCI Chairman in the 119th Congress (January 2025). He has been the principal congressional driver of the ICA retraction push. Key Crawford actions:
- February 2024: Led formal HPSCI investigation designation
- May 2024: Issued NSA subpoena
- December 5, 2024: Released unclassified interim report
- September 2025: Sent criminal referrals to DOJ
- January 2026: Publicly called for immediate recall of the 2023 ICA on X
- March 19, 2026: Extracted yes/no answers from IC heads on retraction

Crawford has been explicit that not every AHI report is the result of hostile foreign action, acknowledging some opportunistic or misidentified cases, but insisting that "a very significant number of credible cases" exist.

## 14. Civil Litigation, FOIA, and the James Madison Project {#litigation}

### The James Madison Project FOIA lawsuit

Attorney **Mark Zaid** represents both AHI victims and the **James Madison Project**, a government transparency nonprofit. Zaid has been litigating a FOIA lawsuit seeking disclosure of documents related to the IC's AHI investigation and the whistleblower complaint. Key developments:

- The ODNI, in response to the lawsuit, confirmed it had sought consultations from **10 different federal agencies** regarding a subset of responsive records — an unusual scope.
- The CIA requested a July 31, 2024 estimated response date for consultation on **32 pages of records**.
- "Agency B" — an agency described in court documents as one whose "mere existence is classified" when associated with AHI — was assigned a July 15, 2024 response date for **42 pages of records**. Zaid noted in April 2024 that "I have never once seen the government's position that the agencies' identity are classified merely because it's tied to AHI."
- DIA was assigned a May 3, 2024 estimated response for consultations covering 31 pages.

Zaid stated that via the FOIA and lawsuit, his team "learned CIA was withholding significant AHI documents from DOD."

### The FBI's release of a 2019 document

In August 2024, the FBI released a [partially redacted section of a 2019 document](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/08/30/us/politics/fbi-havana-syndrome.html) — described as among the FBI's initial assessments — suggesting that some AHI symptoms might stem from "social contagion." The release came via FOIA litigation. The document is consistent with the analytic framing that the 2023 ICA ultimately adopted.

### Class action litigation

No publicly documented class action lawsuit by AHI victims has advanced to trial-ready status as of early 2026. Zaid's representation of more than two dozen current and former federal employees covers individual compensation claims under the HAVANA Act and employment law, not a class action. The principal litigation vehicles have been FOIA-based challenges to IC document withholding rather than personal injury claims, in large part because classified IC determinations make standard tort damages claims difficult.

### Knight Institute v. CIA note

The Knight First Amendment Institute at Columbia University has litigated [FOIA-related cases against the CIA](https://knightcolumbia.org/cases/knight-institute-v-cia), though its primary case of record concerns NSO Group spyware, not AHI materials specifically. No Knight Institute AHI FOIA litigation has entered the public record.

## 15. Procedural Question: What Does "Retraction" Mean for an ICA? {#retraction-procedure}

This is a genuinely novel procedural question. Intelligence Community Assessments are living documents in that the IC continuously evaluates whether its prior judgments remain valid, but there is no standardized publicly documented mechanism for the formal "retraction" of an ICA equivalent to, say, journal article retraction.

Possible procedural forms a retraction could take:

**1. A new coordinated ICA superseding the 2023 and January 2025 assessments.** This would be the most institutionally authoritative mechanism: a new IC-coordinated assessment reaching different conclusions, which would implicitly retire the prior documents. The NIC would lead; ODNI would release it publicly. This is what Crawford has most directly called for — a "fresh start."

**2. An ODNI "no longer represents IC views" statement.** ODNI could issue a press release or formal notice stating that the 2023 ICA (ICA 2023-02286-B) and the January 2025 update (ICA 2025-00008-B) no longer represent the IC's analytic judgment. This is a lower-cost, less analytically rigorous mechanism that stops short of replacing the documents with new analysis.

**3. A formal ODNI withdrawal/recall notice.** ODNI could issue a classified or unclassified memorandum formally withdrawing the documents from the IC analytic record, potentially citing specific analytic integrity violations. This is the language Crawford's committee has used ("recall," "retract"), though no procedural template for this exists in publicly known IC policy.

**4. A tradecraft review, similar to the July 2025 Russia ICA review.** Ratcliffe's CIA could extend the "lessons-learned review" model it used for the 2016 Russia election interference ICA to the 2023 AHI ICA — producing a document that finds the analytic process was flawed and the product should be superseded. This does not itself replace the ICA but builds the evidentiary record for formal retirement.

The *SpyTalk* framing from March 19, 2026 is accurate: the IC heads "pledged a new look" — they agreed to seek retraction, but [the formal act has not occurred](https://www.spytalk.co/p/spy-agency-bosses-pledge-new-look). The review Gabbard has promised — if published — would be the most immediate vehicle for formal action.

The retraction process is in motion. It is politically and institutionally committed to at the highest levels. It has not yet been formally executed. The gap between the verbal commitment and the published document is the defining unresolved feature of the AHI policy landscape as of May 2026.
