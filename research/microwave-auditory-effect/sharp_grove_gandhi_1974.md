# Sharp, Grove & Gandhi (1974): "Generation of Acoustic Signals by Pulsed Microwave Energy"

**Full citation:** Sharp, J. C., Grove, H. M., & Gandhi, O. P. (1974). Generation of acoustic signals by pulsed microwave energy. *IEEE Transactions on Microwave Theory and Techniques*, 22, 583–584.  
**Type:** Letter (correspondence), not a full research article  
**Manuscript received:** October 25, 1973; revised January 11, 1974

## 3. Abstract (verbatim)

> "A discussion of the production of sound when short microwave pulses are directed at an absorber is presented. Possible mechanisms of the phenomenon are presented. These mechanisms may be important for a better understanding of the so-called microwave 'hearing' effect." **[PRIMARY]**

---

## 4. Experimental Setup

**[PRIMARY]** The experiment was discovered serendipitously. While attempting to shield portions of a microwave beam using a carbon-impregnated polyurethane microwave absorber (Emerson and Cumming Eccosorb WG4, surface area 300 cm²), the authors noticed that the apparent locus of a "sound" moved from the observer's head to the absorber — that is, the absorber itself appeared to act as an acoustic transducer.

To verify the acoustic nature of the signal, the authors used:
- A **General Radio model 1551C sound level meter** fitted with a **1561-P5 microphone**
- Two **paper cone waveguides** (1.42 m and 0.78 m long) to acoustically couple the microphone to the absorber
- A **Krohn-Hite model 333BR bandpass filter** set to pass **150–2500 Hz**
- An **HP Fourier analyzer model 5450A** where signals were digitized; 50 epochs of 25.6 ms each were summed at 512 equidistant sample points (sampling interval = 50 µs)

The microwave source was an **Applied Microwave Laboratories, Inc., model PG-68B** pulse signal source, radiating through a **Narda 646 horn** (physical aperture 5.15 × 5.15 cm).

## 6. What Was Actually Measured

**[PRIMARY]** The measurement was acoustic pressure from a *non-biological absorber* (carbon-impregnated polyurethane foam and, separately, crumpled aluminum foil), not in tissue, tissue phantoms, or in vivo subjects.

**Proof of acoustic nature:** The time delay between the trigger pulse and microphone activation corresponded precisely (within experimental error) to the speed of sound (332 m/s). At 1.42 m distance, delay was ~4.25 ms; at 0.78 m, ~3.25 ms. When the paper-cone acoustic coupler was removed, no signal was detected — ruling out RF coupling artifacts. **[PRIMARY]**

**Additional findings:**
- Sounds could be produced in absorbers as small as **4 mm² × 2 mm thick** — extremely small targets. **[PRIMARY]**
- Multiple absorber types worked, as did loosely crumpled aluminum foil. **[PRIMARY]**
- Little difference in signal quality or amplitude was found when carrier frequency was changed from 1200 through 1500 to 2450 MHz, suggesting frequency was not critical over this range. **[PRIMARY]**
- At repetition rates up to 100 pulses/s, "the absorber seemed to track the repetition rate of the pulses" — a key observation for later speech-modulation concepts. **[PRIMARY]**

**No tissue phantoms, no in vivo measurements** were conducted in this paper. The biological "hearing" effect is discussed only in relation to mechanism, not by direct measurement. **[PRIMARY]**

## 7. Proposed Mechanisms

**[PRIMARY]** The authors consider "several likely explanations" without definitively endorsing one:

1. **Radiation pressure:** For a perfect absorber at 75 kW/m², calculated radiation pressure is **2.5 × 10⁻⁴ dyn/cm²** (double for a reflector). The authors note these are "extremely small" pressures but "near the threshold of audible sound pressure."

2. **Piezoelectric effect in bone:** Bone has known piezoelectric properties; strain induced by radiation pressure or electrostrictive forces could generate a difference potential — potentially providing an "electrically mediated sensation."

3. **Bone conduction / basilar membrane coupling:** Direct A.C. excitation of the auditory organ via bone conduction, or direct coupling of the basilar membrane with the microwave energy.

The paper explicitly states "it is entirely possible that more than one of these mechanisms are operating when humans 'sense' microwave pulses." **[PRIMARY]**

**Notably absent:** The paper does not invoke the *thermoelastic expansion* mechanism, which would become the dominant explanation following Foster and Finch's paper in *Science* published the same year ([Foster & Finch, 1974](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4833827/)). Sharp, Grove, and Gandhi were primarily demonstrating the phenomenon in external absorbers, not calculating thermoacoustic pressure in tissue.

## 8. Relation to the Unpublished Speech-Transmission Demonstration

**This is where the published paper and the private communication diverge sharply.**

**[PRIMARY]** The 1974 IEEE paper contains **no mention of voice modulation, speech transmission, or communication**. It is purely an acoustic-physics demonstration on inanimate absorbers.

**[SECONDARY]** The speech-transmission experiment is described separately by Don R. Justesen in his 1975 *American Psychologist* article "Microwaves and Behavior" ([full text via Zory Glaser archive](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)), where he cites a **personal communication from Sharp and Grove dated September 28, 1973** — predating the submitted IEEE manuscript. Justesen's description:

> "Sharp and Grove (Note 2) found that appropriate modulation of microwave energy can result in direct 'wireless' and 'receiverless' communication of speech. They recorded by voice on tape each of the single-syllable words for digits between 1 and 10. The electrical sine-wave analogs of each word were then processed so that each time a sine wave crossed zero reference in the negative direction, a brief pulse of microwave energy was triggered. By radiating themselves with these 'voice-modulated' microwaves, Sharp and Grove were readily able to hear, identify, and distinguish among the 9 words. The sounds heard were not unlike those emitted by persons with artificial larynxes."

Justesen's footnote reference is: *"Sharp, J. C., & Grove, M. Personal communication, September 28, 1973."* — **not** a citation to the published paper. The technique used was **zero-crossing pulse triggering**: a speech waveform was processed so each negative-going zero-crossing generated a microwave pulse.

**Summary of the distinction:**

| | Published IEEE paper (1974) | Unpublished speech demo (1973) |
|---|---|---|
| **Status** | Peer-reviewed letter | Personal communication only |
| **Content** | Acoustic transduction in absorbers | Speech word recognition via voice-modulated microwaves |
| **Subject** | Inanimate absorbers (foam, foil) | Human subjects (Sharp and Grove themselves) |
| **Mechanism discussion** | Radiation pressure, piezoelectric, bone conduction | Not discussed in primary source |
| **Voice modulation** | None mentioned | Core of the demo |
| **Source** | [IEEE via Zory Glaser](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/GENERATION-OF-ACOUSTIC-SIGNALS-BY-PULSED-MICROWAVE-ENERGY.pdf) | Cited as personal comm. in [Justesen 1975](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf) |

**[SECONDARY]** As Justesen noted, "sonic transduction of pulsed microwaves at low-averaged densities has been demonstrated by Sharp, Grove, and Gandhi (1974) in materials lacking in water, for example, in carbon-impregnated plastic and in crumpled sheets of aluminum foil" — accurately characterizing what the published paper actually showed.

## 9. Om P. Gandhi: Role and Context

**[PRIMARY from MTT-S biography]** Om P. Gandhi (b. India) received his B.S. in Physics from the University of Delhi, a post-graduate diploma from the Indian Institute of Science, and his M.S.E. and Sc.D. in Electrical Engineering from the University of Michigan. He joined the faculty at the University of Utah's Department of Electrical Engineering in **1967**, became a full professor in **1973**, and chaired the department from 1992 to 2000.

Crucially, Gandhi was **consulting at Walter Reed Army Institute of Research during the autumn quarter of 1973** — exactly when the Sharp/Grove experiments were occurring. The paper's acknowledgment thanks "Dr. J. C. Lin for his help in setting up the microwave apparatus during the initial phase of this research," confirming the collaborative WRAIR context. **[PRIMARY]**

This paper effectively marked Gandhi's **entry into bioelectromagnetics**, a field he would dominate for the next four decades. His subsequent work focused on:
- RF dosimetry and specific absorption rate (SAR) in biological models
- Conditions of maximum RF absorption in humans and animals (leading to downward revisions of ANSI/IEEE RF safety standards in 1982)
- Anatomically-based numerical models of the human body for EM coupling studies
- Authored the textbook *Microwave Engineering and Applications* (Pergamon, 1981) and edited *Biological Effects and Medical Applications of Electromagnetic Energy* (Prentice-Hall, 1990)

Gandhi was elected IEEE Fellow in 1979, received the Bioelectromagnetics Society's d'Arsonval Medal in 1995, and was cited by the MTT Society in 2001 "for contributions to dosimetry of electromagnetic radiation and resulting revisions of RF/microwave safety standards." **[PRIMARY from MTT-S biography]** His Google Scholar profile lists over 12,000 citations and an h-index of 63. **[SECONDARY from Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=lUPswfIAAAAJ&hl=en)**

## 10. What the Paper Does and Does Not Say

**Stays purely on acoustic-physics side:**
- Demonstrates microwave-to-acoustic transduction in non-biological absorbers
- Characterizes acoustic signal by time-of-flight delay consistent with speed of sound
- Lists multiple possible mechanisms without endorsing one
- Notes the "hearing" effect as the motivation for the study
- Speculates that dual mechanisms may operate in human perception

**Does NOT:**
- Discuss voice modulation or speech transmission
- Measure anything in tissue, tissue phantoms, or living subjects
- Use the word "communication" or refer to information transmission
- Endorse thermoelastic expansion as the primary mechanism (that would come from Foster & Finch the same year)
- Provide absorbed energy or SAR values (power density only, calculated from geometry)

---

## 11. Significance in Context

The paper sits at the intersection of three 1974 contributions that together shifted the microwave-hearing field:

1. **Sharp, Grove & Gandhi (1974):** Demonstrated acoustic transduction in absorbers, proposed multiple mechanisms, established the observable phenomenon outside the human body.
2. **Foster & Finch (1974, *Science*):** Demonstrated thermoacoustic pressure waves in water from pulsed microwaves, quantitatively consistent with click thresholds — the paper that established thermoelastic expansion as the preferred mechanism.
3. **Sharp & Grove's unpublished demo (1973, reported by Justesen 1975):** Extended the effect to speech-like signals via zero-crossing modulation, never formally published as a peer-reviewed study.

The IEEE letter is therefore best understood as the published, physics-focused companion to an unpublished applied demonstration. It provided the scientific grounding (external transduction, acoustic verification) while the speech experiment showed biological applicability — but only the former survives as a citable primary source.
