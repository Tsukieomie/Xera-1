# NIH JAMA 2024 Studies on Havana Syndrome / AHI Brain Imaging

*Research note covering the March 18, 2024, NIH publications in JAMA, their methodological relationship to the earlier University of Pennsylvania studies, and their scientific, political, and interpretive aftermath.*

---

## 1. The Two JAMA Studies Published March 18, 2024

On March 18, 2024, the *Journal of the American Medical Association* published two companion papers from the NIH Clinical Center representing the largest and most methodologically rigorous government-sponsored investigation of anomalous health incidents (AHIs) to date.

### Pierpaoli et al. — Neuroimaging Study

**Citation:** [Pierpaoli C, Nayak A, Hafiz R, et al. Neuroimaging Findings in US Government Personnel and Their Family Members Involved in Anomalous Health Incidents. *JAMA*. 2024;331(13):1122–1134. doi:10.1001/jama.2024.2424](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2816532)

The neuroimaging study enrolled **81 participants** who reported AHIs and **48 matched control participants**. MRI scans were acquired a median of 80 days following symptom onset (range: 14–1,505 days), with some participants scanned as early as 14 days after an incident. The imaging battery was comprehensive: it included voxel-wise and regional structural volumetry, diffusion tensor imaging (fractional anisotropy, mean diffusivity, axial diffusivity, radial diffusivity), mean apparent propagator metrics (including return-to-origin probability and non-Gaussianity), dual-compartment diffusion metrics, and resting-state functional MRI.

The study's core finding was unambiguous: **after adjustment for multiple comparisons, there were no statistically significant differences between AHI participants and controls for any MRI modality** — neither brain volume, diffusion MRI metrics, nor functional connectivity. The reproducibility of measurements was high across longitudinal follow-up visits (49 AHI participants had at least two visits).

At an *unadjusted* threshold (p < .05), the study observed some nominally lower intranetwork connectivity in the salience network in AHI participants, a slightly larger corpus callosum, and diffusion metric differences in white matter tracts including the corpus callosum, superior longitudinal fasciculus, cingulum, inferior cerebellar peduncle, and amygdala — but none survived multiple comparisons correction. No clear relationships between imaging measures and clinical variables emerged. Crucially, the study was explicitly described as exploratory, and the principal component analysis of diffusion metrics showed "an essential overlap of the median values" for AHI and control groups.

Lead author Carlo Pierpaoli was careful to state what the result did and did not mean: ["A lack of evidence for an MRI-detectable difference between individuals with AHIs and controls does not exclude that an adverse event impacting the brain occurred at the time of the AHI."](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-studies-find-severe-symptoms-havana-syndrome-no-evidence-mri-detectable-brain-injury-or-biological-abnormalities) He explicitly noted that "the injury did not produce the long-term neuroimaging changes that are typically observed after severe trauma or stroke."

### Chan et al. — Clinical and Biomarker Study

**Citation:** [Chan L, Hallett M, Zalewski CK, et al. Clinical, Biomarker, and Research Tests Among US Government Personnel and Their Family Members Involved in Anomalous Health Incidents. *JAMA*. 2024;331(13):1109–1121. doi:10.1001/jama.2024.2413](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2816533)

The clinical companion paper enrolled **86 AHI participants** and **30 vocationally matched control participants**. Participants were drawn from Cuba, China, Austria, and other locations and evaluated at the NIH Clinical Center between June 2018 and July 2022, a median of 76 days from their most recent incident.

The assessment battery was extensive: auditory testing, rotational vestibular assessment, ocular motor evaluation, cognitive neuropsychology (including full-scale IQ and subdomains), visual function, balance/posture, and blood biomarkers including glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) and neurofilament light chain (NfL) — both established markers of acute neuronal injury.

The central finding was that **most objective clinical and biomarker measures showed no significant differences** between AHI participants and controls, including auditory, vestibular, visual, and cognitive function, and neither GFAP nor NfL differed significantly between groups after multiple comparisons correction. However, AHI participants self-reported significantly elevated fatigue, post-traumatic stress disorder symptoms, and depression, and demonstrated objective balance impairment compared to controls. **Twenty-four participants (28%) in the AHI group met criteria for functional neurological disorders (FNDs)**, with most meeting criteria for persistent postural-perceptual dizziness (PPPD). When AHI participants were stratified by the intelligence community's AHI-1 designation (cases meeting the four core incident characteristics) versus AHI-2 (all others), and by geographic location, no meaningful between-group differences emerged.

## 2. The Earlier UPenn Studies: What They Claimed

### Swanson et al. (2018)

**Citation:** [Swanson RL, Hampton S, Green-McKenzie J, et al. Neurological Manifestations Among US Government Personnel Reporting Directional Audible and Sensory Phenomena in Havana, Cuba. *JAMA*. 2018;319(11):1125–1133. doi:10.1001/jama.2018.1742](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2673168)

This preliminary retrospective case series at the University of Pennsylvania's Center for Brain Injury and Repair described **21 of 24 identified individuals** who underwent multidisciplinary evaluation an average of 203 days after exposure. The study found persistent cognitive, vestibular, oculomotor, sleep, and auditory dysfunction. Standard MRI scans of the brain were normal in most patients (only 3 of 21 showed white matter changes, within normal ranges), but the advanced neuropsychological battery revealed deficits interpreted as consistent with concussion-like injury. The conclusion was that patients "appeared to have sustained injury to widespread brain networks without an associated history of head trauma" and may represent "a novel clinical entity." The study's major limitation, widely noted subsequently, was the **absence of any matched control group** — the comparison was to normative neuropsychological standards, not to matched government employees. Critics also noted that the threshold for "abnormal" neuropsychological scores was excessively inclusive, meaning many healthy individuals would have met the paper's definition of impairment.

### Verma et al. (2019)

**Citation:** [Verma R, Swanson RL, Parker D, et al. Neuroimaging Findings in US Government Personnel With Possible Exposure to Directional Phenomena in Havana, Cuba. *JAMA*. 2019;322(4):336–347. doi:10.1001/jama.2019.9269](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2738552)

This imaging study by Ragini Verma and colleagues at Penn's DiCIPHR lab examined **40 patients** compared to **48 healthy controls** using advanced structural and functional MRI. It found statistically significant differences in whole brain white matter volume (smaller by ~27 cm³ in patients vs. controls), regional gray and white matter volumes, cerebellar tissue microstructural integrity (lower mean diffusivity in the inferior vermis), and lower functional connectivity in the auditory and visuospatial subnetworks. The paper was widely interpreted as demonstrating a novel pattern of brain injury. However, it had a critical methodological flaw: the **control group was healthy volunteers, not government employees with similar occupational profiles**. Notably, 12 of the 40 AHI patients had prior histories of concussion, compared to none in the control group — a confound that could independently account for white matter volume differences. The authors themselves noted the clinical relevance of the imaging differences was "uncertain," but this caveat received little public attention.

## 3. How the NIH Studies Improved on the UPenn Studies

The NIH investigations addressed three foundational weaknesses of the earlier UPenn work:

**Matched controls.** Rather than comparing AHI patients to community volunteers or healthy adults from the general population, both NIH studies used **US government employees with comparable vocational backgrounds, posting histories, and demographic profiles** as controls. This substantially reduces confounding from occupational stress, security training, lifestyle factors, and selection effects inherent in the intelligence community workforce. [Pierpaoli specifically noted](https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2024/03/18/1239087164/nih-studies-no-pattern-harm-havana-syndrome-patients-brains) that NIH used "a control group that was better matched — in terms of age, profession, and location."

**Blinded, reproducibility-oriented analysis.** The NIH neuroimaging protocol was designed to produce highly reproducible metrics across multiple measurements and statistical models (conventional nonparametric analysis, Bayesian multilevel modeling, and principal component analysis). The use of multiple statistical frameworks — and requiring results to survive adjustment for multiple comparisons — directly addressed concerns about false positive rates that plagued the smaller, single-framework UPenn imaging analysis.

**Sample size and heterogeneity.** The NIH neuroimaging study enrolled 81 AHI participants compared to Verma's 40, and tested across geographic locations and AHI subtypes. This power advantage makes null results more meaningful: if a consistent neuroimaging pattern existed across the AHI population, the NIH study was better positioned to detect it.

**Exclusion of prior concussion confound.** The UPenn cohort's unequal distribution of prior TBI history (12 patients vs. 0 controls) was a widely acknowledged confounder. The NIH's better-matched controls and deep phenotyping reduced this issue.

## 4. Reactions

### NIH Researchers

Pierpaoli and Chan were notably careful not to claim the studies proved that "nothing happened." [Pierpaoli stated](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-studies-find-severe-symptoms-havana-syndrome-no-evidence-mri-detectable-brain-injury-or-biological-abnormalities): "It is possible that individuals with an AHI may be experiencing the results of an event that led to their symptoms, but the injury did not produce the long-term neuroimaging changes that are typically observed after severe trauma or stroke." Chan added that if some external phenomenon caused the symptoms, "it did not result in persistent or detectable pathophysiologic change." On the question of attribution, [Chan was explicit](https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2024/03/18/1239087164/nih-studies-no-pattern-harm-havana-syndrome-patients-brains): "Medical research may not be the answer to this... The answer is counterintelligence — somebody taking a photo of whatever it is."

### AHI Victims and Advocates

**Mark Zaid**, a Washington attorney representing many AHI claimants, stated that the lack of significant medical differences between the two groups after a period of time ["does nothing to negate the theory that a foreign adversary is harming U.S. personnel and their families with a type of directed energy."](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/us-study-finds-no-evidence-havana-syndrome-brain-injury-2024-03-18/) The NIH responded that the studies were not designed to determine attribution and could not rule out an external cause.

**Marc Polymeropoulos**, a retired CIA officer and prominent advocate for AHI patients, did not focus on the March 2024 findings directly in public statements, but had previously characterized the NIH study process as coercive: he later told CNN that ["They wanted us to be lab rats for a week before we actually received treatment at Walter Reed — and at the very least, that is unethical and immoral."](https://www.cnn.com/2024/08/30/health/nih-havana-syndrome-study) He believed participation in the NIH study had been mandated by senior CIA leadership. His complaint contributed to the internal ethics review that ultimately [halted the NIH study in August 2024](https://www.cnn.com/2024/08/30/health/nih-havana-syndrome-study) on grounds that informed consent requirements had not been met due to coercion — though the NIH maintained this finding "does not impact the conclusions of the study."

An NIH spokesperson acknowledged the emotional weight: ["We understand that some patients may be disappointed that researchers were unable to identify clear markers of injury."](https://thehill.com/policy/healthcare/4539463-nih-researchers-find-no-evidence-of-havana-syndrome-in-brain-scans/)

### David Relman's Editorial

Relman, a professor of medicine and microbiology and immunology at Stanford, wrote the accompanying JAMA editorial: [Relman DA. Neurological Illness and National Security: Lessons to Be Learned. *JAMA*. 2024;331(13):1093–1095. doi:10.1001/jama.2023.26818](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2816534)

Relman was the chair of the 2020 National Academies of Sciences panel whose report concluded that "directed pulsed radio frequency energy" was the most plausible mechanism for at least a subset of AHI cases. He did not formally retract that view in 2024. Instead, he took the position that the NIH results were not sufficient to close the case. [He told NPR](https://www.npr.org/2024/03/18/1239283017/more-studies-challenge-the-idea-that-havana-syndrome-comes-from-foreign-adversar): "There are multiple problems with both of the studies." He identified two: (1) "the inadequacy of currently available methods for detecting the kind of brain disruption that may be involved in some of these cases" — meaning even high-quality MRI may miss subtle injury, especially if the brain had time to heal; and (2) the mixing of heterogeneous cases, including patients who may have sustained genuine brain injuries alongside those whose symptoms had psychological origins, which would dilute any signal. In his editorial, he [wrote](https://www.cnn.com/2024/03/18/health/havana-syndrome-studies): "One might suspect that nothing, or nothing serious, happened with these cases. This would be ill-advised." He called for better-defined case selection, earlier scanning, and "new sensitive, standardized, non-invasive assessments of nervous system function, particularly those that involve the vestibular system, like more specific blood markers for various types of cellular damage." He also called for institutional surveillance systems and independent review mechanisms. His position was that the NIH studies improved on prior work but were insufficient to settle the matter either way.

### State Department and CIA

The State Department, on the day of publication, said it was reviewing the NIH findings but that Secretary Blinken was "committed to ensuring that employees and their family members reporting an AHI are treated with respect and compassion and receive timely access to medical care." The CIA, following the coercion controversy in August 2024, stated it "remains dedicated to ensuring continued access to care for affected personnel and to thoroughly investigating any health incident reports."

## 5. The Relman Editorial in Context

Relman's editorial is notable for threading a narrow needle. He acknowledged the NIH studies were the most rigorous to date but refused to treat their null findings as definitive. He identified structural problems with both studies that could systematically bias toward null results: the pooling of heterogeneous presentations, the variable delay between incident and imaging, and the insensitivity of current neuroimaging to certain forms of neurological disruption. He also noted that earlier research had identified abnormalities and that the absence of replication does not necessarily mean the prior findings were wrong — they may reflect a signal present only in a well-defined subset. His call for more granular, case-level analysis was consistent with his 2020 NAS view but constituted a notable moderation of it: rather than pointing toward a specific mechanism (directed pulsed RF energy), he was now calling for better methods before any mechanistic conclusion.

## 6. Policy Aftermath: House Intel and ODNI 2025

The NIH JAMA studies fed directly into an already-heated intelligence policy dispute. The March 2024 ODNI had already been citing the NIH research (before its public release) in its March 2023 intelligence assessment concluding it was "very unlikely" a foreign adversary was responsible for AHIs.

In December 2024, [House Intelligence Committee CIA Subcommittee Chairman Rick Crawford released an unclassified interim report](https://intelligence.house.gov/2024/12/05/crawford-cia-subcommittee-release-interim-report-on-havana-syndrome/) that directly challenged both the IC's 2023 assessment and the use of the NIH medical findings as a pillar of that assessment. Crawford wrote that the IC assessment "lacked analytic integrity," that the Committee had "direct evidence" of irregularities in the assessment process, and that it was "increasingly likely a foreign adversary is responsible for some portion of reported AHIs." Committee Chairman Mike Turner said the IC had "actively attempted to impede our investigation." The interim report was careful not to claim all AHIs were explained by foreign action but documented what it characterized as suppression of evidence and flawed analytic processes.

In January 2025, the [ODNI released an updated IC Assessment (ICA 2025-00008-B)](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf), effective as of December 2024. The vast majority of IC components maintained their "very unlikely" judgment on foreign adversary involvement. However, two IC components revised their positions on foreign directed-energy *capability* — one now assessing it "likely" that a foreign actor has an RF antipersonnel capability capable of producing biological effects consistent with some AHI symptoms; another assessing a "roughly even chance." On the medical research line of inquiry specifically, the IC assessment stated: "The IC continues to assess that medical research indicates that US personnel and dependents reporting possible AHIs do not have a consistent set of physical injuries, based on research published in 2024 from the National Institutes of Health (NIH) that reaffirms prior medical analysis." No IC component called into question the genuine suffering of AHI personnel.

## 7. Limitations: What the NIH Studies Do and Do Not Rule Out

The NIH studies rule out a **consistent, detectable, structurally distinctive pattern of brain injury** across the AHI population as a whole, as measured by current-generation MRI methodology at a median of 80 days post-incident. They do not and cannot:

- Rule out a transient acute injury that resolved before scanning (some participants were scanned more than a year post-event).
- Rule out injury below the sensitivity threshold of current MRI (including sub-voxel white matter disruption or neurochemical changes not captured by diffusion or volumetric metrics).
- Rule out an injury pattern present in a well-defined subgroup (e.g., only AHI-1 cases with immediate vestibular and auditory onset, tested within days of the event) that is obscured when mixed with a broader, heterogeneous population.
- Determine the causal mechanism of symptoms. The studies explicitly disclaim any attempt to adjudicate the question of whether an external device caused the incidents.

Some researchers have already begun re-analyzing the Pierpaoli data in exactly this framing. A [post-publication reanalysis](https://ijirms.in/index.php/ijirms/article/view/2152) extracting only AHI-1 validated cases (n = 43) from the Pierpaoli dataset found statistically significant (adjusted p ~0.02) reduced connectivity in the salience network and trend-level white matter microstructural differences consistent with those found by Verma et al. (2019), arguing that heterogeneous case mixing diluted a real signal. This reanalysis remains in a lower-tier journal and has not been independently replicated.

## 8. The Psychogenic / Functional Neurological Disorder Interpretation

The Chan et al. study's finding that 41% of AHI participants met criteria for functional neurological disorders — with most meeting PPPD criteria specifically — is among the most clinically significant findings in either paper. PPPD is a recognized, non-feigned neurological condition in which the brain's threat-detection networks become dysregulated following an acute vestibular or sensory insult (or even a period of significant stress), producing chronic dizziness, postural instability, and cognitive symptoms without structural brain damage. It is, by definition, not psychosomatic in the pejorative sense: it reflects a genuine functional abnormality in neural processing that produces objectively measurable deficits (balance testing in the Chan study showed objective impairment).

Whether the FND/PPPD finding implies that *the initial triggering event* had no physical cause — or merely that the *chronic sequelae* are functional — is a question the study is not designed to answer. PPPD can be triggered by a real vestibular insult, including one that produces no lasting structural MRI-visible change. The [NIH researchers and neuropsychologist Louis French explicitly noted](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-studies-find-severe-symptoms-havana-syndrome-no-evidence-mri-detectable-brain-injury-or-biological-abnormalities) that the elevated PTSD and depression scores were "not surprising given the ongoing concerns of many of the participants" — acknowledging a social and psychological burden that could generate FND-consistent symptoms independently of any external device. The researchers declined to claim that the FND findings confirmed a psychogenic etiology for the *triggering event*, and Relman's editorial specifically cautioned against this inference.

At the same time, the high FND rate is consistent with prior independent analyses: a 2018 FBI assessment that concluded mass psychogenic illness was most likely responsible had been classified and largely suppressed, according to reporting; and a 2018 JASON defense science report also found microwave etiology "highly unlikely" and pointed to stress-induced functional symptoms. The NIH data, particularly in the context of the 2023 and 2025 IC assessments, substantially shifts the scientific center of gravity toward the view that AHIs as a population do not reflect a common external physical injury, while leaving open the possibility of a small subset of genuine directed-energy incidents that produced transient, non-structurally-persistent neurological effects.
