# HAVANA Act of 2021 (Public Law 117-46): Compensation Framework for AHI Victims

## 1. Legislative History

The **Helping American Victims Afflicted by Neurological Attacks (HAVANA) Act of 2021** was introduced in the Senate on May 25, 2021, by Senator Susan Collins (R-ME) ([congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/117th-congress/senate-bill/1828)). The bill's four principal authors — Senators Collins, Jeanne Shaheen (D-NH), Mark Warner (D-VA), and Marco Rubio (R-FL) — represented a deliberately bipartisan coalition spanning both intelligence committee oversight traditions ([Senator Shaheen press release](https://www.shaheen.senate.gov/news/press/us-house-passes-shaheen-backed-legislation-to-support-havana-syndrome-victims)). Additional co-sponsors included Senators John Cornyn (R-TX), Michael Bennet (D-CO), Roy Blunt (R-MO), Kirsten Gillibrand (D-NY), Richard Burr (R-NC), Martin Heinrich (D-NM), and Ben Sasse (R-NE), among others.

The Senate passed the bill unanimously in early June 2021. The House cleared the legislation on September 21, 2021, after months of procedural delays ([CNN, October 8, 2021](https://www.cnn.com/2021/10/08/politics/havana-act-signed)). President Biden signed it into law without a formal ceremony on **October 8, 2021**, issuing a brief statement affirming the government's obligation to care for personnel who "have encountered unusual health occurrences" ([Biden signing statement, govinfo.gov](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/DCPD-202100824/pdf/DCPD-202100824.pdf)). The law was assigned **Public Law No. 117-46**.

The act's passage came roughly five years after the first reports of anomalous neurological symptoms among diplomats at the U.S. Embassy in Havana, Cuba, beginning in late 2016 — incidents that gave rise to the colloquial term "Havana Syndrome." By the time Biden signed the bill, over 200 cases had been documented worldwide, and many victims had been effectively denied medical treatment at Walter Reed National Military Medical Center and the National Institutes of Health, forcing them to navigate private-sector physicians for diagnosis ([CNN, October 8, 2021](https://www.cnn.com/2021/10/08/politics/havana-act-signed)).

## 2. Substantive Provisions

### Covered Agencies and Personnel

The HAVANA Act authorizes the **CIA Director**, the **Secretary of State**, and the heads of other federal agencies to make one-time payments to current and former employees, and their dependents, who sustained qualifying brain injuries on or after **January 1, 2016** ([congress.gov, S.1828](https://www.congress.gov/bill/117th-congress/senate-bill/1828)). There is no geographic restriction — injuries need not have occurred in Cuba or China; any duty station qualifies ([DoD HAVANA Act FAQ, DCPAS](https://www.dcpas.osd.mil/sites/default/files/2025-07/HAVANA%20Act%20Frequently%20Asked%20Questions%20rev%206.12.25%20.pdf)).

### Eligibility: "Qualifying Injury to the Brain"

Under implementing regulations issued by the State Department and DoD, a **qualifying injury to the brain** requires all of the following:

1. The injury occurred in connection with war, insurgency, hostile act, terrorist activity, or another incident designated by the Secretary of State or relevant agency head.
2. The injury was not the result of willful misconduct.
3. The individual meets at least one of the following medical criteria:
   - An **acute brain injury** (concussion, penetrating injury, or event leading to permanent alterations in brain function), confirmed by CT, MRI, or EEG; **or**
   - A medical diagnosis of **traumatic brain injury (TBI) requiring active treatment for 12 months or more**; **or**
   - Acute onset of **new persistent, disabling neurologic symptoms** confirmed by imaging, EEG, or physical exam, requiring active treatment for 12 months or more ([State Dept. 3 FAM 3667, fam.state.gov](https://fam.state.gov/fam/03fam/03fam3660.html); [DoD FAQ, DCPAS](https://www.dcpas.osd.mil/sites/default/files/2025-07/HAVANA%20Act%20Frequently%20Asked%20Questions%20rev%206.12.25%20.pdf)).

Recognized symptoms, based on University of Pennsylvania criteria adopted as of June 26, 2018, include sharp localized ear pain, tinnitus, vertigo, visual focusing issues, nausea, cognitive difficulties, high-frequency unilateral hearing loss, and sleep disturbances ([State Dept. 3 FAM 3662](https://fam.state.gov/fam/03fam/03fam3660.html)).

### Payment Structure

Compensation is delivered as a **non-taxable, one-time lump sum**. Under State Department and CIA implementing regulations published in mid-2022, two tiers exist:

| Tier | Criteria | Amount (2022) | Amount (2023–24) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Base payment** | Qualifying brain injury confirmed by board-certified physician | ~$140,475 (75% of Executive Schedule Level III) | ~$153,000 |
| **Base+ payment** | Severe impacts: no reemployment potential, SSDI approval, or requires full-time caregiver | ~$187,300 (100% of ES Level III) | ~$195,000–$204,000 |

([CBS News, June 2022](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/havana-syndrome-state-department-cia-establish-federal-payment-rules-for-victims/); [Miami Herald, March 2024](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/world/americas/cuba/article287177125.html); [Government Executive, April 2024](https://www.govexec.com/pay-benefits/2024/04/justice-department-outlines-benefits-rule-havana-syndrome-victims/395909/))

Amounts are indexed to Executive Schedule salary levels and therefore increase annually. Compensation is separate from and does not offset Federal Employees' Compensation Act (FECA) workers' compensation benefits ([DOL blog, February 2022](http://blog.dol.gov/2022/02/03/new-guidance-on-havana-syndrome-workers-comp-claims)).

## 4. Claims Data

Comprehensive agency-by-agency HAVANA Act payment tallies are largely classified. The available public data reflects the broader AHI care population rather than pure HAVANA Act payment approvals:

- As of **January 2024**, **334 people** had qualified for care in the Military Health System, per a GAO report published July 2024 — including active-duty military, current and former civilian federal employees, and family members ([Miami Herald, July 2024](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/national/article290608134.html)).
- As of **April 2024**, **257 non-military** current/former government employees and family members had qualified for military health system treatment. Of these:
  - **88** from the CIA/ODNI
  - **74** from DoD (including DIA, NSA, NGA)
  - **44** from the State Department
  - **28** from the FBI
  - **26** from DHS, Agriculture, NSC, USAID ([Miami Herald, July 2024](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/national/article290608134.html))
- The Senate Intelligence Committee reported that as of December 31, 2023, **all but one** CIA AHI reporter approved for the CIA's Expanded Care Program had also received HAVANA Act payments ([Senate Intelligence Committee report, May 2025](https://www.intelligence.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/AP_Report_24_01_R.pdf)).
- DoD only began processing HAVANA Act payment applications in **July 2025** — years after other agencies — meaning military-specific payment counts are not yet publicly available ([DefenseScoop, July 2025](https://defensescoop.com/2025/07/14/havana-syndrome-dod-military-payment-applications/)).

No public accounting of total aggregate dollar amounts paid out government-wide has been published; CIA payment data remains classified.

## 5. Notable Cases

**Marc Polymeropoulos (CIA):** The most prominent public AHI claimant. A 26-year CIA veteran and former senior operations officer, Polymeropoulos developed severe, persistent headaches and vertigo following what he believes was an AHI incident in Moscow in December 2017. He retired from the CIA's Senior Intelligence Service in July 2019, attributing his departure to continuing symptoms ([Lawfare, September 2021](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/lawfare-podcast-marc-polymeropoulos-cia-911-and-havana-syndrome)). He received compensation under the HAVANA Act based on a formal TBI diagnosis from Walter Reed, which explicitly noted the absence of any pre-existing condition ([Miami Herald, March 2024](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/world/americas/cuba/article287177125.html)). Polymeropoulos became one of the most vocal advocates for victims, publicly criticizing CIA senior leadership for denigrating affected officers and calling for accountability ([War on the Rocks, March 2026](https://warontherocks.com/we-were-right-about-havana-syndrome/)).

**Mark Lenzi (State Department — Guangzhou):** A Diplomatic Security Service officer, Lenzi began experiencing symptoms in 2017 while assigned to the U.S. Consulate in Guangzhou, China. He and his wife underwent the Havana Acquired Brain Injury Test (HABIT) in June 2018, both demonstrating brain injury symptoms consistent with directed-energy exposure. In December 2021, Lenzi became the **first AHI victim to file a federal lawsuit against the U.S. government**, suing Secretary of State Blinken for disability discrimination ([CNN, December 2021](https://www.cnn.com/2021/12/09/politics/state-department-mark-lenzi-lawsuit-havana-syndrome); [Politico, October 2021](https://www.politico.com/news/2021/10/25/state-department-2018-directed-energy-exposure-517055)). The State Department ultimately paid Lenzi and his family over $1 million for their injuries. In 2024, Lenzi alleged whistleblower retaliation after publicly urging Congress to allow him to brief on AHIs, which he attributed to "pulsed microwave standoff attacks by Russian military intelligence operatives" ([YouTube/NewsNation, November 2024](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rp6fF8xd1QE)). A federal judge denied the State Department's bid to dismiss Lenzi's suit in February 2026 ([Bloomberg Law, February 2026](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/state-department-fails-to-toss-havana-syndrome-benefit-suit)).

**Greg Edgreen (DoD/DIA — Advocacy):** Retired Army Lieutenant Colonel Greg Edgreen led the Defense Intelligence Agency's internal investigation into AHI causation. He has been publicly outspoken that a foreign adversary — specifically Russia — is behind AHI incidents, citing a pattern of Russian connections in cases he reviewed. After retiring, he founded **Advanced Echelon LLC**, an organization providing care for AHI survivors and their families ([PBS NewsHour, April 2024](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/havana-syndrome-investigator-on-accusing-russia-of-targeting-u-s-officials)). Edgreen testified before the House Homeland Security Committee and was a central figure in the January 2026 committee investigation into reports that Homeland Security Investigations had acquired a Russian-linked pulsed RF device ([House Homeland Security Committee, January 2026](https://homeland.house.gov/2026/01/15/homeland-republicans-investigate-reports-of-havana-syndrome-device-acquired-by-dhs-under-the-biden-administration/)).

## 6. The Tension Between the HAVANA Act and the 2023 IC Assessment

The HAVANA Act carefully avoided the word "attack," requiring only that injuries occur "in connection with war, insurgency, hostile act, terrorist activity, or other incidents designated" by agency heads — a deliberately agnostic formulation that decoupled compensation eligibility from attribution to a specific adversary.

However, in **March 2023**, the National Intelligence Council released an Intelligence Community Assessment concluding that it was **"very unlikely"** a foreign adversary was responsible for reported AHIs, with five IC components holding moderate-to-high or moderate confidence in that judgment ([ODNI December 2024 update, dni.gov](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf)). The CIA used this analytic conclusion — and an earlier January 2022 interim assessment — as a direct basis for rolling back AHI-related programs and contesting employees' benefits.

The Senate Intelligence Committee found that by December 31, 2023, the CIA had **not concurred with the Fact of Injury-Factual element of a single AHI reporter's workers' compensation claim** since the initial Havana incidents, consistent with its institutional position that AHIs were not foreign attacks. This produced a stark disparity: only **21% of CIA AHI applicants** had been approved for workers' compensation as of that date, compared to **67% of AHI applicants from other U.S. government agencies** ([Senate Intelligence Committee report, May 2025](https://www.intelligence.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/AP_Report_24_01_R.pdf)).

The committee found the situation "untenable": the CIA would not concur with the factual basis of its employees' workers' compensation claims for the same AHI for which it had approved Expanded Care Program access — and, in virtually all approved cases, HAVANA Act payments ([Senate Intel report](https://www.intelligence.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/AP_Report_24_01_R.pdf)). Unlike at other agencies, the CIA inserted counterintelligence analysts into the care and benefits adjudication process, making individual claim determinations difficult to separate from the agency's institutional analytic line.

HAVANA Act payments themselves proved more resilient to the IC assessment than workers' compensation: because the law requires only a medical diagnosis of qualifying brain injury — not proof of foreign causation — payments continued even as the CIA contested workers' comp claims. The Senate committee explicitly recommended that medical and benefits decisions be insulated from analytic conclusions about attribution.

## 7. DoD Extension and Military Personnel Coverage

The original HAVANA Act covered CIA and State Department personnel most explicitly, with broader language for "other agency heads." The **FY2022 NDAA** required DoD to establish an AHI Cross-Functional Team and mandated military health system access for AHI patients. The **FY2023 NDAA** (P.L. 117-263, signed December 23, 2022) formally extended HAVANA Act payment authority to **DoD civilian employees** and their dependents, requiring DoD to issue implementing regulations ([Congressional Research Service](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R47869.html)).

Notwithstanding this expansion, DoD did not finalize its implementing rule until **July 2024** and did not begin processing applications until **July 2025** — effectively leaving DoD-affiliated AHI victims without a compensation pathway for roughly two years after the statutory extension ([DefenseScoop, July 2025](https://defensescoop.com/2025/07/14/havana-syndrome-dod-military-payment-applications/)). The GAO July 2024 report specifically noted that at the time of the report, DoD had still not issued payment guidance to affected personnel, while simultaneously publishing a proposed rule on the same day the report was released ([Miami Herald, July 2024](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/national/article290608134.html)). As of February 2026, there had been **more than 1,500 total AHI reports** across U.S. government since 2016 ([CBS News, February 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/senators-jeanne-shaheen-susan-collins-pentagon-havana-syndrome-research/)).

## 8. Outstanding Controversies

### Denied Claims and Appeals

Three anonymous State Department foreign service workers filed suit after their HAVANA Act claims were denied on the grounds that they failed to tie their symptoms directly to their injuries with sufficient specificity. A federal court declined to dismiss that lawsuit in **February 2026**, allowing the case to proceed ([Bloomberg Law, February 2026](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/state-department-fails-to-toss-havana-syndrome-benefit-suit)). The litigation signals that the eligibility criteria, while broad in statute, have been applied restrictively in practice.

### Burden of Proof and Medical Gatekeeping

Affected personnel have reported a profound mismatch between the law's apparent intent and its administrative implementation. GAO interviews with 65 AHI patients found that **nearly half reported being stigmatized by their employer** after reporting symptoms, including security clearance delays, forced leave, and loss of assignments ([GAO report via Miami Herald, July 2024](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/national/article290608134.html)). One anonymous patient told the Miami Herald: "We need to prove a brain injury. But doctors we are sent to are told not to write brain injury. And they'll never accept anything else additional. This is how they're making it go away."

A Department of Labor rule published in 2024 added further administrative friction by requiring a District Medical Advisor review for "claims for additional conditions beyond traumatic brain injuries" — affecting both new and already-accepted claims ([Miami Herald, July 2024](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/national/article290608134.html)).

### Walter Reed and Federal Medical Facilities

Prior to legislative and administrative reforms beginning around 2021, many AHI claimants were refused treatment at Walter Reed and NIH, forcing them into the private sector. The shift was symbolically important: Walter Reed's month-long intensive TBI program — which Polymeropoulos credited with being life-changing — cost the federal government approximately **$37 per patient** by his account ([War on the Rocks, March 2026](https://warontherocks.com/we-were-right-about-havana-syndrome/)). Denying access to that care for years, while administratively contesting compensation, is a central grievance in victim advocacy.

## 9. The Shifting Causation Picture and Its Effect on the Compensation Framework

The HAVANA Act was designed to be causation-agnostic, but the evolving intelligence and medical picture has created ongoing political and legal turbulence around the compensation program.

**NIH 2024:** A March 2024 NIH study — examining MRI scans of affected personnel — found **no consistent evidence of MRI-detectable brain injury** and no significant biological differences compared to control groups ([NIH study via Miami Herald](https://www.miamiherald.com/news/nation-world/national/article290608134.html); [ODNI December 2024 assessment](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf)). In August 2024, however, NIH halted the study entirely after an internal review found participants had been **coerced into participation** — a finding that patients attributed to CIA pressure, and that generated a separate research integrity controversy ([CNN, August 2024](https://www.cnn.com/2024/08/30/health/nih-havana-syndrome-study)). NIH stated the cancellation did not alter the studies' substantive conclusions, but patient advocates sharply disputed the methodology.

**ODNI December 2024 / January 2025:** An updated IC Assessment released in January 2025 reaffirmed that **"most of the IC continues to assess that it is 'very unlikely' a foreign adversary is responsible"** for reported AHIs, though two components subtly shifted their assessments about whether a foreign actor might have capability to cause "biological effects consistent with some symptoms" in a small number of cases ([ODNI December 2024 assessment, dni.gov](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf)).

**December 2024 House Intelligence Subcommittee Report:** The House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence's CIA Subcommittee, chaired by Rep. Rick Crawford (R-AR), released an unclassified interim report concluding that the 2023 IC Assessment "lacked analytic integrity," was developed in a "manner inconsistent with analytic integrity," and that "it appears increasingly likely and the Chairman is convinced that a foreign adversary is behind some AHIs" ([HPSCI report, December 2024](https://intelligence.house.gov/2024/12/05/crawford-cia-subcommittee-release-interim-report-on-havana-syndrome/)). The report cited direct evidence that the 2023 ICA "was developed in a manner inconsistent with analytic integrity" and that the IC had "attempted to thwart the Subcommittee's investigative efforts" ([HPSCI unclassified AHI report PDF](https://intelligence.house.gov/uploadedfiles/unclassified_ahi_report.pdf)).

**March 2026 Retraction:** In March 2026, U.S. intelligence, military, and law enforcement officials — including DNI Tulsi Gabbard, FBI Director Kash Patel, and NSA acting director William Hartman — **unanimously agreed to withdraw the 2023/2025 IC Assessment** concluding foreign involvement was "very unlikely," after the House committee chairman characterized the situation as a "cover-up" ([Global News, March 2026](https://globalnews.ca/news/11737905/havana-syndrome-foreign-actor-cover-up-canada/)). CIA Director John Ratcliffe acknowledged to the Senate that "there are disparate opinions across" the IC about causation and indicated DNI Gabbard would undertake a comprehensive review ([YouTube/C-SPAN, March 2026](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BW6qd4IVoIE)). Adding material weight to these developments, CNN reported that Homeland Security Investigations had secretly acquired in 2024, for an **eight-figure sum**, a pulsed RF device containing **Russian components** that "fits inside a backpack and emits the kind of energy long suspected in most severe Havana syndrome cases" ([Grey Dynamics, March 2026, summarizing CNN](https://greydynamics.com/havana-syndrome-new-evidence-reignites-debate/)).

**Framework Resilience:** The retraction of the IC assessment does not automatically alter HAVANA Act eligibility determinations. Compensation has always been premised on medical injury — not proven foreign causation. But the retraction does have practical consequences: it undermines the legal basis for the CIA's posture of contesting workers' compensation claims, and it strengthens the advocacy position of victims who allege the agency's analytic line contaminated their benefit adjudications. The February 2026 court decision refusing to dismiss the three State employees' HAVANA Act suit suggests federal courts may begin scrutinizing denial decisions more closely as the attribution picture continues to shift.
