# Joseph C. Sharp and Mark Grove: Career Trace After the 1973 Microwave-Speech Demonstration

*Research compiled from Wikipedia, declassified government documents, published scientific literature, and secondary sources. Where attribution is uncertain, that is noted explicitly.*

---

## The 1973 Demonstration: What Actually Happened

The demonstration typically cited as occurring in "1973" has a slightly ambiguous timeline. The experiment itself — in which Sharp and Grove transmitted single-syllable words (digits 1–10) via zero-crossing-modulated microwave pulses, with subjects (themselves) hearing nine of ten correctly — is referred to in Don Justesen's 1975 article in *American Psychologist* as a [personal communication dated September 28, 1973](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf). The associated peer-reviewed paper appeared in 1974 (see citation below). The distinction matters: what circulates in popular media as "the 1973 demonstration" is largely a shorthand for an experiment whose only contemporaneous primary record was a personal communication to Justesen.

**Full citation for the 1974 paper:**

> Sharp, J. C., Grove, H. M., & Gandhi, O. P. (1974). Generation of acoustic signals by pulsed microwave energy. *IEEE Transactions on Microwave Theory and Techniques*, 22, 583–584.

Note that the co-author's initial is **H. M. Grove**, not simply "Mark Grove." Sources including the Justesen article and a State Department DSS social media post refer to him as "H. M. Grove," while popular accounts call him "Mark Grove." Whether H. M. Grove's given name is indeed "Mark" is unconfirmed by any primary source found in this research. The two names are used interchangeably in secondary literature without sourced justification. See the [Microwaves and Behavior PDF](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf) and the [Wikipedia article on the Microwave Auditory Effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect) for representative usages.

The paper's specific contribution, per the *IEEE Transactions* abstract (as reproduced in Zory Glaser's archive), was a discussion of the production of sound when short microwave pulses are directed at an absorber, including possible thermoelastic mechanisms. This was separate from but complementary to the speech-transmission experiment referenced by Justesen, which remained only in the form of the personal communication note.

## Joseph Cecil Sharp II (May 30, 1934 – January 13, 2021)

### Background Before Walter Reed

Sharp was born in Salt Lake City, Utah. He received his B.S., M.S., and Ph.D. in psychology and neuroanatomy from the [University of Utah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_C._Sharp). His training combined experimental psychology with neuroanatomy — an interdisciplinary background that positioned him for behavioral radiology work.

### Role at Walter Reed Army Institute of Research (1961–1974)

Sharp [joined WRAIR in 1961](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_C._Sharp) as a research psychologist. He rose to become:

- **Chief, Department of Experimental Psychology and Behavioral Radiology**
- **Deputy Director of Neuropsychiatry** (appointed 1970)

He also served simultaneously as **Deputy Commissioner of Public Health for the State of New York** in 1969–1970, an unusual concurrent civilian appointment suggesting broader government connections.

According to the [*Foreign Policy* account of Project Pandora](https://foreignpolicy.com/2017/08/25/the-secret-history-of-diplomats-and-invisible-weapons-russia-cuba/), ARPA contacted Sharp — described there as "former director of research in experimental psychology at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research" — to build what became one of the best-equipped laboratories in the U.S. for studying biopsychological effects of microwave radiation. The same account, confirmed by Don Justesen's 1975 article, notes that Sharp worked alongside Mark Grove in constructing this laboratory in response to the discovery of the Soviet microwave bombardment of the U.S. Embassy in Moscow.

Sharp also appears on the [Walter Reed Army Institute of Research Wikipedia page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Reed_Army_Institute_of_Research) in the list of notable WRAIR researchers under the Neuropsychiatry Division, alongside figures such as David Hubel (later a Nobel laureate) and Robert Galambos.

The *Foreign Policy* article notes Sharp **left the Pandora program in 1968** — before the speech-transmission experiment — though he clearly continued microwave research at WRAIR through at least 1973–1974.

### Publications at Walter Reed

The primary attributable publications from this period are:

1. **Sharp, J. C., & Grove, H. M. (1973).** Individual perception of pulsed microwave radiation. *Symposium on Biological Effects and Measurement of Radio Frequency/Microwaves*, HEW Publication (FDA) 77-8026. (Cited in Substack synthesis by [Peter Anthony Cowan](https://peteranthonycowan.substack.com/p/the-cia-said-the-science-behind-havana).)
2. **Sharp, J. C., Grove, H. M., & Gandhi, O. P. (1974).** Generation of acoustic signals by pulsed microwave energy. *IEEE Transactions on Microwave Theory and Techniques*, 22, 583–584.

The speech-transmission experiment itself — recognizing nine of ten spoken digits transmitted via voice-modulated microwaves — was reported only as a personal communication to Justesen (Note 2 in his 1975 paper), not as a stand-alone peer-reviewed article. This is a crucial point for anyone attempting to assess the scientific record: the most-cited claim about Sharp and Grove rests on a second-hand report in a journal article, not a primary publication by Sharp or Grove themselves.

No additional Sharp publications specifically on microwave bioeffects or behavioral radiology from the 1975–1994 period were located in PubMed, Google Scholar, or IEEE Xplore searches. This absence is consistent with his career transition to NASA Ames (see below), where his research focus shifted to space psychology and human factors, not RF bioeffects.

### Post-Walter Reed Career (1974–2021)

Sharp [moved to NASA's Ames Research Center in 1974](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_C._Sharp), where he worked until 1994 — a 20-year tenure. For the final five years (approximately 1989–1994), he served as **Director of the Office of Space Research** at Ames. This role involved overseeing behavioral and life sciences research relevant to long-duration spaceflight, a clear thematic continuation of his behavioral research background but entirely removed from microwave bioeffects.

After retiring from Ames in 1994, Sharp returned to Utah and took a teaching position at **Southern Utah University's College of Science** in Cedar City, Utah — a relatively modest academic post for someone with his institutional history.

He died on January 13, 2021, and was buried in the Tonaquint Cemetery in Saint George, Utah.

### Public Comments on the Sharp & Grove Demo

No record was found of Sharp making any documented public statement — interview, oral history, conference talk, or written account — specifically addressing the 1973 microwave-speech demonstration after the fact. His Wikipedia stub page contains no such references, and searches of oral history archives (including the Engineering and Technology History Wiki) returned no Sharp interview. Given that he died in January 2021, before most of the intense Havana Syndrome media coverage of 2021–2026, and that he had spent his last three decades in space research and university teaching, this absence is not surprising but remains unconfirmed rather than definitively absent.

### Involvement in Havana Syndrome / V2K Discourse

Sharp (or his name) has been extensively invoked in Havana Syndrome discourse, V2K forums, and "targeted individual" communities — but there is no evidence Sharp himself participated in or responded to any of this. His death in January 2021 preceded much of the peak public attention to these topics. Secondary sources such as the [Substack piece by Peter Anthony Cowan](https://peteranthonycowan.substack.com/p/the-cia-said-the-science-behind-havana) and the [Academia.edu 2026 paper "The Microwave Auditory Effect, Radar Research, RF Bioeffects, and Havana Syndrome"](https://www.academia.edu/165021818/The_Microwave_Auditory_Effect_Radar_Research_RF_Bioeffects_and_Havana_Syndrome_A_Comprehensive_Historical_and_Technical_Analysis_1940s_2026) cite the Sharp & Grove demonstration as foundational precedent. The Wikipedia article on the [Microwave Auditory Effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect) notes that "numerous individuals suffering from auditory hallucinations, delusional disorders, or other mental illnesses have claimed that government agents use forms of mind control technologies based on microwave signals," and directly references the Sharp & Grove work as a historical touchstone misappropriated by these communities.

No estate response to these invocations has been documented.

## Mark Grove / H. M. Grove

### Attribution Warning

This is the most uncertain section of this research. "Mark Grove" is a common name. The published scientific record names the second author only as **H. M. Grove**. The use of "Mark" in popular sources appears to derive from secondary reporting rather than from any primary source identifying the first name. **No source was found that establishes "Mark" as Grove's given name with primary citation.** It is possible that "Mark" is a common abbreviation or nickname for a middle name, or that this attribution is simply an error that propagated through secondary literature. Any career trace of "Mark Grove" that relies solely on the given name should be treated as tentative.

### Background Before Walter Reed

No biographical information about H. M. Grove's pre-WRAIR career was located. Don Justesen's 1975 article in *American Psychologist*, which is the most detailed contemporaneous description of the Sharp-Grove collaboration, describes Grove only as "an electronic engineer" — no institution, no credentials, no prior history. The same characterization appears in the [Microwaves and Behavior PDF](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf). This suggests Grove's role was primarily technical/engineering rather than that of a credentialed researcher with an independent scientific profile.

### Role at Walter Reed

Grove is described consistently across sources as an **electronic engineer** who co-built (with Sharp) the WRAIR microwave research laboratory, working on the biophysical infrastructure of the experiments rather than leading the behavioral research design. He appears to have been the technician who actually configured the microwave apparatus used in the speech-transmission demonstration. Beyond his name on the 1974 IEEE paper and the 1973 HEW symposium paper, and his mention in Justesen's personal communication footnote, no additional publications by Grove were located.

### Post-1975 Career

No career information for H. M. Grove after the mid-1970s was found in any source — not in PubMed, IEEE Xplore, Google Scholar, or general web searches. This is consistent with his apparent role as a government technical employee (civilian engineer at WRAIR) rather than an independent researcher who would have maintained a publication record. Such personnel typically do not appear in academic databases. It is also possible he continued in DoD or Army Research Laboratory roles under classifications that preclude public record.

### Public Comments and Havana Syndrome / V2K Discourse

No public statement by Grove on any topic — including the 1973 demonstration, Havana Syndrome, or V2K — was found. His current status (alive or deceased) is unknown.

## The 1974 Citation in Context

The paper — Sharp, J. C., Grove, H. M., & Gandhi, O. P. (1974) in *IEEE Transactions on Microwave Theory and Techniques*, vol. 22, pp. 583–584 — is a two-page letter-format paper dealing with the *physical mechanism* (acoustic signals from microwave pulses in absorbers), not a full write-up of the speech transmission experiment. Omar P. Gandhi, the third author, was a University of Utah professor of electrical engineering who contributed to the dosimetry and mechanism analysis. Gandhi went on to a substantial independent career in RF bioeffects and dosimetry research. The speech-transmission experiment that became famous remained, technically, only in Justesen's footnote.

This distinction is almost never preserved in popular accounts, which conflate the speech demonstration (personal communication, 1973) with the peer-reviewed paper (acoustic transduction mechanism, 1974) as if they were the same publication.
