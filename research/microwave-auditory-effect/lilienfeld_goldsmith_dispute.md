# The Lilienfeld–Goldsmith Dispute: Moscow Embassy Microwave Epidemiology

## 1. The 1978 Lilienfeld Report

From 1953 to 1976, Soviet intelligence services aimed microwave beams of 2.5–4.0 GHz at the U.S. Embassy building in Moscow. The signal was first detected in 1953, monitored continuously from the early 1960s, and kept secret from embassy personnel until 1976. When exposure intensity increased sharply in mid-1975—reaching a maximum of 15 μW/cm² for up to 18 hours per day, compared with a maximum of ~5 μW/cm² during the preceding two decades—the U.S. Department of State commissioned a full epidemiological investigation ([NSArchive, 2022](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/briefing-book/intelligence-russia-programs/2022-09-13/moscow-signals-declassified-microwave)).

The resulting study, formally titled **"Foreign Service Health Status Study: Evaluation of Health Status of Foreign Service and Other Employees from Selected Eastern European Posts"** (Report #PB288163, submitted July 31, 1978), was led by Abraham M. Lilienfeld, M.D., of the Department of Epidemiology at the Johns Hopkins University School of Hygiene and Public Health. The report runs to more than 400 pages and was never published in peer-reviewed form; it exists as a government technical report ([Environmental Health Trust, 2017](https://ehtrust.org/health-effects-seen-us-embassy-employees-exposed-microwave-radiation-cold-war/)).

### Study Design and Sample

The Lilienfeld study is a retrospective cohort design. The **exposed group** comprised all employees who served at the Moscow embassy between January 1, 1953, and June 30, 1976, and their adult and child dependents who resided in Moscow. The **comparison group** comprised employees (and their dependents) who served during the same period at one of eight other Eastern European U.S. embassies and consulates: Belgrade, Bucharest, Budapest, Leningrad, Prague, Sofia, Warsaw, and Zagreb—chosen to match the Moscow group for work context, social milieu, dietary patterns, and selection criteria ([Elwood, *Environmental Health*, 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)).

The cohort included:
- **1,827 Moscow employees** (of whom 1,205 had medical records reviewed; 869 completed Health History Questionnaires)
- **2,561 comparison-group employees** (1,889 with medical records; 984 completing HHQs)
- **2,819 adult dependents** (1,061 Moscow; 1,758 comparison)
- **5,474 child dependents** (2,161 Moscow; 3,313 comparison)

This totals roughly 12,000 individuals identified across both arms, with employees averaging 7 medical examinations each over the study period. Tracing was highly successful—over 94% of employees were located—but questionnaire response rates were suboptimal: ~51% for Moscow employees and ~40% for comparison employees, acknowledged as a limitation but applied equally to both groups.

### Exposure Assessment

Monitoring data were sparse before 1963 and nearly continuous thereafter. From 1953 to May 1975, maximum irradiance reached ~5 μW/cm² for nine hours per day, affecting the west facade between the 3rd and 8th floors. From May 1975 to February 1976, a second (and stronger) signal was added from the east; average staff exposures reached 1.3–10.2 μW/cm², with a maximum peak of ~24 μW/cm² for limited periods on the 10th floor. Copper screening installed in February 1976 reduced internal exposures to less than 0.1 μW/cm² ([Elwood 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)). For comparison posts, periodic measurements (at least annually) found "only background levels" of RF throughout the study period.

### Key Findings

**Mortality**: Both groups displayed a pronounced healthy worker effect. Standardized Mortality Ratios (SMRs) versus the general U.S. white population were 0.47 for Moscow employees and 0.59 for the comparison group (all causes). All-cancer SMRs were 0.89 (Moscow) and 1.1 (other embassies)—not significantly different from each other or from 1.0. Brain tumor SMR was 0.0 in Moscow (zero observed vs. 0.9 expected) and 3.3 in the comparison group (5 observed vs. 1.5 expected)—the comparison group showing the elevation, not the exposed group. Leukemia SMRs were 2.5 (Moscow, 2 cases) and 1.8 (comparison, 3 cases)—both elevated relative to the U.S. population but statistically insignificant given tiny numbers, and not differentiated from each other ([Elwood 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)).

**Morbidity**: Of 70 specific diseases examined in men by medical record, three were statistically more common in Moscow (protozoal intestinal diseases, benign neoplasms, diseases of the peripheral ganglia) and one more common in comparison (pneumonia). Among 70 diseases in women, only pregnancy complications were significantly elevated in Moscow. Crucially, **none of these findings showed a dose-response relationship with either estimated microwave exposure level or length of service in Moscow**—a key test that Lilienfeld applied to distinguish RF effects from confounding.

Self-reported symptoms on Health History Questionnaires showed a pattern of more symptoms among Moscow employees (e.g., depression, irritability, difficulty concentrating, memory loss in men), but within the Moscow group these symptoms were paradoxically *less* common among those classified as microwave-exposed than among those classified as unexposed—an internal inconsistency that Lilienfeld interpreted as evidence against a causal RF association.

## 2. Why Lilienfeld Concluded No Convincing RF Effect

Lilienfeld's conclusion was carefully worded: *"No convincing evidence was discovered that would directly implicate the exposure to microwave radiation experienced by the employees at the Moscow embassy in the causation of any adverse health effects as of the time of this analysis"* ([Elwood 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/), quoting the original report, p. 246).

The reasoning rested on three analytical pillars that the investigators themselves specified in advance:

1. **Moscow vs. comparison**: Any RF-caused condition should be more common in Moscow than in the control embassies. For cancer and almost all serious morbidities, this difference was absent.
2. **Internal dose-response in Moscow**: Within the Moscow group, conditions should be more common in the more-exposed subgroup. This was not observed for any cancer endpoint.
3. **Duration effect**: Conditions should be more common in those with longer Moscow service. Again, not observed for any serious endpoint.

The team acknowledged major limitations—"hundreds of comparisons" made without formal multiple-testing correction, a relatively short observation window (the peak exposure period lasted only eight months before screening was installed), and incomplete questionnaire return rates. They explicitly recommended long-term follow-up surveillance, which the State Department never implemented.

## 3. John R. Goldsmith — Biographical Context

John R. Goldsmith (1919–1999) was one of the most prominent environmental epidemiologists of the mid-to-late 20th century. A 1942 Reed College graduate and 1945 Harvard Medical School graduate, he built his career on the health effects of air pollution at the California State Department of Health from the late 1950s. He served as the first environmental epidemiologist at the World Health Organization headquarters in Geneva (1964–1966), held visiting professorships in Stockholm, and worked with the National Cancer Institute on environmental cancer causation. In 1978—the same year the Lilienfeld report was finalized—he joined the Faculty of Health Sciences at **Ben-Gurion University of the Negev** in Beersheba, Israel, where he held the position of Professor of Epidemiology until retirement ([Reed Magazine, 1999](https://www.reed.edu/reed-magazine/in-memoriam/obituaries/february2000/john-r-goldsmith-1942.html)).

Goldsmith was a founding member and president of the International Society for Environmental Epidemiology and authored four books and hundreds of research papers. He is credited with some of the earliest systematic environmental epidemiology methodology. He died in Omer, Israel, in October 1999 ([San Francisco Chronicle, 1999](https://www.sfchronicle.com/news/article/John-Goldsmith-2899276.php)).

His introduction to the Moscow data came not through independent research but through a lawyer representing a State Department employee whose wife, a Moscow embassy resident, had developed breast cancer. That lawyer provided Goldsmith with some preliminary case counts which Goldsmith compared to U.S. general population rates, arriving at a rough estimate of 6–8 times higher cancer incidence—a figure derived from a small, self-selected legal caseload, not the full cohort ([Elwood 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)). After the Lilienfeld report was published, the same lawyer provided Goldsmith with additional FOIA-obtained material, including blood study data and employee allegations that exposure data had been altered or withheld—claims Goldsmith incorporated into his subsequent publications.

## 4. Goldsmith's Reanalyses (1995–1997)

Goldsmith published his primary reinterpretations in two venue series:

- **Goldsmith JR (1995)**. "Epidemiologic Evidence of Radiofrequency Radiation (Microwave) Effects on Health in Military, Broadcasting, and Occupational Studies." *International Journal of Occupational and Environmental Health* 1(1):47–57. Notably, Goldsmith himself labeled this paper "an opinion piece, not intended to be a balanced presentation of the literature" ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9990158/)).

- **Goldsmith JR (1997)**. "Epidemiologic Evidence Relevant to Radar (Microwave) Effects." *Environmental Health Perspectives* 105(Suppl 6):1579–1587 ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1469943/)).

Goldsmith also contributed to symposium publications and cited the Moscow data in an informal 1985 paper and a 1998 *Archives of Environmental Health* commentary.

His core claim: the Moscow data, properly analyzed, revealed statistically significant excesses of all-site cancer, leukemia, brain tumors, and breast cancer. His conclusion was that "RF radiation be considered a carcinogenic risk" and that the ICNIRP standard-setting process made "little use of epidemiological data" ([Neil Cherry, quoting Goldsmith 1997](https://www.electricalpollution.com/documents/Cherry2000EMR_ICNIRP_critique_09-02.pdf)). He extended this argument to support precautionary reductions in occupational and public RF exposure limits.

### Goldsmith's Methodological Moves

The key analytical move in Goldsmith's reinterpretation involved two departures from the Lilienfeld design:

1. **Abandoning the Moscow-vs.-other comparison**: Goldsmith asserted—without direct measurement evidence—that comparison embassy staff had also been subjected to Soviet RF surveillance. He therefore treated both the Moscow and other-embassy groups as "exposed" and compared their combined cancer mortality against general U.S. population expected rates.

2. **Pooling employees and dependents**: By adding adult dependents (who had lower, less certain exposures) and child dependents to the employee cancer counts, he substantially inflated both numerators and denominators.

When Moscow and other-embassy data were combined and contrasted with U.S. population rates, significant excesses emerged: all cancers observed 116 vs. expected 80.5 (p<0.05); leukemia observed 10 vs. expected 4.14 (p<0.05); brain tumors observed 8 vs. expected 3.62 (borderline). Goldsmith presented these as evidence that RF exposure caused cancer across all embassy sites ([Elwood 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)).

## 5. Methodological Objections to Goldsmith

Multiple specific objections apply to Goldsmith's analytical approach:

**i. The comparison group invalidation problem.** The entire inferential value of the Lilienfeld design rested on the other-embassy employees as a comparator. Goldsmith collapsed this distinction based solely on unverified employee allegations and a prior plausibility claim—that Soviet intelligence agencies might have surveilled other U.S. embassies. The Lilienfeld report explicitly checked: *"only background levels have been detected at these Eastern European embassies"* (p. 3). Goldsmith provided no independent measurement data, no signals intelligence, and no physical dosimetry to contradict this. His position depended entirely on assumption ([Elwood 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)).

**ii. Inappropriate baseline (general U.S. population).** Comparing embassy employees and their dependents—a highly selected, medically supervised, internationally mobile population—against the general U.S. population unavoidably introduces severe confounding. Both the Moscow and comparison groups showed strong healthy worker effects (overall SMRs of 0.47 and 0.59, respectively). When Goldsmith used U.S. expected rates, he was comparing a health-selected cohort against an unselected reference, inflating apparent relative risks spuriously. The appropriate reference was always the matched comparison group, not a national baseline.

**iii. Multiple comparisons without correction.** The original Lilienfeld report examined "literally hundreds" of health outcomes. Under such conditions, nominally significant p-values (p<0.05) should be expected by chance at a rate of ~1 in 20. Several Goldsmith-highlighted "excesses" (leukemia, breast cancer in Moscow employees) had wide confidence intervals entirely consistent with chance variation: the Moscow leukemia SMR of 2.5 had a 95% CI of 0.3–9.0; the breast cancer SMR of 4.0 had a 95% CI of 0.5–14.4. Neither was statistically significant in the primary analysis ([Elwood 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)).

**iv. Failure of dose-response and exposure-gradient criteria.** The Lilienfeld team's pre-specified key test was whether any condition increased with higher estimated microwave exposure or longer Moscow service. For cancer endpoints, no such gradient existed—not for Moscow employees overall, not for those in the highest-exposure floors, not by years served. Goldsmith's analyses did not address or refute this internal negative finding.

**v. Selective presentation.** Goldsmith's tables showed leukemia deaths for employees and child dependents (which showed excess) but omitted adult dependents (which did not). His exposure characterization described levels of "5–18 μW/cm²" as estimated ranges, not peak maxima, potentially overstating typical exposures. He cited multiple-site cancer data (1.33 sites/person vs. 1.02 expected in Moscow women) without confidence intervals.

**vi. Data source problems.** Much of Goldsmith's supplementary material was obtained through FOIA requests filtered through a plaintiff attorney with a financial interest in a favorable interpretation. Goldsmith alleged that data on exposures and cancers had been deliberately withheld or altered in the Lilienfeld report, but provided no documentary evidence sufficient to establish this claim as fact.

## 6. Elwood's 2012 PMC Review

J. Mark Elwood (University of Auckland) published **"Microwaves in the cold war: the Moscow embassy study and its interpretation"** in *Environmental Health* in November 2012 ([PMC3509929](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)). This is the most rigorous and comprehensive peer-reviewed treatment of the entire dataset.

Elwood conducted a detailed re-examination of the original Lilienfeld report, reconstructed all key tables, and reviewed all subsequent published interpretations including Goldsmith's papers, ICNIRP review citations, and independent commentaries.

His conclusions were unambiguous:

> "The conclusions of the original report are supported. Contrary conclusions given in some other reports are due to misinterpretation of the results."

Elwood specifically identified the mechanism of Goldsmith's error: by combining Moscow and other-embassy groups on the unverified assumption of equal exposure, and then comparing against the U.S. population while ignoring the healthy worker effect, Goldsmith generated apparent excesses that were methodological artifacts rather than genuine signals.

Elwood also noted that the Moscow data had been propagated incorrectly through the scientific literature. The ICNIRP review cited results "attributed to 'Lilienfeld cited by Goldsmith'" with mismatched expected values; other papers reported only the Moscow SMRs without their comparison-group context, creating the impression of a finding where the primary comparison showed no elevation.

Elwood acknowledged the study's genuine limitations: a short window between the 1975 peak-exposure period and the 1978 closeout; low statistical power for rare cancers; and some remaining uncertainty about whether comparison embassies received any residual signals. But he concluded these limitations did not alter the fundamental null result when the correct comparison was made.

The Australian Radiation Protection and Nuclear Safety Agency (ARPANSA) reviewed and endorsed Elwood's analysis, noting it "supports the findings of the original report by Lilienfeld et al." and that "the exposure experienced by staff in the US embassy in Moscow is low compared to the current ARPANSA RF exposure standard" ([ARPANSA](https://www.arpansa.gov.au/review-microwave-weapons-cold-war-moscow-embassy-study)).

## 7. Safety Standard Implications: Non-Thermal Effects

The Moscow dataset has been central to a broader argument about whether current RF safety standards—which are set to prevent thermal tissue damage—adequately protect against putative non-thermal biological effects.

**The thermal-effects position (Lilienfeld, Elwood):** Exposure levels in Moscow (maximum 15 μW/cm², typical 1–10 μW/cm²) were four to five orders of magnitude below the ICNIRP public-exposure reference level of 10,000 μW/cm² for 2–4 GHz radiation ([ICNIRP 2020 guidelines](https://www.icnirp.org/en/activities/news/news-article/rf-guidelines-2020-published.html)). They were far below any established threshold for tissue heating. If the data show no health signal at these levels—and the properly analyzed Lilienfeld data do not—this is strong (if not definitive) epidemiological evidence against non-thermal carcinogenic effects at relevant public exposure levels.

**The non-thermal position (Goldsmith):** Goldsmith explicitly framed the Moscow data as evidence that sub-thermal RF exposures cause cancer at levels "qualitatively similar to those [of] ionizing radiation" ([Goldsmith 1995, Int J Occup Environ Health](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9990158/)). His 1997 EHP paper concluded that "RF exposures are potentially carcinogenic" and recommended "prudent avoidance." He positioned the ICNIRP as having systematically dismissed epidemiological evidence in favor of thermal biophysics ([PMC1469943](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1469943/)).

The debate remains technically unresolved: the Lilienfeld dataset, while the largest direct human RF exposure cohort of the pre-cellular era, has the critical limitation that the high-intensity exposure period lasted only eight months (June 1975–February 1976), most of the study covers very low exposures, and no long-term follow-up was ever conducted. The dataset cannot rule out subtle non-thermal effects with high confidence, nor does it demonstrate them. What it does not do is provide positive evidence for non-thermal carcinogenesis—a point Goldsmith's reanalyses obscured by methodological conflation.

## 8. ICNIRP, FCC, and Mays Swicord on the Moscow Data

**ICNIRP** cited the Moscow study in its epidemiological review, but the citation was derived from Goldsmith's secondary interpretation rather than the Lilienfeld primary source. Elwood noted with concern that the ICNIRP review's expected values did not match those in Goldsmith's table and that the observed counts presented were for Moscow employees only—omitting the comparison group context that was the entire inferential engine of the study ([Elwood 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3509929/)). ICNIRP's 1998 and 2020 guidelines maintained that only established thermal effects warranted standard-setting, with Moscow data treated as consistent with that framework.

**The FCC** incorporated ANSI/IEEE recommendations when establishing its 1996 RF exposure standards, which were grounded in thermal thresholds (behavioral disruption in monkeys at ~4 W/kg SAR). The Moscow data—operating far below thermal thresholds—were not a primary input; the FCC's occupational and public limits are set roughly 1,000,000 times higher (in power density terms) than the maximum Moscow exposure levels ([AFSA, 2004](https://afsa.org/regarding-moscow-signal)).

**Mays L. Swicord** served as Chief of the Electromagnetic Branch, Division of Electronic Products at the FDA, and was listed as a reviewer on the NIOSH criteria document for RF/microwave radiation. Swicord was part of the U.S. federal government scientific establishment that generally held the thermal-effects position. The 1981 National Telecommunications and Information Administration report—which assessed Moscow embassy microwave environment and personnel exposures retrospectively—concluded that "no deleterious biological effects to personnel would be anticipated from the microwave exposures as described," aligning with Lilienfeld's findings ([OSTI Technical Report PB-83-155804, 1981](https://www.osti.gov/biblio/5983075)).

## 9. The Political Dimension

Goldsmith's entry into the Moscow data was facilitated by a plaintiff attorney, and his subsequent work was embedded in a broader advocacy framework. His 1995 paper was explicitly an "opinion piece" published in the inaugural issue of *International Journal of Occupational and Environmental Health*, a journal he helped found. His conclusions—RF radiation as a carcinogenic risk comparable to ionizing radiation, ICNIRP as epistemically captured by the thermal-effects paradigm—made him a reference figure for:

- Litigation surrounding cell phone safety (his papers were cited in court proceedings)
- The BioInitiative Working Group and related activist-scientific literature
- Cell tower siting disputes (expert witnesses cited his conclusions as evidence of precautionary requirements)

Goldsmith himself was intellectually honest about his framing: he labeled his 1995 work an opinion piece, acknowledged that he made no "systematic effort to include negative studies," and that his review thus carried "a positive reporting bias" ([Goldsmith 1995](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9990158/)). This disclosure is notable for its candor but also reveals that his reviews were intended to marshal evidence for a predetermined precautionary conclusion rather than to adjudicate the epidemiological question neutrally.

This does not impugn Goldsmith's scientific credentials in general—his career contributions to environmental epidemiology methodology were substantial and recognized. It does, however, mean his Moscow reanalyses should be read as advocacy documents that selected and repackaged data to argue a precautionary regulatory position, not as balanced primary evaluations of the Lilienfeld dataset.

## 10. Honest Methodological Verdict

The methodological question has a reasonably clear answer, though not without residual uncertainty.

**Goldsmith's position is methodologically weak on its central claims.** His two decisive analytical moves—treating the comparison group as equally exposed without evidence, and comparing a health-selected cohort to the general U.S. population—are textbook errors in occupational cohort design. The apparent cancer excesses he reported were largely or entirely artifacts of these choices. The ICNIRP's inadvertent propagation of his figures, and subsequent secondary literature that presented Moscow SMRs without their matched comparison context, contributed to a misleading scientific record.

**Lilienfeld's original design was methodologically sound** for the era and question. The use of a matched comparator population, pre-specified analytical criteria including internal dose-response tests, and comprehensive tracing represent best practice for a retrospective occupational cohort. The study's genuine weaknesses—short high-intensity exposure window, no formal multiple-testing correction, incomplete follow-up—are acknowledged in the report itself and do not reverse its null finding. They do counsel against treating the null result as definitively ruling out long-term, low-level RF effects.

**Elwood's 2012 review is the methodologically strongest treatment** of the dataset. It is peer-reviewed, presents all key data transparently, critically evaluates both the original report and all alternative interpretations, and reaches measured conclusions. Its verdict—that the original conclusions are supported and that contrary conclusions arise from misinterpretation—has been endorsed by ARPANSA and is consistent with the weight of the international RF health literature.

**The bottom line**: The Moscow embassy data, properly analyzed, provide no positive evidence for RF-caused cancer or serious morbidity at the exposure levels involved. The data also cannot, given their limitations, definitively rule out subtle effects from long-duration, low-intensity RF exposure—a caveat that remains valid and scientifically honest. Goldsmith's reanalyses, though produced by a distinguished epidemiologist, subordinated methodological rigor to an advocacy position and generated apparent findings that do not survive scrutiny of the underlying analytical choices. The scientific community has been slow to correct the secondary literature that uncritically propagated his conclusions.
