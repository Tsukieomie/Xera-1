# Allan H. Frey: Origins, Discoveries, and Disputes

## 1. Biographical Background

Allan H. Frey was born in 1935 and trained as a neuroscientist and biophysicist. In 1960, at the age of twenty-five, he was working at General Electric's Advanced Electronics Center at Cornell University in Ithaca, New York — a Cold War–era applied research facility focused on electronics and radar systems ([Type Investigations / GQ, 2010](https://www.typeinvestigations.org/investigation/2010/01/26/cell-phone-hazardous-health/)).

The discovery that would define his career came through an unexpected encounter. A GE technician who measured radar emissions at a nearby facility in Syracuse approached Frey at a conference and confided that he could "hear" the radar beam — a zip, zip, zip sound with no obvious acoustic source. Frey traveled to the facility, stepped into the radar field himself, and confirmed the perception. "Lo," he later recalled, "I could hear it, too." ([New York Times, September 2018](https://www.stopumts.nl/doc.php/Berichten%20Internationaal/11603/cuba_microwave_weapons_are_prime_suspect_in_ills_of_u.s._embassy_workers)).

Frey eventually left Cornell and GE to work as an independent biophysicist and contractor to federal agencies, including the Office of Naval Research (ONR), which funded him for more than fifteen years with what he described as nearly unrestricted support ([Type Investigations, 2010](https://www.typeinvestigations.org/investigation/2010/01/26/cell-phone-hazardous-health/)). He retired from active research around 1985 and subsequently lived near Annapolis, Maryland, where he kept a forty-foot sailboat and took up philosophy of science. As of 2018 — when he was 83 — he was still reachable and commenting publicly on his work ([New York Post, September 2018](https://nypost.com/2018/09/01/microwaves-are-main-suspect-in-attack-on-us-diplomats-in-cuba/)).

---

## 2. The 1961 Paper: First Formal Report of the Microwave Auditory Effect

Frey's initial findings appeared as a technical note titled **"Auditory system response to radio frequency energy"** in *Aerospace Medicine* (Vol. 32, pp. 1140–1142, 1961) ([Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/1120267bcdb970f75a13bbdafa9a68c70683397a)). This was the first American publication formally documenting what would become known as the microwave auditory effect (MAE) — or simply the "Frey effect."

The paper reported that human subjects could perceive sounds — buzzes, clicks, hisses, or knocking — induced by pulsed microwave radiation, without any conventional acoustic waves reaching the outer ear. Crucially, Frey demonstrated the effect at power densities approximately **160 times lower** than the then-standard maximum safe level for continuous exposure ([New York Times / StopUMTS reprint, 2018](https://www.stopumts.nl/doc.php/Berichten%20Internationaal/11603/cuba_microwave_weapons_are_prime_suspect_in_ills_of_u.s._embassy_workers)). Subjects could hear the transmitter from distances of up to hundreds of feet. Frey also noted accompanying symptoms in some subjects — dizziness, headaches, and pins-and-needles sensations — details that would acquire significance decades later ([Wikipedia: Microwave auditory effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect)).

He experimented with nerve-deaf individuals, finding that they too could perceive the induced sounds, which pointed toward the cochlea or some deeper auditory mechanism as the receiving site rather than the external ear. His early hypothesis was that the effect occurred directly in brain tissue through electromagnetic interaction with neural cells — a claim that went further than what the data strictly supported, but which oriented his subsequent research program.

---

## 3. The 1962 Paper: Expanding the Phenomenon

Frey's follow-up, **"Human auditory system response to modulated electromagnetic energy"**, appeared in the *Journal of Applied Physiology* (Vol. 17, No. 4, pp. 689–692, July 1962) — the paper that Wikipedia and most reference sources cite as the primary publication, sometimes conflating it with the 1961 note ([Journal of Applied Physiology, DOI: 10.1152/JAPPL.1962.17.4.689](https://www.physiology.org/doi/10.1152/jappl.1962.17.4.689)).

This paper provided more systematic experimental detail: a repetition rate of 50 Hz with pulse widths between 10–70 microseconds; perceived loudness linked to peak power density rather than average power density; threshold at 1.245 GHz below 80 mW/cm² peak; and the observation that different transmitter parameters could shift the perceived sound character or induce the sensation of "severe buffeting of the head" without vestibular symptoms such as nausea or dizziness ([Wikipedia: Microwave auditory effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect)).

The 1962 paper also pinpointed the temporal lobes — specifically the auditory cortex — as the likely brain region involved in processing the induced signals, based on both his observations and anatomical reasoning ([Gavin Publishers / Cytology & Histology Reports, 2018](https://www.gavinpublishers.com/article/view/the-frey-effect-of-microwave-sonic-weapons)).

Together, the two papers founded what Frey himself called a "new field of study on radiation's neural impacts." The Soviet Academy of Sciences took notice almost immediately and invited him to Moscow, where he was taken to military facilities and asked to discuss the neural impacts of microwaves — an indication of how quickly the Cold War intelligence community recognized the potential significance of his work ([New York Times / StopUMTS reprint, 2018](https://www.stopumts.nl/doc.php/Berichten%20Internationaal/11603/cuba_microwave_weapons_are_prime_suspect_in_ills_of_u.s._embassy_workers)).

## 4. Beyond MAE: Non-Thermal Claims

Frey did not stop at the auditory effect. Over the next decade and a half, he extended his research into a range of biological effects of pulsed low-power microwave radiation, making several claims that placed him well outside the mainstream consensus on RF safety.

**Cardiac effects.** In studies on isolated frog hearts, Frey and his collaborator Seifert (1968) reported that pulse-modulated microwave irradiation at low average energy density could produce tachycardia — accelerated heart rate. Frey subsequently claimed to have stopped frog hearts entirely by selecting the right modulation parameters, a result he described to journalists as among his most alarming findings ([Type Investigations, 2010](https://www.typeinvestigations.org/investigation/2010/01/26/cell-phone-hazardous-health/)). These cardiac claims attracted immediate replication attempts. At least two subsequent studies — Clapman and Cain (1975, *Journal of Microwave Power*) and a 1984 study in *Bioelectromagnetics* — failed to reproduce the cardiac effects, with the 1984 group finding that apparent bradycardia in irradiated frog hearts was an artifact of electrode interactions with the electromagnetic field rather than a genuine biological response ([PubMed: Bioelectromagnetics, 1984](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6732881/)).

**Behavioral effects.** Frey conducted behavioral conditioning experiments in which animals (including surgically deafened cats) were trained to respond to auditory cues and then exposed to microwave pulses. He found that even cochlea-ablated animals responded to pulsed microwave stimulation, suggesting a direct neural pathway bypassing the conventional auditory route ([YouTube: The Frey Effect episode, 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8wear2gf2Fk)). He also published on the use of RF energy to modify avoidance behavior in rats, work he presented at a 1975 symposium in the *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences* ([Frey, Feld & Frey, 1975, NYAS](https://nyaspubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1749-6632.1975.tb36019.x)).

**Blood-brain barrier permeability.** The most consequential and contested of Frey's non-MAE claims concerned the blood-brain barrier (BBB). In 1975, he published a study in the *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences* reporting that pulsed microwave radiation at low average power densities could cause "leakage" in the BBB of white rats ([Environmental Health Trust, 2012](https://ehtrust.org/newsletter-allan-frey-and-the-inconvenient-truth-about-radio-frequency-radiation/)). His method was straightforward: inject sodium fluorescein dye intravenously, expose the animal to pulsed RF energy, then examine brain sections for fluorescence. Pulsed energy at 1.2 GHz produced statistically significant increases in dye fluorescence in brain sections near the diencephalon, at average power densities around 0.2 mW/cm² — well below any known heating threshold. Continuous-wave (CW) radiation also produced effects but weaker ones, suggesting the modulation pattern itself was biologically relevant rather than any bulk thermal elevation ([Frey 1975, *NYAS*, PDF via GBPPR](http://www.gbppr.net/mil/havana/frey1975.pdf)).

## 5. Reception by Mainstream Bioelectromagnetics

**Where he was accepted.** The MAE itself was rapidly accepted. Other researchers confirmed the phenomenon within a few years — by the mid-1970s the thermoelastic (thermoacoustic) theory, developed by James C. Lin and others, had provided a physically coherent mechanism: brief, intense RF pulses cause rapid (~10⁻⁵–10⁻⁶ °C) thermal expansion in brain tissue, generating a pressure wave that propagates to the cochlea via bone conduction ([PMC / Frontiers in Public Health, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/)). This mechanism accounts for the effect without invoking any exotic non-thermal biology, and it is now the consensus explanation. The effect was renamed the "microwave auditory effect" in a deliberate move away from Frey's name — signaling both the field's acknowledgment of his priority and its desire not to be identified with his broader claims.

**Where he was disputed.** The non-thermal claims — especially on the BBB and cardiac effects — met stronger resistance. Scientists funded by the Pentagon claimed to have failed to replicate Frey's BBB findings but refused to share their raw data or methodology, which Frey publicly characterized as "a most unusual action in science." He alleged direct institutional pressure: he was told by ONR that he must conceal the BBB work or his funding contract would be cancelled ([Type Investigations, 2010](https://www.typeinvestigations.org/investigation/2010/01/26/cell-phone-hazardous-health/)). In 2012, reflecting on the episode, he stated: "What happened then was a naked use of power to try to discredit what had been basic scientific work because it did not comport with what some people in the military and industry wanted to hear." ([Environmental Health Trust, 2012](https://ehtrust.org/newsletter-allan-frey-and-the-inconvenient-truth-about-radio-frequency-radiation/)).

Whether or not the suppression narrative is entirely accurate, the outcome is documented: after Frey's 1975 paper, essentially no American laboratory pursued microwave–BBB research for roughly two decades.

## 6. Project Pandora and Government Funding

Project Pandora was a classified U.S. government effort initiated in the 1960s to investigate the "Moscow signal" — pulsed microwave radiation that the Soviet Union had been aiming at the U.S. Embassy in Moscow since at least the 1950s. The project involved multiple agencies including the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA), the Department of Defense, and the Navy, and used facilities at the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory among others ([DoD FOIA declassified: Operational Procedure for Project Pandora, 1966](https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/FOID/Reading%20Room/Other/Operational_Procedure_For_Project_Pandora.pdf); [BBC: Havana Syndrome and the mystery of the microwaves, 2021](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-58396698)).

Frey's relationship to Pandora is indirect but real. His primary government patron was the Office of Naval Research, which funded him for more than fifteen years. He also served as a contractor and consultant to multiple federal agencies over his career ([Type Investigations, 2010](https://www.typeinvestigations.org/investigation/2010/01/26/cell-phone-hazardous-health/)). He is not named as a primary Pandora investigator in declassified documents, but his published work — particularly on MAE thresholds, behavioral effects, and BBB permeability — was directly cited and used by government analysts assessing the biological effects of the Moscow signal. By 2018, Frey confirmed to the *New York Times* that federal investigators had questioned him about the Cuba diplomatic incidents, underscoring his long-standing advisory relationship with the intelligence and defense community ([New York Times, September 2018, via StopUMTS reprint](https://www.stopumts.nl/doc.php/Berichten%20Internationaal/11603/cuba_microwave_weapons_are_prime_suspect_in_ills_of_u.s._embassy_workers)).

His own conclusions from his government-funded work, as expressed in later interviews, were consistent with a non-thermal hazard hypothesis: he believed the frequencies and modulation patterns used in Cold War and modern telecommunications were biologically active at power levels far below thermal thresholds, and that the engineering and military establishment suppressed this finding to avoid liability and strategic complications.

## 7. The Cell Phone Controversy and Marginalization

By the late 1990s and 2000s, cell phones had emerged as a mass-market technology operating at microwave frequencies that Frey had studied for decades — particularly around 900 MHz and 1.9 GHz. In a 2000 essay for an industry-adjacent bioelectromagnetics forum, Frey argued that the field lacked credible biological data to make public health decisions, attributing this to "biological research corrupted by conflicts of interest" driven by the physics and engineering community's historic dominance of RF bioeffects funding ([GoAEGIS, 2000](https://www.goaegis.com/articles/ah_frey_112700_z4p7g1v8.html)).

In a major 2010 profile in *GQ* (republished by Type Investigations), Frey accused the IEEE — of which he had been a member in the 1970s — of setting microwave safety limits that were far too permissive and of functioning as "a charade" serving defense contractors and the telecom industry. He described cell phones as "tiny, low-power microwave ovens, without walls, that we hold against the sides of our heads" and argued that "what we're doing is a grand world experiment without informed consent" ([Type Investigations, 2010](https://www.typeinvestigations.org/investigation/2010/01/26/cell-phone-hazardous-health/)).

These positions aligned him with advocates for stricter RF regulation but distanced him from the majority of bioelectromagnetics researchers, most of whom have concluded that the existing evidence does not demonstrate health harm from mobile phone use at legally permitted power levels. Notably, the large international Interphone study (2010) and subsequent IARC classification of RF EMF as a Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic) agent reflect genuine scientific uncertainty rather than vindication of Frey's strongest claims.

## 8. The Blood-Brain Barrier Controversy: Salford and Counter-Replications

The most scientifically significant test of Frey's non-thermal BBB claim came from a Swedish group at Lund University. Neurosurgeon Leif Salford and colleagues published a series of studies beginning in the 1990s, culminating in a widely cited 2003 paper in *Environmental Health Perspectives*: **"Nerve cell damage in mammalian brain after exposure to microwaves from GSM mobile phones"** (Salford et al., 2003, DOI: [10.1289/ehp.6039](https://ehp.niehs.nih.gov/doi/10.1289/ehp.6039)).

Using GSM 900 MHz signals at SAR levels as low as 0.002 W/kg — well below mobile phone regulatory limits — Salford's group found:
- Significant albumin leakage through the BBB after 2-hour exposures.
- Neuronal damage in the cortex, hippocampus, and basal ganglia (p < 0.002).
- Effects persisting up to 50 days after a single exposure.

Salford stated publicly that the results suggested a serious public health concern, particularly for adolescents whose developing brains may be more vulnerable. The group published multiple follow-up papers through 2009, consistently reporting BBB effects at low SAR levels ([Nittby et al., 2009, DOI: 10.1016/j.pathophys.2009.01.001](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S0928468009000133)).

However, the Salford findings were not uniformly replicated. A large-scale 2017 study in *Scientific Reports* ([Nature, DOI: 10.1038/s41598-017-15690-1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-017-15690-1)) using 1,120 rats across multiple exposure levels (GSM-1800 and UMTS-1950) found:
- No significant BBB leakage or neuronal degeneration following single or repeated exposures up to 2.6 W/kg.
- BBB effects only at 13 W/kg after repeated exposures — a SAR level far exceeding any from mobile phone use — and attributed these to residual thermal elevation (~0.9°C).
- The study explicitly concluded that low-SAR effects observed by the Salford group were likely chance findings.

European and national regulatory reviews (2010, 2015) conducting systematic literature analyses similarly concluded that low-level RF does not alter BBB permeability under controlled conditions. The earlier counter-replication of Frey's 1975 BBB work also suffered from a methodological problem: critics who "failed to replicate" had injected dye intraperitoneally (into the abdomen) rather than intravenously, making it physically impossible for the dye to reach the brain within the study's short timeframe — a fundamental methodological error that Frey flagged but that was not widely acknowledged in mainstream summaries ([Environmental Health Trust, 2012](https://ehtrust.org/newsletter-allan-frey-and-the-inconvenient-truth-about-radio-frequency-radiation/)).

## 9. Havana Syndrome — Did Frey Comment?

Yes, directly and on the record. When the "Havana syndrome" incidents involving U.S. diplomats in Cuba became public starting in 2017–2018, the Frey effect became central to the scientific debate. In September 2018, the *New York Times* published a major investigative piece by William J. Broad, which included an interview with Frey — then 83 years old — at his home outside Washington ([New York Times, September 1, 2018, via StopUMTS reprint](https://www.stopumts.nl/doc.php/Berichten%20Internationaal/11603/cuba_microwave_weapons_are_prime_suspect_in_ills_of_u.s._embassy_workers)). Frey confirmed that federal investigators had questioned him about the incidents and that he considered microwave radiation a plausible cause. He speculated that Cuban factions aligned with Russia might have deployed the technology to undermine U.S.-Cuban diplomatic normalization: "It's a possibility. In dictatorships, you often have factions that think nothing of going against the general policy if it suits their needs."

In September 2018, physicist Beatrice Golomb of UC San Diego published a paper explicitly connecting the diplomatic symptoms to the Frey effect and to RF sickness, arguing that the pattern of symptoms conformed closely to what is known about pulsed microwave exposure ([Reddit/UnresolvedMysteries summary citing Golomb's paper, 2020](https://www.reddit.com/r/UnresolvedMysteries/comments/k7ba4j/havana_syndrome_likely_caused_by_microwave_energy/)).

The December 2020 report by the U.S. National Academies of Sciences concluded that directed pulsed RF energy was the "most plausible" explanation for at least a subset of cases — a significant institutional acknowledgment. However, as of 2023, the official U.S. government position remains that a state-sponsored attack is "very unlikely," and the National Intelligence Council assessed in 2023 that the cases most probably result from multiple explanations including psychogenic factors and pre-existing conditions, with no confirmed link to a directed energy weapon ([BBC, September 2021](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-58396698); [PMC/Frontiers: Garrett, Foster & Ziskin, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/)).

Regarding Frey's current status: as of his 2018 *New York Times* interview he was alive, living outside Washington, and actively engaged with investigators. No obituary or public announcement of his death has been found in available sources, suggesting he was still living as of that point at minimum. He would be approximately 89–90 years old in 2025.

## 10. Balanced Assessment: What Frey Got Right, Where He Overreached, and What Remains Uncertain

### Where Frey was right

The MAE itself is scientifically unimpeachable. The phenomenon has been independently replicated dozens of times, the thermoelastic mechanism is well understood, and it forms the basis of legitimate research into directed-energy effects and neuromodulation. His 1961–62 papers are genuine contributions to neuroscience and are cited in authoritative reviews today ([Foster, Garrett & Ziskin, Frontiers, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/)).

Frey was also right that MAE thresholds are far below conventional RF safety limits — a fact initially contested but now accepted. He was correct that modulation parameters matter enormously for biological response, a finding that has held up and influenced subsequent research on RF bioeffects.

His early observation that the field was being shaped by conflicts of interest — with funding from defense contractors and telecom companies influencing outcome — has been at least partially corroborated by subsequent analyses. A review by Henry Lai of 350 cell phone studies found that industry-funded research showed effects only 25% of the time versus 75% for independently funded studies ([Type Investigations, 2010](https://www.typeinvestigations.org/investigation/2010/01/26/cell-phone-hazardous-health/)).

### Where he overreached

Frey's cardiac effect claims — stopping frog hearts with microwaves — are not supported by the balance of subsequent evidence. The 1984 *Bioelectromagnetics* study demonstrated that apparent cardiac effects in his experimental setup were likely electrode artifacts, not genuine biological responses ([PubMed, Bioelectromagnetics, 1984](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6732881/)).

His rhetorical escalation in later years — characterizing cell phones as instruments of an uncontrolled mass experiment, comparing the IEEE to a "charade" dominated by industry — went beyond what peer-reviewed science supported and alienated colleagues who might have been allies. His dismissal of the entire post-1975 mainstream consensus, rather than engaging with specific contradictory findings, damaged his credibility as a dispassionate scientist.

### Where the jury is still out

The BBB claim remains genuinely unresolved, not vindicated but not definitively refuted. The Salford group's findings at very low SAR levels have not been replicated by other groups using rigorous methods, and major reviews have concluded against a non-thermal BBB effect. But Salford's own later work found no effects at low SAR with repeated exposure, and the methodological criticism of the original counter-replications (wrong injection site) introduces enough uncertainty that a firm conclusion is premature. The IARC's 2011 classification of RF EMF as "possibly carcinogenic" (Group 2B) reflects this uncertainty, as does ongoing NTP/Ramazzini rodent carcinogenicity work.

The broader claim — that there exist frequency- and modulation-specific non-thermal effects in mammalian tissue at power levels far below any measurable heating — is biologically plausible in principle (since electric fields at biologically relevant frequencies can interact with membrane potentials and ion channels), but has not been established to scientific consensus standards. Frey pointed in the right direction; he did not prove the destination.
