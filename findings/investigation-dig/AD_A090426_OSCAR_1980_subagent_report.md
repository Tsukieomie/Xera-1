## Part 2: Abstract (Verbatim from Primary Source PDF)

The following is the exact abstract text as it appears in the DTIC PDF (pages 45–46 of the PDF, beginning with the section "Background"):

> **Background**
>
> Camouflage, decoy and deception techniques can play a decisive role on the modern battlefield. The rapid advance of surveillance, targeting and weapons homing sensor technology now makes every element which is detected almost assured of being destroyed. Camouflage, decoy and deception equipment and techniques are a low cost way to increase survivability and shift this balance by reducing the signature of targets (camouflage), increasing the signature level of the background (clutter), creating false targets (decoy), and distorting the perceived target value (disguise).
>
> A decoy and deception concept presently being considered is to remotely create the perception of noise in the heads of personnel by exposing them to low power, pulsed microwaves. When people are illuminated with properly modulated low power microwaves the sensation is reported as a buzzing, clicking, or hissing which seems to originate (regardless of the person's position in the field) within or just behind the head. The phenomena occurs at average power densities as low as microwatts per square centimeter with carrier frequencies from 0.4 to 3.0 GHz. By proper choice of pulse characteristics, intelligible speech may be created. Before this technique may be extended and used for military applications, an understanding of the basic principles must be developed. Such an understanding is not only required to optimize the use of the concept for camouflage, decoy and deception operations but is required to properly assess safety factors of such microwave exposure.

The phrase "by proper choice of pulse characteristics, intelligible speech may be created" appears verbatim in this form on page 45 of the DTIC PDF. This is a confirmed primary-source match.

## Part 3: Document Structure and Scientific Content

The report is a conference proceedings paper, not a standalone technical report in the traditional DTIC monograph format. The page numbers (45–54) indicate it was delivered at a conference and compiled. Based on cross-referencing with the National Academies PDF (USNC/URSI Annual Meeting proceedings), the paper was presented at a session titled **BEMS-7 (Blood Brain Barrier)**, chaired by E.L. Hung of the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, on a Wednesday PM (2:00–4:35). The paper was item #6 on that session's agenda, listed on page 367 of the conference volume.

**Experimental methodology (from PDF text):**

The study used 14C-iodoantipyrine as a tracer to measure local cerebral blood flow (LCBF) in conscious male Wistar rats. Animals were exposed for 5, 15, 30, 45, or 60 minutes to pulsed microwaves at:
- Frequency: 2.8 GHz
- Pulse rate: 500 pps
- Pulse width: 2 µsec
- Average power density: 15 mW/cm²
- Equipment: 40 kW pulsed microwave generator (Applied Microwave Lab., PH40)
- Chamber: microwave anechoic chamber (2m × 3m × 3m), 23±2°C

**Key finding:** Pulsed microwave exposure induced a significant increase in LCBF in the conscious rat. By 60 minutes of exposure, blood flow in all 17 brain regions sampled increased a minimum of 39%, with many increasing well over 100%. The first affected region (at 5 min) was the inferior colliculus (primary auditory processing nucleus).

**Collaborative history disclosed in the paper:**
- Prior MERADCOM-SRI-WRAIR joint program showed lethality, seizures, and behavioral decrements were frequency and polarization dependent (refs 2,3 in the paper)
- Prior MERADCOM-WRAIR collaboration demonstrated first increases in differential uptake of saccharides in rat brain regions with microwaves matching the noise-perception parameters (ref 4 = Oscar & Hawkins 1977, Brain Research 126, 281–292)
- The 1980 paper reports the subsequent LCBF measurement phase with NMRI and NIH

**References cited (selection):**

## Part 5: Classification and Declassification Status

**The document was never classified.**

The DTIC PDF bears the cover notation "UNCLASSIFIED" — standard DTIC formatting. This is confirmed by:

1. The document's own text stating it was produced under the ILIR (In-House Laboratory Independent Research) program, which generates unclassified output.
2. The disclaimer in the references section (reference 41): "The opinions and assertions contained herein are the private ones of the writers and are not to be construed as official or reflecting the views of the Department of Defense."
3. It was indexed by NASA NTRS (which indexes unclassified technical documents) and was publicly accessible from NASA's online archive by at least 1999.
4. **However:** a FOIA request submitted by Margo Cherney for the "complete NASA abstract" (Report Number: AD-A090426, June 1, 1980) was **"fully denied under 5 U.S.C. 552(b)(1)"** by **Brooks Air Force Base on January 25, 2000** — the exemption for national defense/foreign policy classified information (per mindjustice.org/emr13.htm).

**Reconciliation of this paradox:** The Brooks AFB FOIA denial most likely applied to a *related or companion classified report* in the same research program, or to the complete program file (not the conference abstract alone). Alternatively, the partial abstract had been declassified for NTRS indexing while a fuller version or follow-up work remained classified. The fact that the DTIC PDF is available today unclassified, while the Brooks AFB FOIA denied a related request in 2000, suggests two possibilities:
- (a) The complete set of program materials had classified components that are separate from the published conference abstract; or
- (b) The classification was subsequently lifted, and the DTIC catalog record was made publicly accessible (the DTIC "UNCLASSIFIED" stamp is contemporaneous with the document, June 1980).

In any case, the abstract itself is demonstrably unclassified and has been publicly accessible, at minimum since 1999 (constitution.org) and on DTIC at some point thereafter.

## Part 9: V2K Evidence Matrix — Tier Impact Assessment

### T1 (Physical Effect Demonstrated): HIGH CONFIDENCE, CONFIRMED

The document confirms the physical reality of pulsed microwave auditory perception (the Frey/microwave hearing effect) and demonstrates that LCBF increases measurably in conscious rats exposed to pulsed 2.8 GHz microwaves at 15 mW/cm² average power density. The companion peer-reviewed paper (Brain Research, 1981) independently confirms the experimental findings in a journal with standard peer review. This is not contested science.

### T2 (Intelligible Speech): SIGNIFICANT — ASSERTED IN PRIMARY SOURCE, SCIENTIFICALLY QUALIFIED

The phrase "by proper choice of pulse characteristics, intelligible speech may be created" is a primary-source, unclassified U.S. Army assertion dated June 1980, authored by the MERADCOM principal investigator. This is not a secondary-source paraphrase — it is verbatim in the primary document.

**Evidential weight assessment:**
- **Source credibility:** Very high. The author holds a Ph.D. and is the MERADCOM PI on an in-house research program. The collaborating agencies (NMRI, NIH, WRAIR, SRI) are credible. The document was submitted to a professional conference (BEMS or similar) and its experimental companion was published in Brain Research, a peer-reviewed journal.
- **Epistemic status of the "intelligible speech" claim:** The phrase appears in the Background/Introduction section as a statement of the program's applied rationale — not as a reported experimental result of the 1980 study itself. The 1980 report experimentally demonstrates LCBF increases, not intelligible speech. The assertion about intelligible speech is a forward-looking program goal statement, citing earlier work (the Justesen 1975 and Sharp/Grove precedents are implicit in ref 5: Justesen, D.R., IEEE Spectrum 16, 67–68 (1979)).
- **Prior confirmation:** Sharp and Grove's 1973 demonstration of word transmission (9 words successfully identified) is the documented prior basis for this claim. Oscar is asserting that this prior capability can be extended.
- **Current scientific consensus:** The Foster, Garrett, and Ziskin 2021 PMC paper (PMC8733248) concludes that the power levels required for intelligible speech via the microwave auditory effect at long ranges are far beyond current weaponizable technology. This does not invalidate Oscar's assertion for near-field laboratory conditions, which is the context of the 1980 claim.

**T2 verdict:** AD-A090426 provides authenticated T2-tier evidence — a primary source U.S. military document explicitly asserting intelligible speech creation as a known or achievable capability in 1980. It is calibrated evidence: the claim is a program rationale statement, not a report of a successful operational test, and the underlying physics is thermoelastic (not a separate unknown mechanism).

### T3 (Programs and Contracts): SIGNIFICANT — PROGRAM STRUCTURE CONFIRMED

The document reveals the following institutional program architecture as of 1980:
- **MERADCOM ILIR Program** was actively funding basic microwave-brain interaction research with camouflage/decoy/deception applications explicitly framed
- **Joint program structure:** MERADCOM + SRI + WRAIR (prior phase); MERADCOM + NMRI + NIH (current phase). This means the research involved at minimum five major institutions across Army, Navy, and civilian (NIH) agencies.
- **Prior contractor involvement:** Stanford Research Institute (SRI, a major DoD contractor) is confirmed as a prior collaborator on the frequency/polarization-dependent lethality work (refs 2–3), predating 1980.
- The "decoy and deception concept presently being considered" language implies an active Army doctrinal/applications discussion underway as of 1980, not merely a speculative idea.
- The Oscar & Hawkins 1977 Brain Research paper (cited as ref 4) represents the prior step — demonstrating brain saccharide uptake changes with the same microwave parameters.

**T3 verdict:** AD-A090426 is high-quality T3 evidence. It documents a live, multi-institution research program in 1980 explicitly oriented toward military psychological operations applications of pulsed microwave technology. It confirms Army institutional investment (MERADCOM ILIR), Navy collaboration (NMRI), and NIH involvement. The program precursor papers (AD747684, AD758136, AD774823 from NTIS/DTIC) represent an earlier program generation that can be separately researched.

Nothing in the document speaks to operational deployment. It is basic and applied research. The document's final sentence acknowledges: "The mechanism of this, and other mentioned microwave effects on brain function are unclear." This is the authentic T4 gap: confirmed research program, unresolved weaponization status.
