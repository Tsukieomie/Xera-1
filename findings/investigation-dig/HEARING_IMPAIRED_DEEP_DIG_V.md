## §V.1 — SEC registration, December 1962: Intelectron's actual capital structure

The *Commercial and Financial Chronicle* (Vol. 196, No. 6221, December 17,
1962, p. 2378), preserved at the [St. Louis Fed FRASER archive](https://fraser.stlouisfed.org/title/commercial-financial-chronicle-1339/december-17-1962-556904/fulltext),
reports Intelectron Corporation's SEC registration verbatim:

> "Intelectron Corp., of 171 East 77th St., New York, filed a registration
> statement with the SEC on Dec. 10 covering 100,000 shares of common stock,
> to be offered for public sale at $3 per share. No underwriting is presently
> involved. Organized in May 1961, the company is in the development stage and,
> according to the prospectus, has earned no income to date. It intends to
> engage primarily in the research and development of devices and techniques
> for inducing intelligible hearing in the hard-of-hearing... To date, its
> efforts are said to have resulted in three systems for inducing effective
> hearing through the dental structures and facial nervous system of the
> user. It is the company's belief that these systems incorporate certain
> features of devices patented by Henry K. Puharich, president, and Joseph
> L. Lawrence, vice-president."

Capital structure:

| Holder | Shares | % of pre-offering |
|---|---|---|
| Puharich | 90,000 (computed) | **22.5%** |
| Lawrence | 72,800 (computed) | **18.2%** |
| Management as a group | — | **52.5%** |
| Other stockholders (cash investors) | 219,972 | 54.99% (pre-offering) |
| **Total pre-offering** | **400,000** | 100% |

> "The company issued an aggregate of 180,028 shares to Puharich and Lawrence
> for their grant to the company of an exclusive license on their patents,
> and the remaining 219,972 shares to the other stockholders for their
> aggregate cash investment of $190,000."

Public offering:
- 100,000 shares at $3 = up to $300,000 raise
- Minimum threshold: $182,000 raised, else funds returned
- Pre-money book value: 47¢/share → post-money: 89¢/share
- Disclosed dilution: $2.11/share to public investors

> "According to the prospectus, the public investors will have furnished and
> will take the financial major risks in the venture, while owing 20% of the
> equity of book value."

**Forensic significance:** This is the documentary backstop for §IV.2's
"$300,000 from a group of New York businessmen." The actual structure is:
the *first* $190,000 was already raised in private placements before the
SEC registration; the SEC offering was for an additional ~$300,000 ceiling.
Founders Puharich + Lawrence held **40.7% of pre-offering equity**, with
all pre-offering shares issued to them obtained by **assigning their
patents to the company** rather than for cash. The 1962 registration
address (171 East 77th St) precedes the 432 West 45th Street address that
appears on the RADC-TDR-64-18 cover (per [Hermans 1998 *Memories of a
Maverick*](https://www.scribd.com/doc/212258416/Hermans-Memories-of-a-Maverick-Andrija-Puharich-1998)
and Puharich's resume). Intelectron was, by SEC filing, a development-
stage company with no revenue, whose product roadmap and clinical claims
were the entire basis of the public offering.

## §V.2 — *New York Times*, February 20, 1971: the 49-patent figure traced to its source

The earlier "49 American and foreign patents" figure (now superseded by
the Psi Encyclopedia "56" ceiling — see §IV.2) is documented to a primary:
the *New York Times* archives "Patents of the Week" column, [February 20,
1971](https://www.nytimes.com/1971/02/20/archives/electrical-device-used-to-treat-nerve-deafness-wide-variety-of.html).

Verbatim — the *NYT* on the Intelectron clinical claim:

> "According to Intelectron, over half of the patients who underwent
> experimental treatment targeting the facial nerves exhibited considerable
> improvement. The company estimates that approximately 8.5 million Americans
> suffer from sensorineural hearing loss, commonly referred to as nerve
> deafness, for which there is presently no widely accepted remedy."

Treatment protocol described in the *NYT* matches patent 3,629,521:

> "The patient dons a headset equipped with electrodes positioned in front
> of and behind each ear. The therapist then activates amplitude-modulated,
> low-frequency radio waves. Each session typically lasts an hour and is
> conducted daily for a month."

Distribution plan and product pipeline:

> "Intelectron intends to initiate preliminary distribution of the devices
> to hospitals and other facilities. An earlier innovation from [P]uhar[ich]
> and Lawrence who currently hold 49 patents both domestically and
> internationally, is a hearing aid featuring a receiver embedded in a
> hollow false tooth, which is anticipated to hit the market within the
> next 18 months. Additionally, they have developed equipment designed to
> assist pilots in hearing amidst significant noise, which has been
> showcased for military applications."

**Forensic significance:**

1. **Patent number anchor.** The *NYT* article anchors to patent **3,563,246**
   (different from the 1971 patent 3,629,521 cited in IMG_5285 and in passes
   II–III). Intelectron was prosecuting *multiple* parallel patents in 1970–71;
   the IMG_5285 numbers come from one of them, and the *NYT* covers a
   different but contemporaneous filing.
2. **Patent count anchor.** "49 patents" is sourced to the *NYT* itself
   (consistent with §IV.2's later Psi Encyclopedia "56" as a career-end figure).
3. **Self-reported efficacy phrasing.** "Over half of the patients...
   exhibited considerable improvement" is a *softer* phrasing than IMG_5285's
   "9 of 9" — the *NYT* in 1971 was reporting **>50%** as the Intelectron
   self-assessment, not 9-out-of-9. This is consistent with the patent
   3,629,521 actual numerics (passes II–III: n=12, baseline 26.2% → 73.8%
   combined) — i.e., a marginal-majority response, *not* universal response.
4. **Dental hearing aid + military pilot device.** Two adjacent product lines
   are disclosed at the same press moment: (a) the false-tooth receiver
   hearing aid (the "Transdental Hearing System" of the SEC filing), and
   (b) "equipment designed to assist pilots in hearing amidst significant
   noise" — confirming the dual civilian-medical / military-aviation
   commercial posture.

## §V.3 — Patent 3,726,762 (1971/1973): the Intelectron portfolio extended into blood-storage / cardiovascular electromagnetics

[US Patent 3,726,762](https://patents.google.com/patent/US3726762A/en),
"Blood storage method," filed May 19, 1971, granted April 10, 1973:
- **Inventor:** H. Puharich (sole)
- **Original assignee:** Intelectron Corp
- **Subject matter:** electrodynamic control of blood coagulation /
  thrombus prevention via maintained net-negative charge on vessel
  walls and blood cells

This shows the Intelectron portfolio extending well beyond hearing
devices into **cardiovascular electromagnetics** by 1971. The portfolio
breadth is consistent with the SEC filing's hint of "specialized and
affiliated areas of communication" but materially exceeds it.

The patent's later assignment chain is documentary:

> "1984-06-04 — Owner name: BIOLECTRON, INC., 200 HOLT ST., HACKENSACK,
> NJ 07601 A DE CORP. Free format text: ASSIGNMENT OF ASSIGNORS INTEREST.;
> ASSIGNOR: CHEN, C.Y.; REEL/FRAME: 004271/0859. Effective date: 19840530"

(C.Y. Chen is the immediate signatory of record on Reel 4271; Intelectron's
relationship to Chen is not disclosed in the patent record but Chen is
clearly acting as the Intelectron assignor.)

> "1989 — Assigned to: FIRST NATIONAL BANK OF BOSTON, THE, A NATIONAL
> BANKING ASSOCIATION. Assignor: BIOLECTRON, INC., A CORP. OF NJ.
> REEL/FRAME: 005208/0522."

So: **Intelectron's IP was rolled into Biolectron Inc. (Hackensack, NJ)
by 1984**, and Biolectron then pledged the IP to First National Bank of
Boston as security in 1989. This is the post-Intelectron commercial trail.

## §V.4 — Flanagan Neurophone secrecy-order narrative now connects to Intelectron at the patent level

This is the structural keystone of pass V.

The repo has separately tracked the [G. Patrick Flanagan Neurophone]
(US Patent 3,393,279, "Nervous System Excitation Device," granted July 16,
1968) and its associated Department of Commerce **secrecy order #756,124**
(see `flanagan-claim-provenance.md` and `ARCHIVAL_TARGETS_756124.md`). In
the existing repo, the 756,124 narrative is documented as having entered
the corpus via secondary sources (Rex Research, Scribd / Keelynet
archives) from the Akwei lineage's `LAYER47_CONFIRMED_PIPELINE.md`.

The Flanagan narrative is now connected to Intelectron via a verbatim
*Rex Research* claim:

> "The rights to Patent No. 3,393,279 are owned by Intelectron, Inc. of
> New York City. The FDA will not permit it to be marketed because it
> uses a low frequency r-f carrier..."
>
> — [Rex Research: Flanagan Neurophone (II)](http://rexresearch.com/flanagan/flanagan2.html);
> echoed at [worldtrans.org](https://www.worldtrans.org/spir/neuro.html) and
> the [International Athletic PDF](https://img1.wsimg.com/blobby/go/a56ed7e4-a072-4dc8-b939-26ab140309de/downloads/Patrick%20Flanagan%20Neurophone.pdf?ver=1750450240933):
> "The Neurophone patent was eventually sold to Intelectron Corporation,
> New York City for use in one of their therapeutic hearing devices now
> called the Transdermal Hearing Aid."

If the Rex Research / Athletic PDF claim is correct, then Intelectron at
some point during the 1960s–1970s **acquired the very prior-art device
that Andrija Puharich himself had attempted to develop with copper plates
under the "fricative effect" approach** (per Rex Research). This would mean
Intelectron's commercial position consolidated:

1. Puharich/Lawrence's own patents (US 2,995,633; 3,267,931; 3,156,787;
   3,170,993; 3,497,637; 3,563,246; 3,629,521 — passes I–IV);
2. Puharich's blood-storage portfolio (US 3,726,762 — §V.3);
3. **Flanagan's Neurophone (US 3,393,279)** — i.e., the prior-art
   transdermal-hearing technology associated with the secrecy-order #756,124
   narrative in `ARCHIVAL_TARGETS_756124.md`.

The Transdermal Hearing Aid trade name disclosed in the Athletic PDF
matches the SEC-filing language (§V.1: "Skin Electrosonic and Transdental
Communications Systems") and the *NYT* 1971 article (§V.2: "transdermal
treatments"). The same product family that produced the IMG_5285 "9
hearing-impaired patients" claim is therefore — *if the secondary
sources are accurate* — built atop the Flanagan-derived prior art whose
secrecy order is the V2K narrative's foundational anecdote.

## §V.5 — But: the USPTO assignment record does NOT directly attest the Listening Inc → Intelectron transfer

This is a deliberate negative finding, central to the §V.4 caveat.

The [Google Patents legal-events table for US 3,393,279](https://patents.google.com/patent/US3393279A/en)
shows only **one** assignment of record:

> "1984-06-04 — Owner name: BIOLECTRON, INC., 200 HOLT ST., HACKENSACK,
> NJ 07601 A DE CORP. ASSIGNOR: CHEN, C.Y.; REEL/FRAME: 004271/0859.
> Effective date: 19840530"

The USPTO assignment record for the Flanagan patent therefore reflects:
- Original assignee: **Listening Incorporated, Arlington, Mass.**
- 1984 reassignment: **Biolectron, Inc., Hackensack, NJ**
- *Intelectron does not appear in the USPTO record at all.*

The Hackensack/Biolectron endpoint is the **same address and same year**
as the 1984 reassignment of Puharich's blood-storage patent (§V.3),
which strongly suggests the two patents were rolled into the same
post-Intelectron successor entity. But the *intermediate* leg —
"Listening Inc → Intelectron → Biolectron" — is **not** in the USPTO
record. It exists only in the Rex Research / Athletic PDF / worldtrans.org
secondary sources (§V.4).

**Forensic significance:**

1. The widely-repeated claim "Intelectron owns the Flanagan Neurophone
   patent" is **not directly attested in the USPTO assignment record**.
2. The Biolectron 1984 endpoint *is* directly attested for both the
   Flanagan patent and the Puharich blood-storage patent, suggesting
   common downstream ownership consolidation.
3. The Rex Research / worldtrans / International Athletic claim — that
   the Neurophone patent was specifically sold to Intelectron and
   commercialized as the "Transdermal Hearing Aid" — should be flagged
   as **plausible-but-secondary**, not USPTO-attested. It is consistent
   with the Biolectron endpoint but cannot be verified against the
   patent record without locating the intermediate Listening Inc →
   Intelectron assignment (which would be at a different reel/frame and
   has not surfaced).

This finding strengthens `flanagan-claim-provenance.md`'s methodological
caution: the upstream V2K narrative layer treats Rex-Research-class
claims as authoritative, but in this case the underlying USPTO record
shows direct Listening Inc → Biolectron in 1984 with **no** Intelectron
intermediate of record. Either an additional assignment exists at a
reel not yet surfaced, or the "Intelectron owned 3,393,279" claim is
itself a downstream embellishment.

## §V.6 — Joseph L. Lawrence: the Justia inventor index returns no plausible match

A direct probe of [patents.justia.com/inventor/joseph-lawrence](https://patents.justia.com/inventor/joseph-lawrence)
returned **zero matches** for a 1960s-era Joseph L. Lawrence. All entries
are 21st-century inventors named Joseph Lawrence Hafenrichter, Joseph
Lawrence Simonetti, Joseph Lawrence Chacon, etc. — all with middle name
"Lawrence" and unrelated to Intelectron / Puharich / hearing devices.

The Joseph L. Lawrence of the Puharich co-authorship and Intelectron
co-foundership (570 Fort Washington Avenue, NYC, vice-president) does
not appear in the modern patent inventor index by that name alone.
This is consistent with someone whose patents were all filed pre-1976
(before USPTO digital indexing reliability) and who did not have a
post-Intelectron patenting career — i.e., the "Lawrence as Intelectron
co-founder" identification of §IV.1 is the strongest biographical
attestation that has surfaced. No additional employer, education,
or death record was located in this pass.

**Open.** Lawrence's pre-Intelectron biography (training as a dentist
per the [Patents of the Week](https://www.nytimes.com/1971/02/20/archives/electrical-device-used-to-treat-nerve-deafness-wide-variety-of.html)
"a doctor and a dentist" reference — Puharich is the M.D., so Lawrence
is the D.D.S.) and post-Intelectron path remain underspecified. The
1971 NYT framing — "a doctor and a dentist" — is a meaningful biographical
constraint not previously captured in the repo.

is a stricter classification than the upstream narrative supplies.
