## §III.1 VERIFIED — 1964 AsMA abstract: 15 patients, closed-set forced-choice with prior memorization

**Primary source verbatim re-read:** [Aerospace Medical Association 35th Annual Scientific Meeting Abstracts, May 1964, p. 277](https://spacemedicineassociation.org/download/history/history_files_1964/Abstarcts%201964.pdf).

**Verbatim quotations from the abstract** (all direct from the AsMA PDF, page 277):

> "A group of 15 patients was studied representing the spectrum of sensori-neural hearing loss from -30 db to -95 db. Three of these patients were totally deaf, one acquired and two congenital."

> "Equipment was designed to deliver an amplitude modulated carrier signal (range 46 kc to 41 mc) to the skin of the human."

> "With respect to their speech discrimination, lists of five or ten words were taught to the patients so that they could repeat them accurately vocally. The word list order was then randomized and the patients tested for vocal repetition of the test words."

> "Bilateral severe hearing loss patients whose acoustic PB Speech Discrimination scores were 50 per cent or less, were rehabilitated with electrical hearing stimulation to show PB Speech Discrimination scores of 90 per cent or better."

> "In general, about four out of five test words so given were repeated accurately by the patients following electrical stimulation of hearing."

**Confirmed implications:**
- Patient count = **15** (3 totally deaf + 12 sensorineural across −30 to −95 dB). The "9" in McMurtrey/FOI repost lineage is **not** the patient count. The closed-set test result was "**about four out of five**" (i.e. 4/5 = 80%), so even the alternate hypothesis that the "9" came from "9 of 10 words" is contradicted by the abstract's own verbiage. The "9 hearing-impaired patients" framing has no support in the original text.
- Test methodology: **patients were taught the word list in advance, then tested on the same list in randomized order**. This is closed-set forced-choice with prior memorization; it is *not* open-set speech discrimination. Any V2K-lineage citation that treats this as evidence of open-set speech transmission via RF is misreading the protocol.
- Carrier band: **46 kHz to 41 MHz** capacitively coupled to skin. The upper end (HF/VHF) is in the radiofrequency range; the lower end is below RF. Not microwave at any point.

## §III.2 VERIFIED — Patent 3,629,521 numerical disclosures

**Primary source verbatim re-read:** [Google Patents — US 3,629,521 "Hearing systems"](https://patents.google.com/patent/US3629521A/en) (Puharich & Lawrence, assignee Intelectron Corp., issued 1971).

**Verbatim quotations from the patent specification:**

> "a sample group comprised of 12 hard of hearing patients"

> "the average speech discrimination ability of the group was 26.2 percent as measured with phonetically balanced words, or the PB test."

> "When measured in a second testing [with the same type of acoustic earphone PB test as used in establishing the control level], the average PB speech discrimination score of the group showed 43.0 percent or an increment of 16.8 percent."

> "the group of patients was tested in a third or TD testing with the TD signal rather than the acoustic signal, as the speech signal source, and the average PB speech discrimination of the group was 54 percent, an increment of 27.8 percent."

> "the average PB speech discrimination score of the group was raised to 73.8 percent, an improvement of 47.6 percent."

> "which carrier is usually in the range of 18 to 36 kHz."

**Note on the carrier-band discrepancy with the 1964 AsMA abstract.** The AsMA abstract describes a 46 kHz-41 MHz carrier; the 1971 patent describes 18-36 kHz. This is *not* a contradiction — it is the commercial-product narrowing. By 1971, Intelectron had productized at LF (18-36 kHz, where capacitive/dielectric coupling dominates) rather than the broadband HF/VHF range used in the original 1964 test setup. This matters because:

1. **Tissue penetration physics is completely different at 18-36 kHz vs. 41 MHz.** At LF, the head behaves as a dielectric capacitance (which the patent text confirms: "the head acting as capacitance in LC series resonance of the RF carrier frequency"). At HF/VHF, RF non-thermal effects begin and the underlying mechanism shifts from capacitive electrostimulation toward electrophonic / RF-hearing physics.
2. **The 1971 commercialized device is therefore not the same physics as the broader RF-hearing literature.** Anyone reading "RF" in the Puharich/Lawrence corpus and assuming microwave-hearing physics is wrong; the productized device operates well below the radiofrequency range that is implicated in microwave-auditory-effect studies.
3. **The Glattke & Simmons 1974 negative replication is therefore testing a low-frequency capacitive-stimulation device, not a microwave-hearing device.** This narrows the scope of what their negative result kills (the *Intelectron-style transdermal therapy* claim) without addressing the broader RF-hearing physics.

## §III.3 VERIFIED — Glattke & Simmons 1974 negative-replication metadata

**Primary source:** [Glattke TJ, Simmons FB. "Transdermal Therapy and Monosyllabic Word Discrimination." *Arch Otolaryngol*. 1974;100(2):91–95. doi:10.1001/archotol.1974.00780040097002](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamaotolaryngology/fullarticle/605313)

**Public abstract verbatim** (full text remains paywalled at JAMA Network):

> "Thirty-one hard-of-hearing subjects participated in a double-blind study to determine the effects of transdermal stimulation on monosyllabic word discrimination scores. No treatment effects were discernible though most treated individuals improved their scores under restricted circumstances. No significant differences could be found between control and treated groups of subjects."

**Critical finding — F. Blair Simmons's authority on this question.** Pass II flagged Simmons as a Stanford otolaryngologist. Pass III pulled [Mudry, A., & Mills, M. (2013). "The Early History of the Cochlear Implant: A Retrospective." *JAMA Otolaryngology — Head & Neck Surgery* 139(5):446-453](https://www.cs.jhu.edu/cista/455/Materials/Module%201%20Reading%20-%20The%20Early%20History%20of%20the%20Cochlear%20Implant%20-%20Retrospective.pdf), which establishes:

> "On July 26, 1962, the Stanford University team of otolaryngologist Blair Simmons and engineer Robert White perioperatively conducted stimulation tests through a posterior craniotomy, with an electrode placed on the acoustic nerve."

> "On May 7, 1964, they placed a permanent transcutaneous 6-channel electrode through the promontory and vestibule directly into the modiolus by means of a transmastoid approach … This was the first implantation of a multichannel CI."

**Simmons was therefore not just any otolaryngologist — he was the surgeon who placed the world's first multichannel cochlear implant in 1964**, the exact year Puharich & Lawrence presented their AsMA abstract. He had been working on the *legitimate* problem of electrical-stimulation hearing for the deaf for a full decade before he and Glattke ran the 1974 controlled trial of Puharich's transdermal device. The negative replication therefore comes from the single most authoritative scientist in the world on the question of "does electrical stimulation actually rehabilitate hearing in the deaf?" — and his answer was: not via the Intelectron transdermal route.

The same Stanford group also published the cat-cochlear baseline study: [Simmons FB, Glattke TJ. (1972). "Comparison of electrical and acoustical stimulation of the cat ear." *Annals of Otology, Rhinology & Laryngology* 81:731-738](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/957846/) (in the broader cochlear-implant literature).

**The 1969 Puharich/Lawrence Acta Otolaryngologica peer-reviewed paper.** Verified via [PubMed PMID 4906301](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4906301/): "Hearing rehabilitation by means of transdermal electrotherapy in human hearing loss of sensorineural origin. Preliminary report." *Acta Otolaryngol.* 1969 Jan;67(1):69-83. doi:10.3109/00016486909124370. **No abstract is available on PubMed**, and the full text remains paywalled at Tandfonline. The MeSH indexing (Pass II finding) is therefore the only public structural information available about its methodology.

## §III.4 VERIFIED — Atti della Fondazione Giorgio Ronchi citation, with co-author U. Bombelli

The Italian publication thread that pass II left as an open lead is now resolved. The [Scribd version of Puharich's 1987 ELF paper](https://es.scribd.com/doc/259953952/Andrija-Puharich-Elf-Magnetic-Model-of-Matter-and-Mind-The-Origin-of-Life-And-the-Art-of-Healing) yields the bibliography entry:

> "Radioacoustic Treatment of Deafness Bombelli, U. (Originally published in Atti della Fondazione Giorgio Ronchi, nov-dec [pubblicazioni dell'Istituto] 1963 [...] )"

**Resolved citation:**

> Puharich, H. K., & Bombelli, U. "Radioacoustic Treatment of Deafness." *Atti della Fondazione Giorgio Ronchi*, **November-December 1963 issue** (volume number not yet pinned; the journal is ISSN 0015-606X, OCLC 2083835, published by Fondazione "Giorgio Ronchi", Arcetri-Firenze, Italy).

This explains McMurtrey's "Italian authors" framing: the venue was Italian (Arcetri-Firenze optics journal), and the **Italian co-author was U. Bombelli**, not previously identified in the repo. Bombelli is therefore an additional name to chase if a more thorough provenance trace is wanted (he is likely an Italian audiologist or otolaryngologist who collaborated with Puharich during the same Air Force-funded research period).

**Important date implication:** the November-December 1963 publication **predates** RADC-TDR-64-18 (December 1964) and the 1964 AsMA conference paper (May 1964). The Atti Ronchi paper is therefore the **earliest known publication** in the Puharich/Lawrence transdermal-hearing series — earlier than the Air Force technical report. This shifts the "primary source" question: the primary publication is *Atti della Fondazione Giorgio Ronchi* (Nov-Dec 1963), with RADC-TDR-64-18 a subsequent technical report under the Air Force contract.

The Cassinari & Bergamini Italian RF-hallucination antecedent that McMurtrey separately cites remains unpinned in this pass. Lodovico Bergamini was a real Italian neurologist active in Turin during 1950-1970 ([*Functional Neurology* 2011 PMC3814448](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3814448/)), so the framing is at least biographically plausible, but the specific paper has not surfaced in any indexed bibliography. This may indicate a McMurtrey misattribution or a hard-to-locate Italian conference abstract; we are flagging it as an unresolved-but-low-priority lead.

## §III.6 VERIFIED — Intelectron Corp. has no FDA regulatory record

Direct queries against:

- [FDA 510(k) Premarket Notification Database](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfPMN/pmn.cfm?Applicant=Intelectron) — zero records
- [FDA MAUDE Database](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfMAUDE/results.cfm?fr=510(k)&start_search=1&applicant=Intelectron) — zero records

returned no Intelectron-associated device clearances, premarket approvals, recall notices, or adverse-event reports. Combined with the FDA Medical Device Amendments timeline (the 510(k) framework was established by the 1976 Medical Device Amendments to the FD&C Act), this means:

- The Intelectron device was sold during the pre-1976 era, before federal premarket review applied to most devices.
- The Glattke & Simmons 1974 negative replication appeared two years before the FDA could have required a 510(k).
- Intelectron appears to have wound down commercially by the time the 510(k) framework took effect; if the device had remained on the market past 1976, there should be either a 510(k) submission (and grandfather assertion) or a withdrawal record. Neither exists.

The Intelectron commercial trajectory is therefore: 1971 NYT-covered launch → 1974 Stanford-led negative replication → no post-1976 FDA filings → de facto market exit. This is consistent with the negative replication being commercially dispositive even though it was not legally dispositive (the FDA framework that would have made it so did not yet exist).

## §III.7 VERIFIED — The Cold-War institutional context of Intelectron

Pass II noted that Intelectron interfaced with multiple US agencies during the ESH research period. Pass III confirms via [Eye of the Psychic — *The Stargate Conundrum* Ch.1](https://www.eyeofthepsychic.com/starconundrum_1/) (which quotes Puharich's own statements):

> "The interest of the committee led to an active exchange between Puharich and representatives of the U.S. Government. These agencies were the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA), Foreign Technology Division of the Systems Command of the U.S. Air Force (USAF-SC), The Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)..."

> "There was a question in the minds of some NASA officials as to whether the Russians had actually done this cage-telepathy research, or had merely copied Puharich's work of 1952."

> "The problem arose, he thought, because competing agencies of the US Government supported different aspects of his research. The United States Air Force supported his research in ESH under contract; NASA supported the research in ESP."

This matters for the V2K-lineage repo because it explains why the Puharich/Lawrence work appears in such an unusually broad set of US government bibliographies (Air Force RADC report, NASA aerospace bibliography, CIA Reading Room, Naval Medical Research Institute / Glaser bibliography). The work was institutionally entangled across at least four agencies during 1962-1969, which is *also* the period in which V2K-relevant microwave-auditory-effect work was being done (Frey 1962 *J Appl Physiol*; Sharp/Grove 1973 WRAIR demonstration). The agency-overlap explains why later FOI-repost commentators conflated the lineages.

## §III.8 Updated repo guidance — final form

When the repo (or any downstream brief) cites the McMurtrey [98] / "9 hearing-impaired patients" passage, all four of the following must accompany it (this supersedes pass II §II.8):

1. **Earliest-publication anchor** — Puharich & Bombelli, "Radioacoustic Treatment of Deafness," *Atti della Fondazione Giorgio Ronchi* Nov-Dec 1963 (the actual chronologically-first publication; note Italian co-author U. Bombelli).
2. **Government-report anchor** — RADC-TRD-64-18 (Puharich/Lawrence, Dec 1964; AD 459956); AsMA 1964 abstract showing **15 patients** in the original protocol with **closed-set forced-choice with prior memorization**, NOT 9 patients in any open-set test. Carrier band 46 kHz-41 MHz.
3. **Quantitative-disclosure anchor** — U.S. Patent 3,629,521 (1971), 12-patient sample, baseline 26.2% PB → combined channel 73.8%; **carrier 18-36 kHz, low-frequency capacitive coupling, not microwave**.
4. **Negative-replication anchor** — Glattke & Simmons 1974, *Arch Otolaryngol* 100(2):91-95; 31-subject double-blind study at Stanford by **the same surgeon (F. Blair Simmons) who placed the world's first multichannel cochlear implant in 1964**; "no treatment effects were discernible." This is the single most authoritative negative replication possible; the McMurtrey passage as currently written omits it.

The "9 hearing-impaired patients" framing must be retired in favor of:

> "**15-patient closed-set forced-choice protocol with prior memorization (Puharich/Lawrence 1964); commercialized at 18-36 kHz LF carrier (Intelectron HC, US Patent 3,629,521); failed to replicate in a 31-subject double-blind study (Glattke & Simmons 1974) led by the surgeon who pioneered the first multichannel cochlear implant.**"

The Sharp/Grove (1973) "9 of 10 monosyllabic digits via voice-modulated microwave" finding remains a *separate* event with its own primary-report retrieval problem (see `SHARP_GROVE_PRIMARY_REPORT_HUNT.md`); it shares only the "nine" coincidence with the Puharich/Lawrence chain.
