### 2.2 Verbatim text from the 1975 APA article (the sole Justesen account)

The following is the passage from pp. 395–396 of Justesen 1975, as reproduced in the Dr. Zory Glaser PDF archive of the article (https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf):

> "Sharp and Grove (Note 2) found that appropriate modulation of microwave energy can result in direct 'wireless' and 'receiverless' communication of speech. They recorded by voice on magnetic tape each of the single-syllable words for digits between 1 and 10. The electrical sine-wave analogs of each word were then processed so that each time a sine wave crossed zero reference in the negative direction, a brief pulse of microwave energy was triggered. By radiating themselves with these 'voice-modulated' microwaves, Sharp and Grove were readily able to hear, identify, and distinguish among the 9 words."

Footnote 2 (Note 2) reads:
> "Sharp, J. C., & Grove, M. Personal communication, September 28, 1973."

The 1975 APA article also includes this closing sentence on transmission limits:
> "Communication of more complex words and of sentences was not attempted because the averaged densities of energy required to transmit longer messages would approach the [still] current 10 mW/cm² limit of safe exposure."

**This is the entirety of what Justesen published about the demonstration.** Across all identified separately-authored Justesen works, no additional protocol details appear: no subject count, no blinding protocol, no RF frequency specification, no error rates, no equipment parameters beyond the zero-crossing modulation method described above.

Contract EY-76-C-06-1830 for Division of Solar Energy, Department of Energy. OSTI: https://www.osti.gov/servlets/purl/1069245

**Content relevant to Sharp/Grove:** The PNL-2634 document cites Justesen 1975a as the source for the RF-hearing auditory effect discussion. The OSTI full-text extract (from a `*In a personal communication to D.R. However, **the SPS bioeffects review does not reproduce, expand, or add to the Sharp/Grove voice-communication demonstration.** It is a broader bioeffects literature review for Department of Energy satellite power system planning; RF hearing is discussed only in background context, and no new Sharp/Grove protocol detail is introduced.

**Distribution note:** PNL-2634 has the NASA Ames Research Center listed in its distribution (prior investigation confirmed connection to PNL-2634 distribution network via NASA Ames). ### 2.4 The 1979 NYAM article — detailed assessment

**Full citation:** Justesen, D.R. (1979). Behavioral and psychological effects of microwave radiation. *Bulletin of the New York Academy of Medicine*, Second Series, 55(11):1058–1078. PMID 120208; PMCID PMC1807730.

**Presented at:** Symposium on Health Aspects of Nonionizing Radiation sponsored by the Subcommittee on Public Health Aspects of Energy of the Committee on Public Health of the New York Academy of Medicine, held April 9–10, 1979.

**Content relevant to Sharp/Grove:** The full text of this article (available at zoryglaser.com and PMC) covers convulsions, work stoppage, work decrement, endurance, RF-field perception (Frey effect), aversive behavior, and neurasthenia. The RF-hearing section (pp. 1062–1063) discusses the Frey effect and thermoelastic expansion mechanism at length. **It does not name Sharp and Grove, does not describe the voice-modulation demonstration, and does not cite "Sharp, J.C." or "Grove, M." in any form.** The reference list (References 1–40 as numbered in the PMC version) confirms this: refs 14–20 cover the Frey RF-hearing literature without naming Sharp/Grove. Justesen references his own 1975 work in reference 11 (as "J Microw Power 1975") but does not cross-reference the Sharp/Grove passage from the 1975 *Am. Psychologist* article.

**Assessment:** The 1979 NYAM article represents a deliberate narrowing of scope — Justesen focuses on well-documented laboratory behavioral effects and explicitly sets aside ("imagined effects") claims not yet confirmed by replication. The Sharp/Grove demonstration, which rested on a personal communication rather than a peer-reviewed protocol paper, is absent, consistent with Justesen's conservative framing in that article.

## Section 4: Classification — Is There a Separately-Authored Justesen Source with Additional Protocol Detail on the Speech Demo?

### 4.1 Direct answer: **NO**

No separately-authored Justesen technical report, conference paper, or unpublished manuscript has been located in any publicly accessible database — DTIC, OSTI, NTRS, PubMed, Google Scholar, or bibliography cross-referencing — that contains additional protocol detail about the Sharp/Grove 28-Sep-1973 speech demonstration beyond what appears in the 1975 *American Psychologist* article.

### 4.2 Evidence supporting this classification

1. **DTIC search (negative):** No AD-numbered Justesen TR located. Justesen's funding base (VA, DOE, HHS) was not primarily DoD, making DTIC deposit unlikely for his independent work.

2. **NTRS search (negative):** No NASA-authored Justesen report. PNL-2634 distribution to NASA Ames did not generate an NTRS-indexed co-authored document.

3. **OSTI — single hit (PNL-2634, 1978):** PNL-2634 is confirmed as a DOE-funded secondary literature review. Its RF-hearing section cites Justesen 1975a (the APA article) but adds no new Sharp/Grove detail. PNL-2634 is a bioeffects overview, not a protocol document.

4. **PubMed — 30 results, none on speech/voice transmission:** Of 30 PubMed-indexed Justesen publications, none has "voice," "speech," "communication," or "Sharp" in the title or abstract. The 1979 NYAM article (PMID 120208), the most proximate major review after 1975, does not reference Sharp/Grove at all.

5. **Journal letters / errata (negative):** No follow-up letter, erratum, or commentary in *American Psychologist* or any other journal from Justesen post-1975 that adds Sharp/Grove protocol detail has been located. APA's *American Psychologist* did not publish a Justesen correspondence or correction in the available bibliographic record.

6. **Symposium proceedings (equivocal):** Justesen participated in multiple conference symposia (NYAM April 1979; FDA/CDRH microwave behavior workshop March 1982 in Midway, Utah, published 1985 as HHS FDA 85-8238). Neither set of proceedings references Sharp/Grove. Justesen's contributions to FDA 85-8238 cover aversive behavior and reinforcement properties of microwave fields — behavioral thermoregulation, not speech transmission.

### 4.3 What the 1975 APA article provides (and does not)

The Justesen 1975 account is both the primary published source and the terminal point of the public citation chain for the nine-word demonstration. It establishes:
- **Method:** zero-crossing pulse triggering on negative sine-wave deflections of recorded voice waveforms
- **Words transmitted:** single-syllable digits 1–10 (nine of ten recognized)
- **Self-experimentation:** Sharp and Grove irradiated themselves
- **Date:** personal communication September 28, 1973
- **Qualitative result:** words "not unlike those emitted by persons with artificial larynxes"
- **Limitation noted:** energy density constraint precluded longer messages

**What it does not provide:** subject count, RF carrier frequency, peak/average power density, pulse width, repetition rate, antenna geometry, distance from source, blinding/unblinding procedure, error analysis, or replication attempts. These parameters remain unlocated in any open-record Justesen document.

### 4.4 Why no separately-authored Justesen source is expected to exist

- Sharp and Grove communicated the demonstration to Justesen as a **personal communication**, not as a formal report deposited with them. Justesen was reporting something told to him — he was not a participant in designing or running the experiment.
- If Sharp and Grove produced a primary technical report, it would appear under their names (WRAIR authorship), not under Justesen's. Prior investigation of DTIC for Sharp/Grove TR returned no results (see SHARP_GROVE_PRIMARY_REPORT_HUNT.md §1.1).
- The description in Justesen 1975 is already more methodological detail than a "personal communication" citation conventionally implies, suggesting Justesen conveyed essentially everything Sharp/Grove shared with him in that conversation.

### 4.5 Residual uncertainty

A small but non-zero probability remains that:
- An internal VA or DOE internal memo by Justesen (not deposited with DTIC/OSTI) described the demo in more detail
- The **Nicholas Steneck collection** (Ford/Bentley Library) contains correspondence between Steneck and Justesen that includes additional detail
- A BEMS conference abstract or short communication (pre-1985) cites the Sharp/Grove method in Justesen's own words — BEMS annual meeting abstracts from 1978–1985 are not fully indexed in PubMed

## Appendix: Key Factual Corrections from This Investigation

1. **Death date:** Justesen died November 9, 2014 (not 2004 as stated in task brief). He was 84, born 1930.
