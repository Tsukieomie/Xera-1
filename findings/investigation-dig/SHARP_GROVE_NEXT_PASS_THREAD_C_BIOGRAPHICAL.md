## Section 1: Joseph C. Sharp — Biographical Profile

### Vital Statistics

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Full name | Joseph Cecil Sharp II |
| Born | May 30, 1934, Salt Lake City, Utah |
| Died | January 13, 2021, Saint George, Utah |
| Buried | Tonaquint Cemetery, Saint George, Utah |
| Obituary | *Salt Lake Tribune*, January 20, 2021 — Legacy.com archive |

### Education

Sharp earned his undergraduate, master's, and doctoral degrees in psychology and neuroanatomy at the **University of Utah**, Salt Lake City. No specific graduation years have been confirmed in the open record, but the trajectory is consistent with completing the doctorate by the late 1950s or circa 1960, immediately before the WRAIR appointment.

### Career Timeline

#### WRAIR (1961–1974)

Sharp joined the **Walter Reed Army Institute of Research (WRAIR)** in 1961 as a research psychologist. His departmental home across this period shifted as the microwave research program matured:

- **1961:** Research Psychologist, WRAIR (debut confirmed by 1963 *Perceptual and Motor Skills* paper on fetal x-irradiation in albino rats)
- **1966:** Named on the distribution list of the **Project Pandora Operational Procedure** alongside H. M. Grove; confirmed involvement in the classified Soviet-signal research program at this stage
- **1969–1970:** Concurrent appointment as **Deputy Commissioner of Public Health, State of New York** (secondary role; Sharp remained at WRAIR)
- **1970:** Appointed **Deputy Director of Neuropsychiatry, WRAIR**; also held the title **Chief, Department of Experimental Psychology and Behavioral Radiology**
- **FY1973–FY1974:** Listed as **Principal Investigator, Department of Microwave Research, Work Unit 057** on WRAIR annual reports — the unit under which the speech-demonstration experiment was conducted
- Described by Don Justesen (1975) as "former director of research in experimental psychology at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research"
- Jack Anderson's 1972 syndicated column identifies him as "Joseph Sharp of Walter Reed Army hospital" in connection with the psychotronic/microwave hearing program

**Project Pandora role:** Sharp served as Principal Investigator during the early phase of Project Pandora (the DARPA-funded program examining whether pulsed microwave signals could convey information or cause neurological effects, triggered by the Moscow Embassy signal). He apparently left the program before 1968 by his own account and later returned to microwave auditory work under the WU 057 designation.

#### NASA Ames Research Center (1974–1994)

Sharp transitioned from WRAIR to **NASA Ames Research Center, Moffett Field, California** in 1974. NASA Ames institutional history identifies him as a "psychophysiologist … assistant director of life sciences." He served at Ames for approximately 20 years, with the **last 5 years as Director, Office of Space Research**, before retiring around 1994.

#### Southern Utah University (post-1994)

After retirement from NASA, Sharp joined the faculty of **Southern Utah University (SUU), Cedar City, Utah**, in the College of Science in a teaching capacity. This placement in Cedar City, close to Saint George where he died in 2021, is consistent with his final decades in southern Utah.

## Section 2: H.M. Grove — Identity Resolution and Biographical Profile

### Identity Resolution: Mark Grove = H.M. Grove

**Finding: Mark Grove and H.M. Grove are definitively the same person.**

The informal given name "Mark" is the second element of his full name; he appears in professional records as **H. Mark Grove** and in the bureaucratic or classified record as **H. M. Grove**. The following five independent sources confirm the identity:

| Source | Form Used | Affiliation Stated |
|---|---|---|
| Project Pandora Operational Procedure, 1966 (FOIA/WHS) | H. M. Grove | WRAIR (distribution list alongside J. Sharp) |
| IEEE UFFC Newsletter, February 1973 | H. Mark Grove | Dept. of Microwave Research, WRAIR; Chairman, COMAR |
| Justesen 1975 (*American Psychologist*), Note 2 | Grove, M. | Personal communication, September 28, 1973 (co-author of speech demo) |
| IEEE T-MTT, 1974 paper (Sharp, Grove, Gandhi) | Grove, H.M. | WRAIR affiliation |
| IEEE T-MTT, 1976 paper (Lenox et al.) | H. Mark Grove, Senior Member IEEE | System Avionics Division, AFAL, Wright-Patterson AFB |

Jack Anderson's 1972 column describes him as **"engineer-microwave expert Mark Grove of the Air Force"** — which, in context, likely reflects Grove's Joint Services role (WRAIR is an Army institution, but Grove's engineering background was cross-service) rather than a strict Air Force assignment at that moment.

### Biographical Profile

#### Career Timeline

| Period | Role / Affiliation |
|---|---|
| Early 1960s–early 1973 | Electronic engineer, Dept. of Microwave Research, WRAIR |
| February 1973 | Chairman, IEEE Committee on Man and Radiation (COMAR), while still at WRAIR |
| September 28, 1973 | Co-demonstrator with Sharp: microwave speech encoding to a human subject (personal communication cited by Justesen 1975) |
| 1974 | Co-author, IEEE T-MTT mechanism paper (Sharp, Grove, Gandhi); WRAIR affiliation |
| By 1976 | System Avionics Division, **Air Force Avionics Laboratory (AFAL), Wright-Patterson AFB, OH** (confirmed by Lenox et al. 1976 byline) |
| ~1975–1980 | OSD/DoD: **Management Steering Committee for Embedded Computer Resources (MSC-ECR)**; Director of Embedded Computer Resources |
| 1980 | Shepherded formation of the **Ada Joint Program Office (AJPO)** (Ada DoD HOLWG history) |
| 1982 | Published "DOD policy for acquisition of embedded computer resources," *Concepts* 5:9–36, Autumn 1982 |
| March 1982 | Listed in **DoD Locator Chart** as "H Mark Grove, 2A318" |
| November 1989 | Listed in *Army Aviation Magazine* |
| Post-career | **"H. Mark Grove Award for Excellence in Software Management"** established by the Software Program Managers Network in his honor |

#### Significance of the Post-WRAIR Career Trajectory

Grove's career after 1974 moved entirely away from microwave biophysics and into **DoD software acquisition policy** — specifically the embedded computer resources problem that became the Ada programming language initiative. This explains the complete absence of Grove's name from any microwave research publication after the 1976 enzyme-inactivation paper. The eponymous software management award is the most definitive single piece of biographical evidence confirming that Grove's later identity was as a software policy figure, not a microwave researcher.

#### IEEE Standing

Grove held the grade of **Senior Member, IEEE**, as confirmed by the 1976 IEEE T-MTT byline — a grade requiring at least 10 years of significant professional practice, consistent with a career beginning in the early-to-mid 1960s.

### Obituary Status

**No obituary for H. Mark Grove has been located** in the open record as of the date of this report. The absence is consistent with his relatively obscure public profile and the cross-agency nature of his career.

## Section 3: First-Person Statements About the 1973 Demonstration

### Primary Finding: No First-Person Account Exists in the Open Record

Neither Joseph C. Sharp nor H. Mark Grove is known to have published or publicly stated a first-person account of the **September 28, 1973 speech-encoding demonstration** in any open-record source located by this investigation.

The demonstration is documented exclusively via **Don Justesen's third-person citation** in the 1975 *American Psychologist* article:

> "Sharp, J.C., & Grove, M. Personal communication, September 28, 1973."

Justesen's account (Note 2) reads: Sharp and Grove, at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, "have found that speech may be transmitted to a human being by means of pulsed microwave irradiation." Justesen describes them encoding "single-sideband amplitude modulation of the microwave carrier" such that an operator could understand words "whispered" directly into their microwave antenna. This is the **sole primary published account** of the demonstration. Sharp and Grove themselves wrote no published paper specifically describing the speech protocol.

### Sharp's Only Confirmed Public Statement (Adjacent Topic)

One direct quote from Sharp has been located, commenting on the classified status of Project Pandora — not on the speech demonstration itself. The statement appears in Jack Anderson's syndicated column, reprinted in the **Congressional Record, May 16, 1972**, and in a CIA clipping compilation:

> **"Pandora was classified in those days and still is."** — Joseph C. Sharp

Context: Sharp made this comment in connection with Anderson's reporting on the microwave hearing program's classified history. It confirms Sharp was willing to acknowledge Pandora's existence to journalists but did not provide technical details.

No additional Sharp quotes — and no Grove quotes of any kind — have been found.

### Implications for the Investigation

The gap is significant. The demonstration took place in **September 1973**, but the first published description did not appear until **Justesen's 1975 paper**, and the mechanism paper (Sharp, Grove, Gandhi, 1974) does not describe the speech-encoding protocol in detail — it addresses the acoustic-signal generation mechanism. This leaves a gap of nearly two years in which the demonstration existed only as an internal WRAIR communication.

The HEW/FDA 77-8026 symposium paper (Sharp & Grove 1977, status: **unconfirmed** — see Section 1) would, if verified, be the closest thing to a first-person account by Sharp and Grove. Locating the physical proceedings volume at NLM is the highest-priority open task.

## Section 4: Archive Deposit Leads

The following repositories are ranked in approximate order of likely yield for Sharp/Grove primary materials:

### 1. WRAIR Gorgas Memorial Library Archives *(Highest Priority)*
- **Location:** Silver Spring, Maryland
- **Contact:** https://wrair.health.mil/Library-Archival-Collection/
- **Rationale:** The finding aid explicitly notes holdings of "working papers of selected military and civilian researchers." Sharp and Grove were the principal researchers in the WRAIR Department of Microwave Research (Work Unit 057) during the critical 1972–1974 period. Lab notebooks, annual technical reports, and correspondence relating to the speech-demonstration experiment would most naturally be held here.
- **Action:** Submit a records inquiry specifying: Joseph C. Sharp, H. Mark Grove, Work Unit 057, Department of Microwave Research, FY1972–FY1975.

### 2. National Library of Medicine, History of Medicine Division
- **URL:** https://catalog.nlm.nih.gov
- **Rationale:** NLM holds the physical copy of HEW Publication (FDA) 77-8026 (*Symposium on Biological Effects and Measurement of Radio Frequency/Microwaves*, Rockville, MD, 1977). This is the volume that may contain the Sharp & Grove "Individual Perception of Pulsed Microwave Radiation" paper. The NLM catalog should confirm whether the paper appears in the proceedings. NLM HMD also holds the Zory Glaser microwave bioeffects bibliography collection, which may contain additional indexed materials.
- **Action:** Search NLM catalog for HEW 77-8026; request interlibrary loan or table-of-contents scan to confirm Sharp/Grove paper presence.

### 3. National Security Archive, George Washington University
- **URL:** https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/briefing-book/intelligence-russia-programs/2022-09-13/moscow-signals-declassified-microwave
- **Rationale:** Holds the largest open collection of declassified Project Pandora and Moscow signal documents. Best suited for programmatic context — Pandora oversight reports, Senate committee materials, and DARPA correspondence — rather than laboratory-level notes. Sharp appears by name in the Senate report held here (NSArchive document 15).
- **Action:** Request document set from the 2022 "Moscow Signals Declassified" briefing book; cross-reference for Sharp/Grove memo traffic.

### 4. AFRL / AFAL Historian, Wright-Patterson AFB
- **Contact:** Air Force Research Laboratory History Office, Wright-Patterson AFB, OH 45433
- **Rationale:** Grove transferred to the **System Avionics Division, Air Force Avionics Laboratory (AFAL)** at Wright-Patterson by 1976. AFAL records from 1974–1980 may hold Grove's project reports, technical memoranda, and correspondence related to his transition from microwave biology to embedded computer resources.
- **Action:** File a records request with the AFRL History Office for personnel and project files relating to H. Mark Grove, System Avionics Division, ca. 1974–1980.

### 5. IEEE History Center (Hoboken, NJ / online)
- **URL:** https://ethw.org/
- **Rationale:** Grove chaired the **IEEE Committee on Man and Radiation (COMAR)** as of February 1973. IEEE committee records, including agendas, minutes, and correspondence from COMAR's early years, may be held at the IEEE History Center. This would document Grove's professional activities in the period immediately preceding the September 1973 demonstration.
- **Action:** Contact IEEE History Center to inquire about COMAR archival records, 1972–1975.

### 6. University of Utah Marriott Library Special Collections
- **URL:** https://lib.utah.edu/collections/special-collections/
- **Rationale:** Sharp's alma mater. No finding aid for Sharp papers has been identified, but institutional alumni records and departmental archives in the psychology department may contain biographical materials, thesis copies, or correspondence.
- **Action:** Contact Special Collections to request a search for Sharp alumni/faculty materials; also contact the Department of Psychology for any retained records.

## Section 5: Patent Inventory

### Result: No Sharp or Grove Patents Located

Searches of USPTO records covering the period **1970–1990** have located **no patents naming Joseph C. Sharp or H. Mark Grove as inventors** in the microwave auditory, neuromodulation, or related fields.

### US3951134A — Clarification of Common Misattribution

This patent is **frequently and incorrectly attributed to Sharp and Grove** in online discussions of microwave hearing and psychotronic weapons. The facts are:

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Patent number | US 3,951,134 A |
| Title | Apparatus and method for remotely monitoring and altering brain waves |
| **Actual inventor** | Robert G. Malech |
| **Assignee** | Dorne and Margolin Inc. |
| Filed | August 5, 1974 |
| Granted | April 20, 1976 |
| Sharp named? | **No** |
| Grove named? | **No** |
| URL | https://patents.google.com/patent/US3951134A/en |

This patent describes a method for **remotely monitoring brain waves**, not for encoding speech into microwave pulses. The subject matter is different from Sharp and Grove's work, and the inventors have no documented connection to WRAIR or the 1973 demonstration.

### Related Later Patent Branch (Not Connected to Sharp/Grove)

Patents US6587729B2 and US6470214B1, filed by O'Loughlin and Loree (USAF, ~2002–2003), relate to voice-to-skull microwave transmission but are a distinct later development; Sharp and Grove are not named and had no documented involvement.

### Assessment

The absence of Sharp or Grove patents is not surprising given the classified context of their work. Research conducted under military classification at WRAIR during the Cold War was rarely patented by individual researchers; findings were disseminated through controlled technical reports and, selectively, through peer-reviewed publication.
