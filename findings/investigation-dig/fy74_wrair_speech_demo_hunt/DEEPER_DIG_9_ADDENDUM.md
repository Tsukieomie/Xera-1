**The single highest-value finding of round 9** is in the project number: **AD-747684 (Frey/Messenger/Eichert 1972) was funded under WRAIR Project 1J662712AJ21 — the exact same project number as both AD-758136 (Hawkins/Grove/Heiple/Schrot 1973) and the 28 September 1973 Sharp/Grove voice-modulation demonstration** (Justesen 1975 *Am Psychol* Note 2 round 0 §1; Justesen 1979 NYAM round 3 §3). The Frey 1972 report is therefore not a parallel Frey-group precursor — it is a **direct documentary predecessor** within the same WRAIR-USAMERDC project line, carried out by Randomline, Inc. (Allan Frey's corporate vehicle) under USAMERDC contract DAAK02-71-C-0213 in the 14-month window February 1971 – April 1972, with the final report dated June 1972 — **15 months before the Sharp/Grove demonstration**.

The abstract is the most consequential round-9 verbatim retrieval. From hOCR lines 52-53:

> *"The objective of the experimentation reported herein was to obtain additional data on the rf sound phenomenon. This phenomenon is the perception of what is reported to be a sound when the head is illuminated with rf energy of particular characteristics. In the first phase of the experimentation, we assembled a portable rf sound demonstration unit. This was successfully used to demonstrate the phenomenon and its characteristics. In phase two, we determined what rf parameters are relevant to generating the perception of rf sounds of significance. We learned enough in this phase to successfully create a perceived sound that we desired to create. **In phase three, we explored the possibility of determining if the perception of speech could be induced. It was found that aspects of vocoder technology could not be used to generate speech. It was also found that perceived rf sound can not be generated using the rules of the periodicity pitch phenomenon.**"*

The Phase 3 body section (hOCR lines 411-499) elaborates verbatim:

> *"Having established that a specific sound could be created, we entered into phase three of the project. From the data obtained to this point it was clear that because of the nature of rf sound transduction, the acoustic procedure used in vocoders could not be applied. The quality differences and timbre character[istics] … the construction of speech from simple buzzes and hisses. A buzz, for example, … Thus, we explored the possibility of using the periodicity pitch phenomena from acoustics as a means of generating the perception of speech sound. … Thus, the periodicity pitch phenomena is not the path to pursue in order to generate speech sounds. We could conclude from this phase three data that it would be necessary to have a better understanding of the rf sound transducer and its mechanism before it will be possible to generate speech sounds."*

The final Conclusions section (hOCR line 500) restates:

> *"In phase three, we explored the possibility of determining if the perception of speech could be induced. It was found that aspects of vocoder technology could not be used to generate speech. It was also found that perceived rf speech can not be generated using the rules of the periodicity pitch phenomenon. It would appear that meaningful sounds can be created if desired. **The possibility of creating speech, however, is uncertain. The creation of speech, if it is possible, will require additional knowledge of the mechanism of the effect.**"*

Phase 2 of the Frey 1972 report (hOCR line 381) does document successful creation of *meaningful* (non-speech) RF sounds — *"bongo drums, lawnmower engines, small model airplane engines, electric saws, crickets, knocking on doors, and tapping of a pencil"* — and a successful test reproduction of *"the sound of an incoming bullet"* (hOCR lines 385, 411): *"The subjects reported that the rf sound, though low in intensity, sounded like the tape recorded incoming bullet sound. This effort established that meaningful rf sounds could be constructed."* Frey's group thus achieved environmental-sound RF reproduction in 1971-72 but explicitly **failed to induce speech perception** through either vocoder-technology pulse trains or periodicity-pitch acoustic analogs.

This is the **first documentary precursor** to the 1973 Sharp/Grove voice-modulation demonstration in the entire investigation. **The Sharp/Grove demonstration was, on its own contemporary project record, the first successful induction of speech perception via radio-frequency illumination on a WRAIR-USAMERDC project line where a 15-months-prior attempt by the field's leading auditory-effect investigator had explicitly failed.** Sharp's documented method (the Justesen 1975 *Am Psychol* Note 2 / Brodeur 1977 round-7 §3 / Bowart round-8 §5 zero-crossing pulse-trigger technique using prerecorded voice waveforms) is, in hindsight, a **divergence** from Frey's approach: Frey 1972 attempted construction of speech from synthetic vocoder-style buzz/hiss pulse trains and from periodicity-pitch acoustic analogs, both of which failed; Sharp/Grove 1973 used direct waveform-zero-crossing triggering of microwave pulses from a recording of human speech, producing intelligible perception. Frey himself wrote in the conclusions section that *"the creation of speech, if it is possible, will require additional knowledge of the mechanism of the effect"* — and 15 months later, on the same WRAIR project number, Sharp and Grove demonstrated it.

The Frey 1972 reference list (hOCR lines 504-507) cites Frey's own 1965 *Psych Bull* "Behavioral Biophysics" review, his 1971 *IEEE T-MTT* MTT-19(2):153-164 "Biological function as influenced by low-power modulated rf energy," and the foundational [Frey 1962 *J Appl Physiol* 17(4):689-692](https://journals.physiology.org/doi/abs/10.1152/jappl.1962.17.4.689) "Human auditory system response to modulated electromagnetic energy," plus Ritsma (acoustical-society pitch-residue work) and Schouten/Ritsma/Cardozo (1962 acoustical-society pitch papers). **No Hawkins, Sharp, Grove, Heiple, Schrot, Justesen, or other WRAIR-group references appear** — the Frey 1972 report is structurally a Randomline-internal Frey-lineage document, not a WRAIR-collaboration report, even though it was funded on the same WRAIR project number.
