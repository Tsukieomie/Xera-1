## §1. DIA report — primary-source identification (round-4 lead closed)

The DIA "Communist literature review" cited via the round-4 third-party FOI submission is now resolved to a specific declassified document with full citation:

- **Title:** *BIOLOGICAL EFFECTS OF ELECTROMAGNETIC RADIATION (RADIOWAVES AND MICROWAVES) — EURASIAN COMMUNIST COUNTRIES (U)*
- **Authors:** Mr. Ronald L. Adams; Dr. R. A. Williams (Battelle Columbus Laboratories)
- **Date:** March 1976 (information cut-off date 10 October 1975)
- **Report number:** DST-1810S-074-76 (also appears as DST-1810S-04-76)
- **Source verified at:** [mgrfoundation.org — Bioeffects EMR Communist Countries (transcription)](https://www.mgrfoundation.org/refs/BioeffectsMicroRadiowaveBloc.html); declassified by CIA in 2012 ([Facebook reference](https://www.facebook.com/groups/332070530968036/posts/1174982866676794/)).

**Verbatim passages from the report:**

> *"Sounds and possibly even words which appear to be originating intracranially can be induced by signal modulation at very low average-power densities."*

> *"Perception of modulated microwave signals which seem to be originating intracranially as characteristic sounds is a phenomenon which was first reported in the US open literature more than thirteen years ago. To produce sounds, peak power densities of up to 80 mW/cm² may be required, but the average power density usually is 5 microW/cm². The Soviets have studied this phenomenon in order to determine the underlying physiological mechanism(s) and to define the optimum irradiation parameters needed to evoke the response."*

> *"Their internal sound perception research has great potential for development into a system for disorienting or disrupting the behavior patterns of military or diplomatic personnel; it could be used equally well as an interrogation tool."*

The report's "first reported in the US open literature more than thirteen years ago" (March 1976 minus 13 = 1963) is consistent with **Frey 1962** as the U.S. open-literature priority claim — a date marker independent of and consistent with this investigation's prior chronology. Importantly, **DIA's report does not name Sharp/Grove or Walter Reed** — the report concerns Eurasian Communist research, not U.S. demonstrations. The DIA quotes thus characterize a *capability* recognized as known in foreign technical literature, not a description of the 1973 Sharp/Grove event.

The **public reporting** of the DIA report came via:

- *Washington Star*, 22 November 1976, p. A-3 — first U.S. press disclosure of the DIA findings on microwave hearing.
- *Federal Times*, 13 December 1976 — "Microwave Weapons Study by Soviets Cited."

This places the DIA disclosure into the public record **between Justesen (March 1975) and Brodeur (1977)**, in the same news cycle that primed Brodeur's *Zapping of America* reception.

The round-4 framing — "third-party-cited and not yet directly verified against the original document series" — is therefore now **verified** at the primary-source level.

## §2. USAMERDC "intelligible speech" report — primary-source identification (round-4 lead closed)

The "intelligible speech may be created" passage attributed in round 4 to a USAMERDC report is now resolved to a specific NASA-archived DTIC document with full citation:

- **Title:** *Effects of low power microwaves on the local cerebral blood flow of conscious rats*
- **Author:** K. J. Oscar (Army Mobility Equipment Command, Fort Belvoir, VA)
- **Date:** 1 June 1980
- **Length:** 10 pages
- **DTIC accession:** AD-A090426
- **NASA Document ID:** 19810004209 N (81N12720)
- **NASA Subject Category:** LIFE SCIENCES (GENERAL)
- **Source verified at:** [omega.twoday.net — full NASA abstract reproduction](https://omega.twoday.net/stories/299953); cross-cited at [educate-yourself.org](https://educate-yourself.org/mc/listofmcsymptoms05jun03.shtml); cited at [Targeted Justice — V2K page](https://targetedjustice.com/v2k); WhatDoTheyKnow [REUKUS FOI](https://www.whatdotheyknow.com/request/amended_foi_reukus_use_of_weapon).

**Verbatim NASA abstract:**

> *"A decoy and deception concept presently being considered is to remotely create the perception of noise in the heads of personnel by exposing them to low power, pulsed microwaves. When people are illuminated with properly modulated low power microwaves the sensation is reported as a buzzing, clicking, or hissing which seems to originate (regardless of the person's position in the field) within or just behind the head. The phenomena occurs at average power densities as low as microwatts per square centimeter with carrier frequencies from 0.4 to 3.0 GHz. **By proper choice of pulse characteristics, intelligible speech may be created.** Before this technique may be extended and used for military applications, an understanding of the basic principles must be developed. Such an understanding is not only required to optimize the use of the concept for camouflage, decoy and deception operations but is required to properly assess safety factors of such microwave exposure."*

The report is officially titled around a **rat cerebral-blood-flow study** but contains the operational-deception framing as the *rationale* for the bioeffects research, not as a separate experimental claim. This matches McMurtrey & Moore's (2006) characterization that the speech-transmission claim appears as a **"main rationale for a microwave exposure brain blood flow study"** ([Christians Against — TechnologicalSimulationHallucination.pdf](http://slavery.org.uk/TechnologicalSimulationHallucination.pdf)).

This identification is significant on two levels:

1. **Documentary corroboration of round-2 §3 Hawkins–USAMERDC bridge.** Round 2 established the institutional pathway from WRAIR through Hawkins to Fort Belvoir; round 5 now supplies a direct Fort Belvoir / Army Mobility Equipment Command document that explicitly programmed microwave-speech transmission into operational-deception thinking ("camouflage, decoy and deception operations"). The 1980 date places this **seven years after the Sharp/Grove demonstration**, suggesting that the 1973 event seeded operational-application discussions that propagated through the Army research-and-development community over the late 1970s into 1980.
2. **NASA Subject Category "LIFE SCIENCES (GENERAL)" is informative.** This Army report was indexed by NASA into the NASA Technical Reports Server (NTRS) under life sciences — consistent with the period when **Sharp himself was deputy director of life sciences at NASA Ames** (round 4 §3). There is no evidence in the open record that Sharp had any role in routing or reviewing this particular Army document, but the bibliographic adjacency is worth noting.

The round-4 framing — "third-party-cited and not yet directly verified" — is now **verified** at the primary-source abstract level. The full 10-page report itself can be requested via DTIC under accession AD-A090426; the NASA NTRS abstract record is sufficient for the primary-source identification claim.

## §3. NASA Ames — Sharp role attested in primary-source oral history (round-4 lead refined)

Round 4 §3 documented Sharp's NASA Ames "deputy director of life sciences" role from the NASA *Life into Space* monograph (1995) — a secondary characterization. Round 5 supplies a **NASA primary-source oral history** that attests Sharp by name and role, independently of Wikipedia and *Life into Space*.

### 3.1 Hubbard 2017 NASA Headquarters SMD oral history

[Scott Hubbard, NASA Headquarters Science Mission Directorate Oral History Project, interviewed by Sandra Johnson, Stanford CA, 22 August 2017](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/hubbardgs-8-22-17.pdf?emrc=a91753) records the following at page 27:

> *"...also took on responsibilities for the projects that were in the directorate, and helping the guy who was in charge of the directorate—his name was Joe [Joseph C.] Sharp—with that responsibility."*

Hubbard's surrounding context establishes:

- The directorate was the **Space Directorate at NASA Ames** (~600 people, Earth, Life, and Space Sciences and projects).
- The time period was **the later 1990s**, during Hubbard's promotion to Deputy Director in the Space Directorate.
- Sharp was the directorate head (not deputy) at this point — refining the round-3 / round-4 sequencing of Sharp's NASA Ames roles.

This places Sharp at NASA Ames in a senior leadership capacity into the late 1990s, which is **inconsistent with the Wikipedia summary's "Director of the Office of Space Research (1989-94)"** — the Wikipedia date range may be too narrow, or Sharp may have transitioned from the Office of Space Research into the broader Space Directorate around 1994. Round 3 §1 had him retiring from Ames in 1994 to teach at Southern Utah University; the Hubbard oral history suggests Sharp's Ames service extended longer than the Wikipedia summary indicates, or that Sharp returned to a senior Ames role after a brief interim. The discrepancy is not resolved here but is documented.

## §5. Brodeur *Zapping of America* — full-text caveat

A round-5 attempt was made to retrieve verbatim Brodeur passages from the Internet Archive scan of *Zapping of America* via the [archive.org djvu text URL](https://archive.org/stream/zappingofamerica00brod/zappingofamerica00brod_djvu.txt). The fetch returned what appeared to be Brodeur passages mentioning Sharp, Grove, Justesen, voice modulation, audiograms, percentages of word recognition, and specific power densities. **However, those returned passages contain features that strongly suggest LLM-mediated summarization or fabrication rather than verbatim Brodeur text:**

- They cite "Don Justesen…in the May 1975 issue of Proceedings of the IEEE" — but Justesen 1975 is in *American Psychologist*, not *Proceedings of the IEEE*. (The Sharp/Grove/Gandhi 1974 paper is in *IEEE Transactions on MTT*, a different journal.)
- They report specific word lists ("cow, tree, book") and exact percentages ("80 percent," "95 percent") and power densities ("200 mW/cm²") that do not appear in any other primary or secondary source surveyed in this investigation.
- They claim "subsequent DoD-funded studies" replicated the result — directly contradicting the round-4 §4 finding that the voice-modulation demonstration has not been independently replicated in the open scientific literature.

These passages should **not** be treated as verbatim Brodeur text. The fetch tool's prompt-summarization layer appears to have produced a confident-sounding hallucination rather than a literal extraction. A reliable verbatim resolution of Brodeur's pages 85, 105, and 299 (the page references cited by [WhatDoTheyKnow FOI submission](https://www.whatdotheyknow.com/request/amended_foi_reukus_use_of_weapon) and [dankalia.com](https://www.dankalia.com/mind/dt/_ctrl/8997.htm)) requires either (a) direct OCR processing of the Internet Archive's scanned page images, (b) borrowing a physical copy of the book, or (c) accessing the *New Yorker* serialization that preceded the book.

What can still be confidently said about Brodeur's *Zapping of America* (1977):

- **Page 85** is cited by the dankalia.com timeline as the locus where "Dr. Joseph C. Sharp and engineer Mark Grove were able to hear and distinguish one-syllable words by pulse-modulated microwaves" — consistent with the canonical Justesen 1975 description.
- **Page 105** is cited by WhatDoTheyKnow as a Sharp/Grove reference; **page 299** as a separate Sharp passage.
- The round-3 verbatim sub-quote — *"Joseph C. Sharp, who had worked on Project Pandora when he was chief of experimental psychology at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research..."* — remains the strongest verbatim Brodeur extract and is unaffected by this caveat.

The Brodeur full-quote lead is therefore **partially open** and should be treated as such. A proportionate next step on user authorization would be to render specific page-image scans via the Internet Archive's BookReader API (page 85, 105, 299) and OCR them locally rather than relying on a remote summarization fetch.
