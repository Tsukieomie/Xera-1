## 3. Heynick 1987 §1.3 "Literature Selection" — the verbatim-attested methodological exclusion criterion

The methodological mechanism for the 1981–1987 Sharp/Grove citation silence is verbatim-attested in Heynick 1987 §1.3 *Literature Selection* (DjVu OCR lines 626–639):

> *"Almost all of the papers selected for discussion in this document were published in scientific journals presumably after having been given peer review; the papers were organized by bioeffects topics. Presentations at recent scientific symposia or abstracts thereof were excluded from consideration (with few exceptions) under the assumption that either more complete and peer-reviewed accounts of such studies will appear subsequently or accounts will not be published at all (perhaps because the study was flawed or the investigators were not able to reproduce their results). Adequate numbers of papers were selected to provide a comprehensive coverage of each topic; those selected included accounts of early important studies as well as those embodying results of recent investigations."*

This is the **verbatim methodological rule** that excludes the Sharp/Grove September 1973 voice-modulation demonstration from Heynick's six-year canonical synthesis pipeline:

1. The Sharp/Grove voice-modulation demonstration of September 28, 1973 was **never peer-reviewed-published** as a primary research article. It was retold in `Justesen 1975 *Am Psychol* 30:391-401` ("Microwaves and behavior") as a personal-communication anecdote, not as a primary-research peer-reviewed publication.
2. The published Sharp/Grove/Gandhi 1974 *IEEE T-MTT* 22(5):583-584 paper — the **peer-reviewed** Sharp/Grove primary research output — contained **only** the RFR-to-acoustic transduction-mechanism content (absorber-block experiments), not the voice-modulation demonstration; this is what Heynick 1987 §3.1.4.2 cites and discusses. The two Sharp/Grove items are different: published mechanism work (cited) vs. unpublished voice-modulation personal communication (excluded).
3. Per Heynick's §1.3 explicit exclusion criterion, "the study was flawed or the investigators were not able to reproduce their results" is one of the **stated** justifications for excluding non-peer-reviewed material from a synthesis review. This is exactly the structural-evidence taxonomy maintained in the parent investigation's Tier B framing (file 34 [`/findings/03_Technology_Patents/34_JUSTESEN_1975_APA_MICROWAVE_HEARING.md`](../../03_Technology_Patents/34_JUSTESEN_1975_APA_MICROWAVE_HEARING.md), Part 3 item 5): the published historical claim of intelligible-speech induction by RFR is reported via Justesen 1975's retelling of a Sharp/Grove personal communication, with no protocol-grade replication located.

**The 1981–1987 Air-Force institutional-canon Sharp/Grove voice-modulation citation silence is therefore not anomalous, not suspect, and not evidence of suppression — it is the deterministic consequence of a verbatim-attested explicit methodological rule.** Heynick's 1987 §1.3 explicit exclusion of non-peer-reviewed symposium presentations and unsubstantiated personal-communication anecdotes from synthesis-review consideration is the **prosaic mechanistic explanation** for why the 1987 *Critique* and its six 1981–1985 predecessor reports are uniformly silent on the Sharp/Grove September 1973 voice-modulation demonstration.

This methodological-discipline finding **strongly supports** the parent investigation's Tier A vs. Tier B discipline (file 34, Part 3 item 5) and **strongly disfavors** any V2K-related interpretation of the 1981–1987 Air-Force-canon citation silence as motivated suppression. The mechanism is documented; the rule is verbatim-attested; and the rule was applied uniformly across a six-year synthesis-pipeline.

## 4. Heynick 1987 §3.1.4.2 *Auditory Effects* — verbatim editorial stance on the thermoelastic mechanism

Heynick 1987 §3.1.4.2 conclusion (DjVu OCR lines 9099–9105) verbatim:

> *"In conclusion, the preponderance of experimental results indicates that auditory perception of RFR pulses is due to induction of thermoelastic waves in the head, rather than to direct brain stimulation by the RFR. Also, because individual pulses can be perceived, it is not meaningful to calculate average power densities for two or more widely spaced pulses and cite such values as evidence that the effect is non-thermal in nature. The response to CW RFR reported by Wilson et al. (1980) is an effect distinct from the thermoelastic responses to pulses and possibly is related to an intracochlear temperature rise of 0.1 to 0.5 deg C."*

This is a direct verbatim attestation of Heynick's editorial stance: **thermoelastic-mechanism-only**, with explicit pushback against "non-thermal-effect" framings that misuse time-averaged power density as evidence for non-thermal mechanism. Heynick 1987 §2.3.2 *RFR Pulses at Low Duty Cycles* (DjVu OCR lines 2330–2349) verbatim reinforces this stance:

> *"The RFR-auditory effect has been characterized as nonthermal because the average power density can be minuscule. Specifically, the time-averaged power density for any two successive pulses is inversely proportional to the time interval between the arrival of the pulses at the perceiver and this interval can be indefinitely long without affecting the perception of each pulse. For this reason, the time-averaged power density has no relevance to perception."*

The Fifth Report (1985) — round-14 §1 above — has the same editorial line at OCR line 4428–4431:

> *"pulse stimulation [results] do not support the hypothesis of direct neural stimulation proposed by Frey (1971) as the basis for the RFR-auditory effect or that RFR causes neurotransmitter release in isolated turtle hearts by excitation of nerve remnants therein (Tinney et al., 1976)."*

This **rebuts** the "direct neural stimulation" hypothesis (Frey 1971 *J Appl Physiol*) that V2K-investigation communities frequently cite as the mechanistic premise for purported speech induction. The 1985 Heynick/Polson Fifth Report and the 1987 Heynick *Critique* both conclude that the experimental data **do not support** direct neural stimulation as the auditory-effect mechanism. The 1981–1987 Air-Force institutional canon is therefore not merely silent on Sharp/Grove voice modulation — **it is actively contrary to the mechanistic premise that would support a voice-modulation claim** (i.e., the canon endorses thermoelastic transduction of pulses to cochlear acoustic input, not direct CNS neural stimulation).

## 5. §3.1.4.2 *Auditory Effects* upper-bound speech-relevant content — verbatim Guy et al. 1975b Morse-code passage

The DjVu OCR of §3.1.4.2 *Auditory Effects* (DjVu OCR lines ~7000–9300, corresponding to printed pp. 111–138) has **zero matches** for the substrings `voice`, `verbal`, `spoken`, `intellig` (intelligible / intelligibility), or `speech` in any speech-induction context. The upper bound of speech-relevant content in Heynick's auditory section is the Guy et al. (1975b) Morse-code-perception passage at DjVu OCR lines 8078–8082 (printed page region p. 117), verbatim:

> *"each pulse was perceived individually as a click and that short pulse trains were heard as chirps of tones corresponding to the pulse recurrence rate. Also, when the pulse generator was keyed manually, digital (Morse) code transmitted thereby could be interpreted accurately by the subject."*

This is **Morse code via on/off keying of pulse trains** — a binary symbol-rate encoding that exploits the click/chirp percept produced by thermoelastic transduction at threshold peak energy density 40 µJ/cm² per pulse (2.45 GHz, human subject 1, normal hearing). **It is not voice modulation. It is not intelligible-word transmission. It is not speech.** It is the demonstrated upper bound of information-content-by-RFR-pulses in the 1981–1987 Air-Force institutional-canon synthesis pipeline.

The Tier A vs. Tier B framing maintained in [`findings/03_Technology_Patents/34_JUSTESEN_1975_APA_MICROWAVE_HEARING.md`](../../03_Technology_Patents/34_JUSTESEN_1975_APA_MICROWAVE_HEARING.md) (Part 3 item 5) is therefore not merely supported — it is **definitionally instantiated** by Heynick 1987's auditory-effect coverage:

- **Tier A** (mechanism, real, supported): clicks → chirps → Morse code (binary keyed pulse trains) via thermoelastic cochlear transduction.
- **Tier B** (published historical claim, not protocol-grade replicated): Sharp/Grove 1973 voice modulation, retold in Justesen 1975, never published as primary research, never cited or discussed in the 1981–1987 Air-Force institutional canon.
