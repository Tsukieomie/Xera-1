## §1. Don R. Justesen biographical resolution

The round-3 lead "Justesen's KU Medical Center research files" was held back by an unresolved biographical question: when did Justesen die, and where did his papers go? Round 4 closes the biographical side of that question.

- **Don R. Justesen, born 1930, died 9 November 2014, age 84**, at the Veterans Home in Warrensburg, Missouri. Source: [Don Justesen obituary, Kansas City Star / Legacy.com](https://www.legacy.com/us/obituaries/kansascity/name/don-justesen-obituary?id=4169060).
- The Justesen *et al.* 1975 *American Psychologist* article (the primary source for the Sharp/Grove demonstration) gives Justesen's institutional addresses explicitly in the reprint-request line: **Laboratories of Experimental Neuropsychology, VA Medical Center / Hospital, Kansas City, Missouri 64128**, with a dual affiliation at the **Department of Psychiatry, University of Kansas Medical Center, Kansas City, Kansas 66103**. Source: [Justesen 1975 PDF, p. 1 reprint-request line](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf).
- Justesen's death in 2014 sets a **concrete lower bound on any "papers deposited" event**. Personal academic papers from a researcher with dual VA / university affiliations are typically deposited at one of the two institutions, occasionally at both. The two candidate repositories for Justesen's papers are therefore:
  1. **University of Kansas Medical Center Archives** (Kansas City, KS) — the academic affiliation. KU Medical Center has a dedicated archives unit; a finding aid for Justesen has not yet been located online but is the natural first inquiry.
  2. **VA Medical Center Kansas City** (Kansas City, MO) — the research-laboratory affiliation. VA institutional records typically retain administrative papers but are less likely to hold personal research files.
- The Kenneth Spencer Research Library (KU Lawrence campus) was checked in round 2 §8 as an additional possibility because of its general KU manuscripts mandate; KU Medical Center Archives in Kansas City KS is the tighter match for Justesen's institutional affiliation and is now the more specific lead.

This biographical resolution does not by itself produce a 28 September 1973 contemporaneous note; it sharpens the archival inquiry by identifying the precise repository targets and confirming that Justesen's working papers are no longer in private hands.

## §3. Sharp NASA Ames role — specificity beyond Wikipedia

Round 3 §1 documented Sharp's NASA Ames career (1974-1994) using Wikipedia and adjacent sources, with the specific role given as "Director of the Office of Space Research (1989-94)." Round 4 surfaces an earlier, more specific NASA role and the first independent confirmation of Sharp's NASA Ames footprint outside Wikipedia.

### 3.1 Deputy director of life sciences, NASA Ames

The NASA monograph **"Life into Space, 1965-1990"** (NASA Reference Publication 1372, 1995) records Sharp's role at Ames as **deputy director of life sciences** — a specific position predating the directorship. Source: [NASA — life_into_space_vol1.pdf](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/life_into_space_vol1.pdf). This refines the Wikipedia summary, which had only the 1989-94 directorship; "deputy director of life sciences" is consistent with a 1974-1989 Ames career arc that culminated in the directorship.

### 3.2 Sharp 1987 Mars-mission paper (NASA NTRS 19870008327)

Sharp authored a paper titled **"NEED FOR ARTIFICIAL GRAVITY ON A MANNED MARS MISSION?"** in *Manned Mars Mission Working Group Papers, Volume 2, Section 5* (NASA NTRS 19870008327, 1987). Source: [archive.org — NASA_NTRS_Archive_19870008327](https://archive.org/stream/NASA_NTRS_Archive_19870008327/NASA_NTRS_Archive_19870008327_djvu.txt).

This is direct documentary evidence that Sharp's NASA-era publication record was in **bioastronautics and space life sciences**, not microwave bioeffects. It substantiates the round-3 finding (§1 of the round-3 addendum) that Sharp did not continue microwave research after leaving WRAIR; his post-1974 publication trail is firmly inside NASA's space-life-sciences program.

### 3.3 Sharp oral-history cameo in "Life into Space"

Sharp appears in the oral-history apparatus of "Life into Space" with brief recollections of his Ames tenure — referenced in a secondary archive transcription at [archive.org — Animales en el Espacio (full text)](https://archive.org/stream/Animalsinspace/Animales%20en%20el%20Espacio_djvu.txt). The cameo is brief and does not address the WRAIR period or the 1973 voice-modulation demonstration, but it confirms that Sharp left an institutional oral-history footprint at NASA Ames. Pursuit of the full oral-history transcript (NASA Ames Historical Archives, Moffett Field) remains an open lead.

## §4. Voice-modulation independent-replication search — closure

Round 3 §7 framed Sharp/Grove voice-modulation as a "single-attestation event" — described once primarily (Justesen 1975 Note 2, on Sharp/Grove's account) and thereafter cited but never independently re-described. Round 4 broadens that search across the open scientific literature.

### 4.1 Click / buzz / tone perception — broadly replicated

The microwave auditory effect (Frey effect) for **clicks, buzzes, and short tones** is well-established and has been independently replicated many times since Frey 1962. Sources:

- [Wikipedia — Microwave auditory effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect): "The microwave auditory effect, also known as the microwave hearing effect or the Frey effect, consists of the human perception of audible clicks, or even speech, induced by pulsed or modulated radio frequencies..."
- [PMC8733248 — Foster *et al.*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/) — comprehensive review of the auditory-effect literature.
- [Academia.edu (2026) — *The Microwave Auditory Effect: Historical Development, Mechanism, and Scientific Limits*](https://www.academia.edu/164797197/The_Microwave_Auditory_Effect_Historical_Development_Mechanism_and_Scientific_Limits).

### 4.2 Voice-modulated speech transmission — NOT independently replicated

Across these reviews and supporting literature, **the Sharp/Grove voice-modulation demonstration specifically has never been independently replicated in the open literature.** Only the click/buzz/tone perception has been repeated. The handful of cited demonstrations of intelligible speech remain tied to the 1973 Sharp/Grove event and downstream secondary citation chains (Justesen 1975 → Brodeur 1977 → Lin 1978 → modern reviews).

### 4.3 Critical-physics framing

The Foster / Guy line of criticism — that the **power densities required to transmit intelligible speech via the Frey-effect mechanism would produce thermal injury** — is recurrent in the review literature. Foster's framing in PMC8733248 captures the standard objection: *"power levels required for transmitting sound...[would cause] brain damage due to...thermal effects."* This places the Sharp/Grove demonstration in tension with the bioeffects mainstream: as a single-attestation event it was either (a) at unreported high power for the brief demonstration, (b) using a modulation scheme that the open literature has not characterized, or (c) overstated by Sharp/Grove and never independently checked.

The replication-search lead from round 3 §8 is therefore **closed for the open scientific literature**: no independent replication exists. Re-opening would require classified or grey-literature sources outside the present scope.

## §5. DIA and USAMERDC secondary government documents

A WhatDoTheyKnow FOI submission ([whatdotheyknow.com — REUKUS use of weapon](https://www.whatdotheyknow.com/request/amended_foi_reukus_use_of_weapon)) cites two government documents that frame microwave-speech transmission in weaponization-application terms. These are **secondary attestations** — they describe a capability adjacent to the Sharp/Grove demonstration without specifically re-describing the 1973 event — but they are documentary points worth recording.

### 5.1 DIA Communist literature review (cited via FOI submission)

A Defense Intelligence Agency review of Communist-bloc microwave-bioeffects literature is cited as containing the line:

> *"Sounds and possibly even words which appear to be originating intracranially (within the head) can be induced by signal modulation at very low average power densities."*

This is a DIA characterization of capability surveyed in foreign technical literature. It does not source-cite Sharp/Grove and is not a U.S. demonstration record; it places intracranial-speech induction within the U.S. intelligence-community knowledge picture as of the document's date (date not yet confirmed against the original DIA series).

### 5.2 USAMERDC report on microwave speech (cited via FOI submission)

An Army Mobility Equipment Research and Development Command (USAMERDC) report is cited as containing:

> *"By proper choice of pulse characteristics, intelligible speech may be created"*

with the application framing of *"camouflage, decoy, and deception operations."*

This is a direct documentary tie to the **Hawkins–USAMERDC affiliation** developed in round 2 §3 (the bridge from Sharp's WRAIR work, through Hawkins, to Fort Belvoir / USAMERDC microwave bioeffects work). Round 2 §3 had established the institutional bridge; round 4 §5.2 supplies a direct USAMERDC document that **explicitly considered weaponization applications** of microwave speech transmission. This corroborates round 2's "Hawkins bridge" hypothesis on the application side: the U.S. military's interest in microwave speech was not only laboratory bioeffects; it was at least conceptually programmed into operational-deception thinking.

### 5.3 Status of these citations

The DIA and USAMERDC quotes in §5.1 and §5.2 come from a third-party FOI submission; the **original document IDs and dates were not directly verified** in this round. They are recorded here as documentary leads — both are candidates for FOIA confirmation against the original DIA and USAMERDC series. They are also consistent with what is already established in primary form in this investigation (Hawkins / NTIA 79-19; round 2 §3-§5).
