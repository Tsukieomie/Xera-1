## §1. Summary of new findings (TL;DR)

1. **The "blood brain barrier" report cited mid-FY74 is identifiable with high confidence as Frey/Feld/Frey (1975, *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences* 247:433-438), which was presented at the February 1974 NYAS Conference on Biological Effects of Nonionizing Radiation.** The same conference proceedings volume contains the Sharp/Grove/Gandhi 1974 dosimetry paper that appears as Publication #2 of WU 057. The two teams were thus physically present at the same conference where the BBB result was circulated, mid-way through the FY74 reporting period. Independent corroboration comes from the Frey 1997 EHP commentary [PMC1533043](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1533043/) which describes the BBB-funding trajectory from this period.
2. **The WU 057 in-house BBB replication attempt referenced in the FY74 narrative ("not been replicated") is most plausibly the precursor work that became Oscar & Hawkins (1977), *Brain Research* 126:281-293.** Author T. Daryl Hawkins is the SAME person listed on Sharp's WU 057 in FY74 (Vol II, AD-A009338, line 9229 = WU 057 personnel; line 9310 and line 53646 = additional WU 057 listings — Hawkins is named four times across Vol II as a WU 057 investigator). However: by 1977 the Oscar & Hawkins paper attributes both authors to *U.S. Army Mobility Equipment R&D Command, Fort Belvoir* — NOT WRAIR. This means Hawkins moved out of WRAIR (or detailed away) between mid-1974 and 1977, and the WRAIR-internal BBB replication effort referenced in WU 057 FY74 was either subsumed into the Fort Belvoir program or terminated when Hawkins left. The paper's positive finding (microwave-induced BBB permeability change at <3 mW/cm²) is therefore not technically a WRAIR replication — it is a Hawkins-led replication carried out at a different Army installation. This nuances but does not invalidate the original Hawkins-bridge observation.
3. **The full WRAIR Annual Progress Report series for FY75-FY78 is NOT publicly available on archive.org or DTIC.** Series resumes in archive.org with FY80 (DTIC ADA108791). The four-year gap is not an obvious archival accident — adjacent fiscal years (FY73, FY74, FY80, FY81, FY82, FY83, FY84) are all present. Pre-1979 WRAIR Clinical-Investigation-only APRs exist (e.g. ADA055878 for FY77 covers Clinical Investigation Service only, not the full WRAIR research portfolio). The FY75-78 full APRs may exist as DTIC limited-distribution holdings, may have been withdrawn, or may simply not have been digitized. This is an evidentiary gap, not a closed door.
4. **The WRAIR 1981 Microwave Research Program Review (Vols I-III)** is recorded in the WRAIR Archives finding aid (Series 1, folders 112-114) at the Gorgas Memorial Library, Silver Spring, MD. There is no online digitized version. Physical access requires an on-site visit to WRAIR Archives or a FOIA/PA request through the WRAIR FOIA office.
5. **Don R. Justesen papers at KU Spencer Research Library:** Spencer's online finding-aid index does not surface a dedicated "Don R. Justesen Papers" collection in current search. Justesen's primary affiliation was the VA Medical Center / KU Medical Center (KUMC) Department of Psychiatry — not the main KU Lawrence campus — so any paper deposit is more likely to be at the KUMC Dykes Library or to remain in family / departmental custody. This is an open lead, not yet resolved.

The cumulative effect: the FY74 narrative is now embedded in a denser network of contemporaneous citations (Frey/Feld/Frey 1974 NYAS BBB; Hawkins-led 1977 *Brain Research* publication), but the official WRAIR documentary record from FY75 forward is conspicuously sparser online than would be expected.

## §2. The blood-brain-barrier report — identification

The FY74 narrative for WU 057 (AD-A009338, lines 9483-9485, p. 985-986) reads:

> "...there has been a report that signals similar to those used in the auditory study may cause changes in the blood brain barrier..."

Following standard 1973-1974 microwave-bioeffects literature, the candidates for "a report" — meaning a circulated document, not necessarily a published paper — circulating in the latter half of FY74 (Jan-Jun 1974) are:

### 2.1 Primary candidate: Frey, Feld & Frey (1975 ANYAS)

- **Citation:** Frey, A.H., Feld, S.R. & Frey, B. (1975) "Neural function and behavior: defining the relationship." *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences* 247:433-438.
- **Conference of presentation:** Conference on Biological Effects of Nonionizing Radiation, New York Academy of Sciences, February 12-15, 1974. Proceedings published 1975 as ANYAS Vol. 247, edited by P.E. Tyler.
- **Why this paper fits the narrative:**
  - The Sharp/Grove/Gandhi paper "Generation of acoustic signals by pulsed microwave energy" (IEEE T-MTT MTT-22(5):583-585, May 1974, DOI 10.1109/TMTT.1974.1128293) and its NYAS conference companion appear as Publication #2 in the WU 057 publications list (FY74 Vol II line 9573 and adjacent). Sharp, Grove and Gandhi were therefore at the Feb 1974 NYAS conference — the same conference where Frey et al. presented their BBB paper.
  - The conference timing (Feb 1974) falls squarely in the "latter half of the reporting period" (1 Jul 1973 – 30 Jun 1974) language used in the FY74 narrative.
  - The Frey 1997 EHP commentary [PMC1533043](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1533043/) explicitly identifies the Frey/Feld/Frey BBB work as the trigger for downstream DoD funding decisions on BBB research — and describes the DoD's eventual move to "effectively terminate funding for blood-brain barrier experiments that used low intensity microwave energy" after the Merritt et al. (1978) non-replication attempt.

### 2.2 Secondary / corroborating candidate: Oscar & Hawkins (precursor to 1977 *Brain Research*)

- **Citation of final paper:** Oscar, K.J. & Hawkins, T.D. (1977) "Microwave alteration of the blood-brain barrier system of rats." *Brain Research* 126(2):281-293. DOI 10.1016/0006-8993(77)90726-0. PubMed [PMID 861720](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/861720/). Affiliation on the published paper: U.S. Army Mobility Equipment R&D Command, Fort Belvoir, VA.
- **Why this matters for FY74 WU 057:** T. Daryl Hawkins is named as a WU 057 investigator in FY74 Vol II at lines 9229, 9310 (twice), and 53646. He is also named in WU 057's reference list at line 9567 ("Schrot, J. and Hawkins, T.D.: Lethal effects of 3000 MHz...") and line 54310 ("Githens, S.H., Hawkins, T.D., and Schrot, J: DRL schedule..."). This is the same person (initials, name, time period, microwave research field — all consistent). The Oscar & Hawkins (1977) paper reports a positive BBB-permeability finding at average power densities <3 mW/cm² for 1.3 GHz, single 20-min exposures — directly relevant to the FY74 audio-frequency-modulated microwave parameters used by Sharp/Grove.
- **Important caveat on the institutional bridge:** Although Hawkins is at WRAIR in FY74, by the 1977 publication date he is at Fort Belvoir's Mobility Equipment R&D Command. The "internal WRAIR replication attempt" originally hypothesised in the FY74 hunt cannot be presumed to have remained internal — it appears to have followed Hawkins to Fort Belvoir or been re-initiated there. The FY74 line "to date, not been replicated" is therefore consistent with two scenarios:
  - (a) The WRAIR-internal effort failed/was negative through end-FY74, after which Hawkins transferred to Fort Belvoir and the work was re-attempted there with a different result; or
  - (b) The attempt was always going to be carried out at Fort Belvoir under Hawkins' lead, and the FY74 narrative is reporting on a not-yet-positive collaborative effort.
- The 1977 paper's explicit thanks/acknowledgments and methodology (single 20-min exposure, 1.3 GHz, low average power density) — combined with its positive finding — make it the most likely terminal output of the work referenced in WU 057 FY74.

### 2.3 Additional corroborating reference (third-party)

- Albert, E.N. (1977). "Light and electron microscopic observations on the blood-brain barrier after microwave irradiation." Symposium on Biological Effects and Measurement of Radio Frequency Microwaves, FDA, Rockville MD, February 1977. This is an additional independent BBB study from the same era. Not directly relevant to the WU 057 FY74 internal-replication question, but it confirms that BBB-microwave research was active across multiple labs in the 1974-1977 window.

### 2.4 Documentary chain of custody

The Frey/Feld/Frey 1974 NYAS paper sits in the same conference proceedings volume (ANYAS 247, 1975) as the Sharp/Grove/Gandhi paper. Both papers are explicitly available via the New York Academy of Sciences archive. The Frey 1997 commentary [PMC1533043](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1533043/) provides the post-hoc narrative tying these papers together with the eventual DoD funding-termination decision. The Marino/Becker (Amarino website) Chapter 23 [PDF](https://andrewamarino.com/PDFs/MB/MB_Ch23.pdf) provides an additional secondary-source review citing both the Frey BBB work and Oscar & Hawkins (1977) within a single discussion of low-intensity microwave BBB effects.

## §3. The Hawkins bridge — refined analysis

The original FY74 hunt hypothesised a "Hawkins bridge" — that the same person (T. Daryl Hawkins) appears in the WU 057 FY74 personnel roster AND as co-author of the 1977 *Brain Research* BBB paper, making the latter a delayed publication of the WRAIR-internal replication referenced in WU 057.

The corrected, evidence-supported version of that hypothesis is:

- **Hawkins-as-individual: confirmed.** T. Daryl Hawkins is in WU 057 in FY74 (four explicit references) and is the second author of Oscar & Hawkins (1977). Same field, same time period, same name — same person.
- **Institutional continuity: not confirmed.** The 1977 paper's affiliation is U.S. Army Mobility Equipment R&D Command, Fort Belvoir — not WRAIR. Either Hawkins was reassigned, or the BBB work moved with him, or the BBB work always belonged operationally to Fort Belvoir with Hawkins on detail to WRAIR for WU 057's auditory dosimetry. Without the FY75/FY76/FY77 WRAIR APRs to track personnel rosters, this cannot be definitively resolved from public sources.
- **What this means for the FY74 narrative:** The "to date, not been replicated" language is consistent with a Hawkins-led replication effort that was *in progress* during the FY74 reporting period and that subsequently produced positive results (published 1977). The exact calendar of when Hawkins's group obtained their first positive result is unknown, but the existence of the 1977 *Brain Research* paper means the "not been replicated" claim — if it was meant as a permanent debunking — was incorrect by 1977. Sharp's lab may have known this in real time; the WU 057 program closure in FY74 (the work was discontinued after FY74) may have been a strategic decision rather than a scientific verdict.

This is the strongest piece of new evidence in this addendum: **the WU 057 FY74 narrative's "not been replicated" claim is in tension with the Hawkins-led 1977 *Brain Research* paper.** The two-author paper has a single sentence in its discussion that makes the implicit replication explicit (positive at <3 mW/cm² with pulsed 1.3 GHz energy — directly comparable to the parameters in Sharp/Grove WU 057's hearing-effect work).

This is a five-fiscal-year gap (FY75 through FY79) in which only Clinical Investigation Service partial APRs are publicly digitised, while the full multi-volume WRAIR APR (covering Behavioural Sciences, Microwave Research, Combat Casualty Care, Tropical Medicine, Communicable Diseases, etc.) is absent from public archives.

Possible explanations, ranked by plausibility:
1. **Limited-distribution holdings at DTIC.** The reports may exist in DTIC's collection but have not been migrated to public-release status. A FOIA request to DTIC (form available at dtic.mil) for "Walter Reed Army Institute of Research Annual Progress Report, fiscal years 1975-1979" would resolve this.
2. **Reduction-in-force documentary disruption.** WRAIR underwent a Reduction in Force in 1976 (per the WRAIR finding aid Series 1 folders 132-133). Internal report production may have been disrupted, deferred, or consolidated for some of these years.
3. **Reorganisation around the 1981 Microwave Research Program Review.** The 1981 special review (Vols I-III, in WRAIR Archives Series 1 folders 112-114) may have absorbed or replaced the per-fiscal-year APR coverage of the microwave program for several years prior, with material rolled up rather than reported annually.
4. **Active withdrawal.** Less likely but cannot be excluded — the FY75-78 reports may have been actively withdrawn from public distribution after the 1976 *60 Minutes* / Project Pandora press coverage and the 1977 Senate Committee on Human Resources hearings on covert microwave research.

The empirical bottom line is: **no public-domain WRAIR APR exists for FY75 through (most of) FY79.** This is the most significant evidentiary finding of this deeper-dive sweep. It does not prove that Sharp/Grove resumed the modulated-microwave / auditory-study work after FY74 — but it does mean that we cannot rule it in or out from the publicly available record alone. The work-unit successor to WU 057 (if one existed) is invisible.

## §5. WRAIR 1981 Microwave Research Program Review

The WRAIR Archives finding aid [Records of the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research](https://wrair.health.mil/Portals/87/Documents/WRAIR%20Finding%20Aid.pdf) records:

- Series 1 (Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, 1955-2019), folders 112-114: "Microwave Research Program Review, volume I/II/III, 1981"
- Series 1, folder 128: "Project Pandora, 1969-1970"
- Series 1, folder 132 / 133: "Reduction in Force, 1976" (two folders)
- Series 1, folder 136: "Research Programs of the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research 1976-1977, 1976"

Folder 136 is particularly interesting — it suggests an internal WRAIR-Research-Programs publication for the 1976-77 period that may partially substitute for the missing FY75/FY76 APRs.

The WRAIR Archives are at the Gorgas Memorial Library, Silver Spring, MD. There is no online digitized version of the 1981 Microwave Research Program Review. Access requires either:
- An on-site visit to the WRAIR Archives;
- A FOIA/PA request via the WRAIR FOIA office; or
- A research-services request to WRAIR Archives staff.

This is a known-good lead with a known-good location. Its full content is not yet captured in the present investigation.

## §6. Justesen archival lead

The targeted search for Don R. Justesen papers at the Kenneth Spencer Research Library (KU Lawrence) produced no dedicated finding aid hit. Justesen's institutional affiliations during the 1970s were primarily:
- Veterans Administration Medical Center, Kansas City, MO (Behavioral Radiology Laboratory)
- KU Medical Center (KUMC), Department of Psychiatry, Kansas City, KS

The paper trail for Justesen's bioeffects research from the 1973-1980 window — including his 1975 "microwave-induced auditory effect" *American Psychologist* paper (DOI 10.1037/0003-066X.30.3.391) and his correspondence with WRAIR/Sharp's group — would more likely be held at:
- KUMC Archie R. Dykes Health Sciences Library, special collections; or
- The VA Medical Center's research records (which would have been transferred to NARA on retirement); or
- Family / departmental custody if the papers were never formally accessioned.

This lead remains open. A direct enquiry to the Dykes Library reference desk would be the next concrete step.

## §7. Updated assessment of parent-file conclusions

The parent file `FY74_SPEECH_DEMO_HUNT.md` §7-§8 reached two conclusions:

> **Parent §7:** "WU 057 in FY74 explicitly references prior 'audio-frequency-modulated microwave signal' work, asserts that no further investigation is in progress, and references an unspecified BBB report."
>
> **Parent §8:** "The WU 057 FY74 narrative is consistent with — but does not itself prove — a WRAIR-internal replication attempt of the BBB work that did not produce a positive result by mid-1974."

These conclusions are now updated as follows:

### Revised §7
WU 057 in FY74 explicitly references prior audio-frequency-modulated microwave work (the Sharp/Grove "speech demonstration" and related auditory studies) as a prior phase. The narrative also references "a report that signals similar to those used in the auditory study may cause changes in the blood brain barrier" — and that report is now identifiable with high confidence as the Frey/Feld/Frey paper presented at the February 1974 NYAS Conference (published 1975 as ANYAS 247:433-438). The Sharp/Grove/Gandhi 1974 paper (also in ANYAS 247) places the WU 057 personnel at the same conference. The "report" was not an obscure unpublished communication — it was a peer-reviewed conference paper presented in the same room that Sharp/Grove/Gandhi attended.

### Revised §8
The WU 057 FY74 narrative is consistent with a Hawkins-led BBB replication attempt that did not yet produce a positive result by mid-1974. By 1977, however, Hawkins (now at Fort Belvoir) had co-authored a positive BBB paper at parameters (1.3 GHz, <3 mW/cm², 20-min exposure) directly relevant to the auditory-study microwave parameters. The "to date, not been replicated" claim in the FY74 narrative was therefore — at minimum — premature, and at worst inconsistent with what Hawkins's own group published three years later. Whether this discrepancy reflects scientific evolution, institutional politics (the WU 057 work was discontinued after FY74), or the WRAIR-vs-Fort-Belvoir institutional split cannot be determined from the public record alone.

### New §9: The FY75-FY79 documentary gap
The full WRAIR Annual Progress Report for fiscal years 1975 through 1979 is not publicly available via DTIC, archive.org, or NLM open access. The series resumes in FY80 (ADA108791). This means any successor to WU 057 — whether continuation, replacement, or termination of the "Modulated microwave signals" work — cannot be tracked through the public APR record. Resolution requires either a DTIC FOIA request for the FY75-78 APRs or on-site research at the WRAIR Archives.

### New §10: The Hawkins-Fort Belvoir cross-link
The Hawkins-bridge is real but operates across institutions, not within WRAIR. This complicates but does not destroy the original observation. A focused secondary investigation of T. Daryl Hawkins's career — Officer Records, USAMERDC personnel records, transferred publications — would resolve the timing of his move from WRAIR to Fort Belvoir. This is a discrete, achievable next step.
