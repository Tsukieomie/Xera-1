## §1. Sharp's post-WRAIR institutional move — RESOLVED (and reverses round-1 hypothesis)

Round 1 stated: "Sharp moved to NIH in the late 1970s." This is **incorrect** and is hereby retracted.

The [Joseph C. Sharp Wikipedia biography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_C._Sharp) (sourced from his 2021 obituary) records:

> "Joseph Cecil Sharp II (May 30, 1934 — January 13, 2021) was an American psychologist. ... In 1961, he began his career as a research psychologist at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research where, in 1970, he was appointed deputy director of neuropsychiatry. He also served as Chief of the Department of Experimental Psychology and Behavioral Radiology at Walter Reed, and in 1969 and 1970, was Deputy Commissioner of Public Health for the State of New York. **He later moved to California and worked at NASA's Ames Research Center from 1974 to 1994.** For the last five of those years, he was the director of the Office of Space Research. After retiring from Ames, he began teaching at Southern Utah University's College of Science in Cedar City, Utah."

### Implications

- **Sharp moved to NASA Ames in 1974, not NIH in the late 1970s.** This narrows the WRAIR exit window to 1973-early 1974, consistent with Justesen's March 1975 article calling him "former director of research in experimental psychology at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research."
- The 28 September 1973 personal-communication date for the speech demonstration (established in round 2 §1) therefore falls during what was already Sharp's **last few months** at WRAIR before his NASA Ames move.
- The speech demonstration was **not** part of an ongoing program of Sharp's that continued at WRAIR after 1973-74; Sharp himself was leaving. This reframes WU 057's continuation: after Sharp's departure, the auditory-effects line at WRAIR was carried on by **Mark Grove** (still listed in 1974 IEEE T-MTT paper) and the microwave-bioeffects program transitioned toward the BBB-oriented Oscar/Hawkins/Hunt work documented in NTIA 79-19 (round 2 §3).
- Sharp's NASA Ames career (1974-1994, finishing as Director of the Office of Space Research) means he had a substantial public scientific record after leaving WRAIR — but in space life sciences, **not** in microwave-bioeffects research. He did not continue publishing on microwave hearing.

### Caveat — third-party 1968 claim
A secondary Facebook source (60 Minutes-related social post, Feb 2026) asserted "In 1968, Joseph Sharp, the lead Pandora researcher at Walter Reed, left the program." This is **inconsistent** with both:
- Justesen's 28 Sept 1973 personal-communication citation, which describes Sharp performing the demonstration himself and would be impossible if Sharp had left in 1968; and
- Wikipedia's biography placing Sharp at WRAIR through 1974.

The "1968 Pandora departure" claim is therefore rejected as inaccurate or as referring to a different programmatic boundary (e.g., Pandora itself was wound down in March 1970 per the [DARPA letter to Magnuson](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/sites/default/files/documents/rhwvuj-tzy4b/14.pdf), which may have been confused with Sharp's personal timeline).

## §2. Justesen's 1979 retrospective — DROPPED the speech demonstration

In December 1979, Don R. Justesen published a follow-on review article in the *Bulletin of the New York Academy of Medicine*:

- **Title:** "Behavioral and psychological effects of microwave radiation"
- **Citation:** *Bull N Y Acad Med.* 1979 Dec;55(11):1058-78. [PMID 120208 / PMCID PMC1807730](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/120208/)
- **Author affiliation:** Director, Neuropsychology and Behavioral Radiology Laboratories, Veterans Administration Medical Center, Kansas City, Missouri (same affiliation as Justesen 1975)
- **PDF:** [zoryglaser.com — BEHAVIORAL-AND-PSYCHOLOGICAL-EFFECTS-OF-MICROWAVE-RADIATION.pdf](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/BEHAVIORAL-AND-PSYCHOLOGICAL-EFFECTS-OF-MICROWAVE-RADIATION.pdf)

**The 1979 paper does not mention Sharp, Grove, "voice-modulated microwaves," or the speech demonstration.** Sections on RF hearing (pp. 1061-1062) cover the same biophysical territory as the 1975 paper — pulsed-microwave auditory perception, thermoelastic mechanism, Frey 1961/1965/1973 — but the entire paragraph that anchored the speech-demonstration claim in the 1975 *American Psychologist* article has been **removed** from the 1979 NYAM treatment.

### Why this matters

The 1975 *American Psychologist* article was Justesen's first authoritative public account of Sharp/Grove. Round-2 §1 confirmed it cited the demonstration as "Sharp, J. C., & Grove, M. Personal communication, September 28, 1973." Four years later — with the 1977 Senate hearings on the public record (round 2 §5), with Brodeur's [*Zapping of America*](https://archive.org/details/zappingofamerica00brod) (1977) having popularized the demonstration, and with Lin's [*Microwave Auditory Effects and Applications*](https://catalog.nlm.nih.gov/discovery/fulldisplay?vid=01NLM_INST%3A01NLM_INST&search_scope=MyInstitution&tab=LibraryCatalog&docid=alma994914563406676) (Charles C. Thomas, 1978) having entered the academic literature with the same Sharp/Grove citation — Justesen had every opportunity to expand or update his treatment. Instead, he silently dropped it.

This is **not** dispositive (a 20-page review necessarily omits much), but it is anomalous given the four-year evolution of the topic's prominence. Possible explanations include:

1. **Editorial choice** — the 1979 paper was a more general behavioral review and the speech demonstration may have been judged outside its scope.
2. **Source-protection courtesy** — Sharp had left WRAIR for NASA Ames; a personal-communication citation at a current Veterans Administration affiliation may have been re-evaluated.
3. **Reduced confidence** — the demonstration was never replicated in a published, instrumented protocol after 1973-74 by anyone other than Justesen's narrative; over four years the absence of independent replication may have reduced Justesen's willingness to repeat the secondhand account.
4. **Classification posture** — by 1979, post-Senate-hearings DoD posture on microwave-bioeffects had hardened, and a Veterans Administration researcher writing a wide-readership NYAM review may have steered around the most operationally suggestive material.

None of these explanations is conclusive, but **the disappearance of Sharp/Grove from Justesen's 1979 retrospective is itself a documentary fact** worth recording in this investigation. The 1975 *American Psychologist* article remains the primary unclassified published account of the demonstration; Justesen never returned to it in print.

## §3. Brodeur's *Zapping of America* (1977) — Sharp on the record about Pandora

Paul Brodeur's [*The Zapping of America: Microwaves, Their Deadly Risk, and the Cover-Up*](https://archive.org/details/zappingofamerica00brod) (W.W. Norton, 1977) — partially serialized in *The New Yorker* in 1976 — quotes Sharp directly. The book's chapter sequence includes "Project Pandora Revisited" (Ch. 9), "The Mind-Control Connection" (Ch. 24), and "The Zapping of America" (Ch. 25).

Per the [Scribd index](https://www.scribd.com/document/619626962/Paul-Brodeur-The-Zapping-of-America-Microwaves-Their-Deadly-Risk-And-the-Coverup) and a [CIA reading-room copy of an early draft](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/cia-rdp88b01125r000300120111-8) of the same material:

> "Joseph C. Sharp, who had worked on Project Pandora when he was chief of experimental psychology at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research..."

The book identifies Sharp as a participant in Project Pandora — a directly stated linkage that round 1 had to triangulate from circumstantial evidence (DARPA letter, NTIA 79-19, Hawkins bridge). Brodeur's *Zapping of America* therefore serves as the **second** open-source primary attestation linking Sharp to Pandora-era WRAIR work, alongside Justesen 1975.

The Internet Archive's `archive.org/details/zappingofamerica00brod` holds the book; the OCR text file is rate-limited to authenticated sessions, but the chapter index and Sharp citation are accessible from the catalog page and from third-party redistributions.

## §4. Lin's 1978 *Microwave Auditory Effects and Applications* — Sharp/Grove canonical citation

James C. Lin's foundational monograph:

- **Title:** *Microwave Auditory Effects and Applications*
- **Publisher:** Charles C. Thomas, Springfield, Illinois
- **Year:** 1978
- **NLM catalog record:** [alma994914563406676](https://catalog.nlm.nih.gov/discovery/fulldisplay?vid=01NLM_INST%3A01NLM_INST&search_scope=MyInstitution&tab=LibraryCatalog&docid=alma994914563406676)

Per third-party citations (e.g., the [WhatDoTheyKnow FOI submission](https://www.whatdotheyknow.com/request/amended_foi_reukus_use_of_weapon)):

> "While working for the Advanced Research Projects Agency at Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, Sharp and Grove discovered 'receiverless' and 'wireless' voice transmission. ... The discovery's applications are 'obviously not limited to therapeutic medicine' according to James C. Lin in *Microwave Auditory Effects and Applications*."

Lin (then at the University of Illinois at Chicago) therefore canonized the Sharp/Grove demonstration in the principal academic monograph on the subject within four years of the event. Lin's citation pattern matches Justesen 1975 — i.e., Lin too treated the demonstration as a real but secondhand-attested event worth describing in a textbook.

A [later Lin volume](https://searchworks.stanford.edu/view/14019007), *Auditory Effects of Microwave Radiation* (more recent), reprises the same material. Both volumes establish Sharp/Grove as a canonical citation in microwave-bioeffects literature, anchored ultimately in Justesen 1975's personal-communication footnote.

## §5. Mark Grove — career trail

Mark Grove (sometimes "M. Grove" or "H. M. Grove" in third-party social-media references — the latter is unverified and may be a confusion with another Grove) appears in:

- **Sharp/Grove/Gandhi (1974)** *IEEE Transactions on Microwave Theory and Techniques* 22(5):583-585 — co-author with Sharp and Om P. Gandhi
- **Justesen (1975)** Note 2 personal communication — co-source with Sharp
- **WRAIR FY74 APR (AD-A009337/AD-A009338)** — co-investigator on WU 057 "Modulated microwave signals"

Grove is identified by Justesen as **"an electronic engineer"** — i.e., the engineering counterpart to Sharp's psychology background. Beyond 1974-75, Grove does not appear in the FY77 NTIA 79-19 WRAIR project listings (which name Oscar, Hawkins, Hunt, Schrot, but not Grove). No further open-source publication record was located in this round under the name Mark Grove or M. Grove with WRAIR or microwave-bioeffects affiliation through the early 1980s.

The most parsimonious interpretation: **Grove was an engineering staff member rather than a principal investigator**, which would explain his disappearance from the published record after the 1974-75 window — staff engineers typically do not maintain independent publication trails comparable to PIs. His role in the speech demonstration was likely instrumentation construction (the pulse-triggering sine-wave-zero-crossing detector described in Justesen 1975).

## §7. Implications for the speech-demonstration question

The round-3 findings consolidate the picture established in rounds 1-2:

1. The Sharp/Grove demonstration was a **real event with a specific date** (28 Sept 1973), occurring at WRAIR's Forest Glen Annex during Sharp's final months there.
2. The demonstration was **described publicly only via Justesen's 1975 personal-communication citation** and was canonized in Lin 1978 and Brodeur 1977; **no other principal involved (Sharp, Grove, Gandhi, Hawkins, Oscar, Hunt) ever published a primary-source technical-report account of it.**
3. Sharp's move to NASA Ames in 1974 means the demonstration **had no continuation at WRAIR under its original investigator** — the auditory-pulse research at WRAIR transitioned toward BBB-modulation work (Oscar, Hawkins, Hunt, 1300 MHz, FY77 NTIA listing) without any documented follow-on speech-modulation work.
4. Justesen's silent **omission of the demonstration from his 1979 NYAM retrospective** is not dispositive but is a notable documentary fact, suggesting that even within Justesen's own publication record the 1975 description was not subsequently corroborated or re-described.
5. The Sharp/Grove demonstration thus stands in the open-source record as a **single-attestation event** — described once primarily (Justesen 1975, on Sharp/Grove's own account) and thereafter cited but never re-replicated or independently described.

This is the strongest synthesis the open-source investigation can support without access to (a) Justesen's KU Medical Center research files (round-2 §8 item 5 — still the highest-value remaining lead), (b) WRAIR institutional records for FY75-FY79 not in DTIC, (c) Sharp's own NASA Ames or post-retirement personal papers (location unknown), (d) the 1977 Senate hearings Serial 95-49 full transcript.
