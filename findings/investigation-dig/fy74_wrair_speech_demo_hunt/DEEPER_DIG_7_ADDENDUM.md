## 1. URSI 1975 Annual Meeting — Hawkins at WRAIR Microwave Research, verbatim

The **1975 USNC/URSI Annual Meeting** was held at the University of Colorado, Boulder, **20–23 October 1975**. The full program (340 pages) is hosted by the National Academies at [pga_183521.pdf](https://sites.nationalacademies.org/cs/groups/pgasite/documents/webpage/pga_183521.pdf). The Commission I (Biological Effects) sessions list **four Hawkins-co-authored papers, all from the WRAIR Department of Microwave Research**, plus a fifth WRAIR-supported acknowledgment in a University of Utah paper.

The author index entry (p. 327) reads:

> *"Hawkins, T.D.    222, 223, 232, 233"*

Verbatim session listings (pages numbered as printed):

### 1.1. B8a-1 — Oscar & Hawkins BBB paper, two years before *Brain Research* publication

> **"B8a-1 ELECTROMAGNETIC RADIATION EFFECTS ON THE BLOOD-BRAIN BARRIER SYSTEM OF RATS: Kenneth J. Oscar, U.S. Army Mobility Equipment R & D Center, Fort Belvoir, Virginia, and American University, Washington, D.C., and T. Daryl Hawkins, Department of Microwave Research, Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, Washington, D.C."**
>
> *"A study was conducted to assess the effect of microwave energy on the blood-brain barrier (BBB) system of rats. A quantitative radioactive isotope method was used in an attempt to verify the increased BBB permeability of rats reported by A.H. Frey, who used a flourescien dye technique. Male, Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, WISTAR derived, 230-270 gram rats were subjected to microwave energy at a frequency of 1.3 GHz. The permeability measurement, after the method of W. H. Oldendorf, uses two radioactive labeled indicators which are injected simultaneously into a common carotid artery of the anesthetized rat. One test substance is labeled with ¹⁴C and the other with tritiated water which is highly diffusible in the brain tissue and provides a standard for comparison. The rats are sacrificed 15 sec after injection and tissue samples from 5 different regions of the brain counted. … The results, to date, indicate a statistically significant increase in BBB permeability with both pulsed and CW microwave energy at average power levels considerably below 10 mW/cm²."* — Wednesday 22 October 1975, 1330, URSI Session B-8a (CNS Effects-I), UMC East Ballroom, Chairman R. Adey, UCLA. Page 222.

This is **two years prior** to the *Brain Research* 126:281-293 paper of round 6 §2 — the BBB study existed in oral / conference form well before journal publication, and the affiliation pattern is **Oscar at USAMERDC + Hawkins at WRAIR Microwave Research** as joint authors. It also names **A.H. Frey** as the explicit prior worker the Oscar–Hawkins study was attempting to verify (the Frey & Feld 1975 J Comp Physiol Psychol paper of round 6 §3 is the same Frey lineage). The Wistar rats are explicitly **Walter Reed Army Institute of Research-derived**, even though Oscar's USAMERDC affiliation appears first.

### 1.2. B8a-2 — Hawkins & Hunt audiogenic-seizure paper

> **"B8a-2 REDUCTION IN SENSITIVITY TO AUDIOGENIC SEIZURE FOLLOWING A SINGLE, 2450 MHz, CW IRRADIATION OF RATS: T. Daryl Hawkins and Edward L. Hunt, Department of Microwave Research, Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, Washington, D.C."** — same B-8a session, Wed 22 Oct 1975 1330. Page 222–223.

Edward L. Hunt is one of the most-published Walter Reed microwave-bioeffects researchers (he chaired URSI Session B-6b at the same meeting — page 194 — *"Chairman: E. Hunt, Walter Reed Army Institute of Research"*). ### 1.3. B9b-1 — Githens, Hawkins & Schrot colonic-temperature paper

> **"B9b-1 COLONIC TEMPERATURE CHANGES DURING MICROWAVE EXPOSURE: Sandra H. Githens, T. Daryl Hawkins, and John Schrot, Department of Microwave Research, Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, Washington, D.C."** — Wed 22 Oct 1975, 1600, URSI Session B-9b (Polarization Effects), UMC West Ballroom. Page 232.

Three more WRAIR Microwave Research staff: **Sandra H. Githens** and **John Schrot** appear together with Hawkins. They studied colonic temperature increases during plane-wave exposures at 100 mW/cm² across 710 / 1700 / 2450 MHz in mice, small rats, and large rats. The paper explicitly correlates differential frequency effects with body size — the dosimetry-validation work that would later support BBB experiments.

### 1.4. B9b-2 — Schrot & Hawkins frequency-orientation interaction paper

> **"B9b-2 MICROWAVE FREQUENCY AND E-FIELD ORIENTATION INTERACT WITH ANIMAL SIZE: John Schrot and T. Daryl Hawkins, Department of Microwave Research, Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, Washington, D.C."** — same B-9b session, Wed 22 Oct 1975, 1600. Page 232–233.

Convulsion-time-to-tonic-clonic-seizure as the dependent variable across 710 / 985 / 1700 / 2450 / 3000 MHz in two E-field orientations and three rat sizes — at **150 mW/cm² CW** in a 16′×16′×32′ anechoic chamber. This is straight WRAIR high-intensity dosimetry work.

## 2. Oscar–Hawkins follow-on: Oscar–Gruenau–Folker–Rapoport 1981 *Brain Research* — USAMERDC → NIH/NIMH publication lineage

A previously-unsurfaced follow-on paper by Oscar runs the BBB-and-blood-flow line forward from Hawkins+WRAIR (1977) into NIH/NIMH (1981):

> **K.J. Oscar, S.P. Gruenau, M.T. Folker, and S.I. Rapoport (1981), "Local cerebral blood flow after microwave exposure," *Brain Research* 204(1):220–225.** [PubMed PMID 7248750](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7248750/). DOI 10.1016/0006-8993(81)90412-X.
>
> Abstract (verbatim, PubMed): *"Experiments were performed which demonstrate that low power, pulsed microwave exposure induces a significant increase in local cerebral blood flow within the brain of the conscious rat. Previous studies purporting to examine changes in brain vascular permeability induced by microwave radiation must be re-evaluated in the light of these observed changes in blood flow."*

The fourth author, **Stanley I. Rapoport**, is the long-time chief of the **Laboratory of Neurosciences, National Institute on Aging (NIA), NIH** — the principal NIH BBB methodologist of the 1970s–80s (his Oldendorf-method extensions are referenced in the 1977 *Brain Research* abstract). Oscar's June 1980 USAMERDC report **AD-A090426** of round 5 §2 — *"Effects of low power microwaves on the local cerebral blood flow of conscious rats"* — and the May 1981 *Brain Research* paper share the same title scope and exposure protocol; the 1981 paper is the **peer-reviewed publication of the AD-A090426 USAMERDC technical-report study**. The author list adds three NIH/NIMH collaborators (Gruenau, Folker, Rapoport) to Oscar.

This **closes the USAMERDC → NIH publication lineage** for the Oscar BBB / blood-flow line: the 1980 USAMERDC technical report containing the *"by proper choice of pulse characteristics, intelligible speech may be created … camouflage, decoy, and deception operations"* rationale (round 5 §2) is the same study published in 1981 with a USAMERDC-NIH author block, in *Brain Research* — the same journal that ran Oscar/Hawkins 1977. The "intelligible speech may be created" language **does not appear** in the 1981 *Brain Research* paper or its abstract, only in the AD-A090426 USAMERDC report — i.e., the operational-deception framing was carried in the technical-report version distributed to government readers, not in the open-literature peer-reviewed version.

A third Oscar paper in the lineage — **Oscar 1981 (single-author or different co-authors), "Local cerebral blood flow after rapid entry into hyperbaric air," *Aviation, Space, and Environmental Medicine*** — is referenced in the IEEE Std and Chou/Adair reviews; this falls outside the microwave/BBB axis but indicates Oscar's continued NIH-adjacent CBF work.

The **Oscar–Hawkins BBB → Oscar–Rapoport CBF** publication arc therefore runs:

| 1975 (Oct) | URSI Session B-8a-1 (oral) | Oscar, Hawkins | USAMERDC + WRAIR |

The 1980 USAMERDC report's *"intelligible speech may be created … camouflage, decoy, and deception operations"* rationale is therefore embedded inside a peer-reviewed publication arc that ran *through* — not adjacent to — Hawkins's WRAIR group from 1975 to 1977 and through Rapoport's NIH BBB lab into 1981. The 1973 Sharp/Grove WRAIR demonstration's documentary penumbra now reaches NIH/NIA in 1981.

## 3. Oscar/Hawkins 1977 *Brain Research* — full PubMed abstract verbatim

Round 6 §2 located the paper. Round 7 retrieves the **full PubMed abstract** verbatim ([PubMed 861720](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/861720/)):

> *"Rats were exposed to 1.3 gHz microwave energy to assess the uptake of several neutral polar substances in certain areas of the brain. A quantitative, radioactive isotope method, which uses an internal standard, was employed to measure the loss of test substances to brain tissue. Single, 20 min exposure, to either pulsed or continuous wave (CW) microwave energy induced an increase in the uptake of D-mannitol at average power densities of less than 3.0 mW/sp. cm. The permeability change was greatest in the medulla, followed, in decreasing order, by the cerebellum and hypothalamus, with small or negligible changes in the hippocampus and cortex. Permeability increases were observed for mannitol and inulin but not for dextran. Increased permeability was observed both immediately and 4 h after exposure, but not 24 h after exposure. After an initial rise, the permeability of cerebral vessels to saccharides decreased with increasing microwave power. Differences in the level of uptake occurred between CW energy and pulsed energy of the same average power. Microwaves of the same average power but different pulse characteristics also produced different uptake levels. Our findings suggest that microwaves induce a temporary change in the permeability for small molecular weight saccharides in the blood-brain barrier system of rats."*

The published 1977 abstract preserves the **pulsed-vs-CW differential** that the URSI 1975 oral version reported, and adds the **immediate / 4-h / 24-h time-course** data. The conclusion threshold of **average power densities less than 3.0 mW/cm²** is a key dosimetric anchor used by every subsequent BBB review (D'Andrea 2003, EPA 1984 [600/8-83-026F](https://nepis.epa.gov/Exe/ZyPURL.cgi?Dockey=940023K0.TXT), Chou 1987). The full text of the paper (10.1016/0006-8993(77)90726-0) remains paywalled at ScienceDirect and was not retrieved verbatim in round 7; the abstract above is the most complete open-source rendering.

The Frey 1998 commentary referenced in round 6 §2 ties the Oscar/Hawkins BBB result back to Frey's auditory-effect lineage; the URSI 1975 oral version of round 7 §1.1 makes that linkage explicit by naming Frey as the **prior fluorescein-dye worker** the Oscar-Hawkins quantitative isotope method was attempting to verify.

## 5. Lin 1978 *Microwave Auditory Effects and Applications* — "obviously not limited to therapeutic medicine"

Per McMurtrey's citation [20] (Scribd 561568706), **James C. Lin's 1978 textbook *Microwave Auditory Effects and Applications* (Charles C. Thomas, Springfield IL)** contains the line:

> *"the applications of [the microwave auditory effect] are obviously not limited to therapeutic medicine."*

Round 3 §3 had located the Brodeur Pandora-quote portion of the Lin lineage; round 7 fixes the canonical quote that signals Lin's awareness of the non-medical (i.e., **military / signal**) applications already published-textbook-public by 1978. Lin was at the Department of Bioengineering, University of Illinois Chicago at the time and is the same Lin in the Oscar/Lin 1983 review on microwave-induced pressure waves in mammalian brains (*IEEE Trans BME-30(5):289-294*) cited in the D'Andrea 2003 review reference list. The "obviously not limited to therapeutic medicine" framing pairs with Justesen 1975 Note 2 and Brodeur 1977 Ch 24 to constitute the **three contemporaneous open-literature attestations** that the 1973 WRAIR voice-modulation demonstration was understood by working microwave-bioeffects scientists in the 1975–78 window as having explicit non-medical applications.
