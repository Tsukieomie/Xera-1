### 1.3 Section II Part 10 — "Internal Sound Perception" (page 15) — verbatim

> *"(U) Perception of modulated microwave signals which seem to be originating intracranially as characteristic sounds is a phenomenon which was first reported in the US open literature more than thirteen years ago. To produce sounds, peak power densities of up to 850 mW/cm² may be required, but the average power density usually is 5 μW/cm². The Soviets have studied this phenomenon in order to determine the underlying physiological mechanism(s) and to define the optimum irradiation parameters needed to evoke the response. They found that when the fundamental frequency of the electromagnetic stimulus was raised from 2050 to 2500 MHz, the reaction threshold rose significantly, but at a frequency of 3000 MHz there was no reaction in the auditory centers. The average intensity of electromagnetic radiation required to evoke the response was less than 10 mW/cm²; it was concluded that the fundamental signal frequency rather than the amount of energy constituted the primary stimulus and that the observed phenomenon was sensory in nature.*

> *(U) The Soviets will continue to investigate the nature of internal sound perception. Their research will include studies on perceptual distortion and other psychophysiological effects. The results of these investigations could have military applications if the Soviets develop methods for disrupting or disturbing human behavior."*

The "first reported in the US open literature more than thirteen years ago" gives a date marker of 1962-1963, matching Frey 1962 *J. Appl. Physiol.* — the reference conventionally credited as the U.S. priority claim.

A round-5 numerical correction: the round-5 §1 transcription had *"peak power densities of up to 80 mW/cm²"* — the actual primary-source value is **850 mW/cm²**. The correct figure is now in hand and round 5 §1 is superseded on this number.

### 1.4 Section VI — "Trends, Conclusions, and Forecast" (page 25) — verbatim weaponization-relevant passage

> *"No Eurasian Communist research activity has been identified which can be clearly or directly related to any military offensive weapons program. However, Soviet scientists are fully aware of the biological effects of low-level microwave radiation which might have anti-personnel weapons application. Their internal sound perception research has great potential for development into a system for disorienting or disrupting the behavior patterns of military or diplomatic personnel; it could be used equally well as an interrogation tool. The Soviets have also studied the psychophysiological and metabolic changes and the alterations of brain function resulting from exposure to mixed frequencies of electromagnetic radiation. One physiological effect which has been demonstrated is heart seizure. This has been accomplished experimentally in frogs by synchronizing a pulsed ultrahigh frequency microwave signal of low average-power density with the depolarization of the myocardium and beaming the signal at the thoracic area. A frequency probably could be found which would provide sufficient penetration of the chest wall of humans to accomplish the same effect. Another possibility is alteration of the permeability of the blood-brain barrier. This could allow neurotoxins in the blood to cross. As a result, an individual could develop severe neuropathological symptoms and either die or become seriously impaired neurologically.*

> *The above study is recommended reading material for those who have an interest in the application of microwave energy to weapons. A discussion of weapons is not within the scope of this study."*

## §2. Hawkins–Oscar 1977 Brain Research paper — the WRAIR-USAMERDC bridge made directly documentary

This is the **most important new finding of any round so far.** The round-5 USAMERDC report (Oscar 1980, AD-A090426) had a precursor — published in the open peer-reviewed literature — that places **Hawkins** as a **named co-author** with Oscar three years earlier. This converts the round-2 §3 "Hawkins-bridge speculation" into a directly documentary finding.

### 2.1 Citation

- **Title:** *Microwave alteration of the blood-brain barrier system of rats*
- **Authors:** **K. J. Oscar, T. D. Hawkins**
- **Journal:** *Brain Research*, Volume 126, Issue 2, 6 May 1977, pages 281-293
- **DOI:** 10.1016/0006-8993(77)90726-0
- **PubMed PMID:** 861720
- **Source verified at:** [PubMed PMID 861720](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/861720/); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0006899377907260); [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Microwave-alteration-of-the-blood-brain-barrier-of-Oscar-Oscar/0585a9bc5aa70865caabe5b135054b256d3f253d). Cited verbatim by Frey 1998 commentary in [PMC1533043](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1533043/pdf/envhper00526-0027.pdf?billing_country=US): *"Oscar KL [sic — actually KJ], Hawkins TD. Microwave alteration of the blood-brain barrier system of rats. Brain Res 126:281-293 (1977)."*

### 2.2 Verbatim PubMed abstract

> *"Rats were exposed to 1.3 gHz microwave energy to assess the uptake of several neutral polar substances in certain areas of the brain. A quantitative, radioactive isotope method, which uses an internal standard, was employed to measure the loss of test substances to brain tissue. Single, 20 min exposure, to either pulsed or continuous wave (CW) microwave energy induced an increase in the uptake of D-mannitol at average power densities of less than 3.0 mW/sp. cm. The permeability change was greatest in the medulla, followed, in decreasing order, by the cerebellum and hypothalamus, with small or negligible changes in the hippocampus and cortex. Permeability increases were observed for mannitol and inulin but not for dextran. Increased permeability was observed both immediately and 4 h after exposure, but not 24 h after exposure. After an initial rise, the permeability of cerebral vessels to saccharides decreased with increasing microwave power. Differences in the level of uptake occurred between CW energy and pulsed energy of the same average power. Microwaves of the same average power but different pulse characteristics also produced different uptake levels. Our findings suggest that microwaves induce a temporary change in the permeability for small molecular weight saccharides in the blood-brain barrier system of rats."*

This 1977 paper is the **single most important piece of documentary evidence** developed across rounds 1-6 for the round-2 §3 Hawkins-USAMERDC bridge hypothesis. The chain is now:

The Hawkins co-authorship in 1977 *Brain Research* — three years before Oscar 1980 — establishes that **the same Hawkins identified at NTIA 79-19 as the Microwave Bioeffects Branch chief was actively co-publishing with Oscar on the precursor BBB studies that the 1980 USAMERDC speech-rationale report extended.** This is no longer a "Hawkins bridge speculation" (round 2's framing) — it is a directly documented author collaboration in the open peer-reviewed literature.

### 2.4 Frey 1998 commentary — the canonization

In [Frey 1998 *Environmental Health Perspectives* 106(3):101-103](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1533043/pdf/envhper00526-0027.pdf?billing_country=US), Allan Frey himself characterizes Oscar & Hawkins as the *extension* of his own work:

It was also found that microwave energy exposure of the same average power but with different pulse characteristics produced different uptake levels."*

Reference 15: *"Oscar KL [sic], Hawkins TD. Microwave alteration of the blood-brain barrier system of rats. Brain Res 126:281-293 (1977)."*

Frey himself — the founder of the auditory-effect line of research — places Oscar & Hawkins squarely in the BBB-extension lineage. This is a third independent attestation (after PubMed and ScienceDirect) of the Oscar–Hawkins 1977 paper, by a primary-source figure in the field.

## §3. Frey & Feld 1975 — verified, corrected (journal correction)

The round-1 carry-forward "Frey/Feld/Frey 1975 ANYAS 247:433-439" was a journal-misidentification. The actual paper is:

- **Title:** *Avoidance by Rats of Illumination with Low Power Nonionizing Electromagnetic Energy*
- **Authors:** Allan H. Frey, Sondra R. Feld
- **Journal:** *Journal of Comparative and Physiological Psychology*, Vol. 89, No. 2, pp. 183-188, **April 1975**
- **DOI:** 10.1037/h0076662
- **PubMed PMID:** 1133237
- **Source verified at:** [PubMed PMID 1133237](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1133237/); full-text PDF at [cellphonetaskforce.org](https://cellphonetaskforce.org/files/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/avoidance-by-rats-of-illumination-with-low-power-nonionizing-electromagnetic-energy.pdf) (fetch returned bad_robots in this round; PubMed abstract sufficient).

### 3.1 Verbatim abstract

> *"Rats spent more time in the halves of shuttle boxes that were shielded from illumination by 1.2 GHz microwave energy than in the unshielded. In Experiment 1, rats avoided the energy when it was presented as 30-musec pulses with a pulse repetition rate of 100 pulses per second (pps). The average power density was about .6 mW/cm², and the peak power density was about 200 mW/cm². In Experiment 2, the energy was presented both continuously and in pulse-modulated form, i.e., .5-msec exponentially decaying pulses at a rate of 1,000 pps. The average power density of the continuous energy was 2.4 mW/cm², and the average power density of the pulse-modulated energy was .2 mW/cm². The peak power density of the modulated energy was 2.1 mW/cm². The rats avoided the pulsed energy, but not the continuous energy."*

### 3.2 BBB-parameter relevance

The round-1 carry-forward "BBB parameters" tag is somewhat off-target: this 1975 Frey/Feld paper is a **rat-avoidance behavioral study**, not directly a BBB-permeability study. It establishes that **pulsed (but not CW) microwave energy at low average power densities is aversive to rats** — providing behavioral correlate evidence relevant to (a) Frey-effect perception thresholds at 1.2 GHz and (b) the foundation upon which Oscar & Hawkins 1977 (1.3 GHz, BBB permeability — round 6 §2) extended.

The numerical parameters now in hand:

| Frey & Feld 1975 Experiment 1 | Value |
|---|---|
| Carrier frequency | 1.2 GHz |
| Pulse width | 30 μsec |
| Pulse repetition rate | 100 pps |
| Average power density | ~0.6 mW/cm² |
| Peak power density | ~200 mW/cm² |

| Frey & Feld 1975 Experiment 2 | Continuous | Pulse-modulated |
|---|---|---|
| Pulse width | n/a (CW) | 0.5 msec exp. decay |
| Pulse repetition rate | n/a | 1,000 pps |
| Average power density | 2.4 mW/cm² | 0.2 mW/cm² |
| Peak power density | n/a | 2.1 mW/cm² |
| Rat avoidance? | No | Yes |

These parameters bracket the Sharp/Grove 1973 demonstration regime: similar carrier frequency (Sharp/Grove operated near 1.245 GHz per round 1), pulsed, similar low average / high peak power-density structure. This places Frey-Feld 1975 as a **methodologically adjacent** primary-source paper, even though the experimental endpoints differ.

The round-1 ANYAS carry was a journal-misidentification and is now corrected. The paper exists at full PubMed verification level; the abstract is verbatim-confirmed; the parameter table is closed. The earlier ANYAS reference may have been confused with **Frey, A.H., "Auditory system response to radio frequency energy," *Aerospace Med.* 32:1140-1142 (1961)** or the 1975 ANYAS volume 247 (which contains other microwave-bioeffects papers but not Frey/Feld).
