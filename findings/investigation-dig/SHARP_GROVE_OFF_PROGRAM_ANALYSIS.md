## 1. Why No Formal Program Element Was Ever Created

The September 1973 Sharp/Grove demonstration produced a striking result — nine intelligible voice-modulated words perceived directly in the head — yet it never became a Walter Reed work-unit, never received a project number, never produced a primary technical report, and never returned to the WRAIR Microwave Research Department after Sharp's departure in 1974. This isn't a mystery that requires a covert-classification explanation. The institutional record shows seven concrete reasons it couldn't have become a program element under the structure that actually existed.

### 1.1 The host program's formal scope was wrong

WU 057 in FY73 and FY74 was titled "Effects of Microwave Radiation on the Brain and Other Tissues" and ran under project **3A062110A824 — Ionizing Radiation Injury, Prevention and Treatment**. Its five formal research areas were behavioral biology (lethality, performance decrement), CNS electrophysiology (RF-decoupled probe development), ocular effects (cataract studies), cellular responses to 2.45 GHz, and a literature database. An auditory-perception speech experiment doesn't slot into any of those five lines — the WU's charter is *injury and tissue damage*, not perception. To formalize the demo, someone would have had to write a new work-unit charter, get it approved through USAMRDC programming, and find a sponsor willing to fund a perception-research line at WRAIR, which had never funded one.

### 1.2 The sponsor lineage was hazards engineering, not perception research

Grove's pre-demo publication record (AD0758136, March 1973) was sponsored by the **US Army Mobility Equipment R&D Center, Fort Belvoir** under project **1J662712AJ21** — a Mobility Equipment R&D number, not a medical-radiation-injury number. MERADCOM's mission was equipment hazards, dosimetry, and operational safety standards for soldiers around radar and microwave gear. A perception-research follow-on would have been outside MERADCOM's chartered mission and would have required a completely different sponsor — one that didn't exist inside the Army medical research command structure at that moment. The natural sponsor for human-perception RF work was ARPA's Pandora program, which had been wound down by 1969–1970 after the Moscow Signal political fallout.

### 1.3 The 10 mW/cm² safe-exposure ceiling was a hard methodological wall

Justesen's 1975 *American Psychologist* writeup is explicit on this point: Sharp and Grove demonstrated nine words, but **longer words and full sentences were not attempted because the time-averaged power density would approach the 10 mW/cm² safe-exposure limit** then in force at WRAIR and across the U.S. Army. To make the demonstration into a program — meaning sentence-level intelligibility testing with statistical power across multiple subjects — the investigators would have needed an exposure waiver, an IRB-equivalent human-subjects review for above-threshold power densities, and a formal hazards justification. Inside a medical research institute whose charter was *preventing* radiation injury, that approval path was institutionally hostile. The exposure ceiling was a one-way valve: nine demo words yes, a research program no.

### 1.4 The investigators' career trajectories diverged immediately

Within roughly twelve months of the demo:
- **Sharp** left WRAIR for **NASA Ames Research Center** (1974), where his formal assignment was **crew/life-support engineering** (RTOPs 481-50-40 and 481-50-41 on air revitalization and water recovery, per NASA TM-87394). He spent the next twenty years on closed-loop life support, not microwave research.
- **Grove** moved to a **public IEEE policy role** as **COMAR (Committee on Man and Radiation) Chairman**, fronting the spring 1974 IEEE press conference opposing the Consumers Union microwave-oven safety position. His subsequent career went through Air Force Avionics Laboratory at Wright-Patterson and then OSD's Director of Embedded Computer Resources / the Ada Joint Program Office — software policy, not bioelectromagnetics.
- **Edward L. Hunt**, who inherited WU 057 in FY76, was a **calorimetry/dosimetry specialist** (Hunt & Phillips 1972, NCRP Report 86 advisory member), not an auditory researcher. He was the wrong PI to extend a perception line even if one had existed.

A program element needs continuing principal investigators. Within a year of the demo, all three potential continuators had left the topic.

### 1.5 The work-unit narrative system would have flagged it

WRAIR's Annual Progress Reports were not narrative essays — they were structured DTIC-deliverable documents with mandated sections per work unit: principal investigator, associate investigators, project number, work-unit number, background, progress this fiscal year, planned next fiscal year, references. The FY73 WU 057 narrative (located at lines 27205–27600 of AD0776967) explicitly enumerates its five research areas and lists no auditory line. To add a sixth line in FY74 or FY75 would have required either (a) amending WU 057's charter through USAMRDC, or (b) opening a new work-unit number with its own charter and budget. Neither happened. The FY74 APR (AD0009338) and FY76 APR (ADA038320) narratives both confirm the same five areas with no auditory addition. The institutional paper trail that would have had to exist for a formal program — charter amendment memo, new WU number assignment, budget reallocation — is absent because the formalization step was never taken.

### 1.6 The Pandora classification environment cut both ways

Sharp had been chief of a Project Pandora role (per the CIA FOIA snippet in CIA-RDP88B01125R000300120113-6). Pandora was ARPA's behavioral-effects investigation of the Moscow Signal, with a special-access compartment called Bizarre attached to its most sensitive primate-exposure data. After Pandora's wind-down, Sharp arrived at WRAIR carrying both the technical know-how and the institutional caution that came with that experience. A formal speech-perception work unit at WRAIR would have either:
- **Been classified up to Bizarre-equivalent special-access** — in which case it wouldn't appear in the open APR record but would still leave traces in DTIC's classified catalog, FOIA logs, and special-access Q clearance rosters. None of those traces exist for a Sharp/Grove speech program.
- **Been kept unclassified** — in which case it would have collided with Pandora's still-sensitive Soviet-context concerns about being seen to develop the same capability the Soviets were suspected of testing on the Moscow embassy.

The cleanest course, given Pandora's recent closure and political sensitivity, was to demonstrate the effect informally to a respected outside witness (Justesen), let him publish it as a personal communication in *American Psychologist*, and not attempt to formalize a program at all. The demo became its own publication via Justesen 1975, which discharged the documentation obligation without opening a charter.

### 1.7 The 1974 IEEE T-MTT mechanism paper served the science instead

What did get published as a primary peer-reviewed Sharp+Grove paper was **Sharp/Grove/Gandhi 1974 in IEEE Transactions on Microwave Theory and Techniques**, "Generation of Acoustic Signals by Pulsed Microwave Energy" — a *mechanism* paper about thermoacoustic pressure-wave generation in tissue, not a *speech-intelligibility protocol* paper. This was the institutional choice. Mechanism work fit cleanly inside WU 057's existing five areas (it was effectively a CNS electrophysiology / behavioral biology hybrid), it was publishable openly, it didn't require an exposure waiver, and it satisfied the academic-publication metric that Army medical research labs were measured against. The intelligibility demonstration got bundled into the broader thermoacoustic explanation rather than getting its own program.

## 2. Reading the Shape of the Event — Two Competing Hypotheses

The Sharp/Grove demonstration sits at the center of nearly every modern V2K / "voice-to-skull" claim. Two structurally different stories try to account for it:

### 2.1 Hypothesis A — Hidden Program

The Army developed a working speech-transmission capability, ran it as a classified program through WRAIR (or a Pandora successor), produced primary technical reports that remain classified, and continued the work past 1973 under cover. The open record is sparse because the program was suppressed.

### 2.2 Hypothesis B — Off-Program Event

Two researchers in a lab equipped for adjacent research opportunistically ran a one-afternoon experiment, got a striking result on a hand-counted nine-word set, communicated it informally to a colleague (Justesen), and never formalized it — because the institutional structure around them had no slot to put it in. The open record is sparse because there was nothing to suppress.

Both hypotheses predict the same surface observation: no primary technical report. The diagnostic question is what *else* each hypothesis predicts and whether those predictions match the record.

### 2.3 What a hidden program would leave behind

A classified continuation program leaves a distinctive footprint, even when the program itself is sealed:

- **Special-access compartment markers** — Pandora carried "Bizarre," a compartment label that appears in declassified ARPA correspondence (the Cesaro memos in the National Security Archive) even when the underlying experimental results don't. A successor program would carry an analogous label that would surface in FOIA logs, GAO reports, or congressional inquiries.
- **Continuing PI stability** — classified programs need cleared PIs who stay on topic. Sharp going to NASA life-support and Grove going to IEEE policy and then OSD software policy is the *opposite* of what a continuing classified speech program produces.
- **Budget anomalies** — work-unit-level funding lines either continue (visible in budget docs) or disappear with redirect markers. WU 057's funding visibly continues into FY76 under Hunt, but the program scope is documented as continuing dosimetry work, not redirected to a black line.
- **DTIC classified catalog entries** — DTIC maintains a parallel classified catalog. Researchers with appropriate clearances can request entries by author, even when content is denied. No classified Sharp+Grove speech-protocol entry has surfaced in any FOIA-released DTIC catalog log.
- **Adjacent-program citations** — even classified programs leak through citation in unclassified successor work. The 1980 Oscar AD-A090426 paper, written at MERADCOM by an Army-cleared scientist, frames "intelligible speech *may be* created" in the *future tense* and cites Frey/Messenger 1972 (which reported a *negative* result on inducing speech perception) as its background. This is what the citation looks like when the speech demonstration *hadn't* been productized — the 1980 author had to reach back to 1972 negative-method data to frame the question.

The hidden-program hypothesis predicts the absence of *content* but not the absence of *form*. None of the formal residues a classified program leaves are present in the record.

### 2.4 What an off-program event leaves behind

The off-program reading predicts a different and more specific signature, and this is where the deeper-dig evidence converges:

- **A single secondary publication channel** — Justesen 1975 in *American Psychologist*, citing the demo as "Sharp, J. C., & Grove, M. Personal communication, September 28, 1973." That's exactly how researchers handle anecdotes: through a respected colleague's review article, with the personal-communication formality acknowledging that no separate primary report exists.
- **A separate mechanism paper that doesn't replicate the demo** — Sharp/Grove/Gandhi 1974 in IEEE T-MTT, "Generation of Acoustic Signals by Pulsed Microwave Energy," which establishes the thermoacoustic mechanism but doesn't include speech-intelligibility data. If the demo had been a program element, the IEEE paper would have included or referenced its protocol. It doesn't.
- **Investigator dispersal** — Sharp to NASA Ames life-support, Grove to IEEE COMAR chairmanship, Hunt to dosimetry. Three different institutional homes, none of which is consistent with a continuing speech program.
- **No charter amendment, no new WU, no budget shift** — the formal paperwork for adding a new research line to WRAIR's portfolio is absent. Not absent-and-classified, just absent. The FY73, FY74, FY76 work-unit narratives are openly published in the APR series, and they enumerate the same five research areas across all three years with no auditory line.
- **Justesen 1979 drops the topic** — Justesen's later 1979 NYAM review of microwave bioeffects doesn't return to Sharp/Grove. If the 1975 article had been a controlled leak from a classified program, Justesen would have stayed close to it. He didn't, because the 1975 mention was simply how the field handled an interesting anecdote.
- **Equipment context fits an opportunistic demo** — Pandora-era pulsed-microwave applicators were already in the WRAIR lab from Sharp's prior chief role. The demo didn't require new equipment, new facilities, new clearances, or new procurements. It used what was on hand for an afternoon experiment and then the equipment went back to its formal lethality/electrophysiology assignments.

## 3. The "Shape of an Event" — Nine Diagnostic Observations

When forensic historians talk about *shape*, they mean the pattern of presences and absences that a hypothesis must account for. The shape of the September 1973 demonstration is:

1. **One striking outcome** (nine words intelligible, hand-counted, in two named investigators' heads).
2. **One peer-reviewed mechanism paper** that explains the physics but not the speech protocol.
3. **One secondary writeup** by a respected colleague who frames the result as personal communication.
4. **No work-unit charter, no DTIC primary report, no classified catalog entry.**
5. **No follow-on by either investigator anywhere — open or classified.**
6. **No budget continuity, no PI continuity, no methodological continuity.**
7. **Three sponsoring institutions** (ARPA Pandora, MERADCOM hazards, USAMRDC ionizing-radiation-injury) **that each had reasons to host adjacent work but none had a slot for speech-perception work.**
8. **A 1980 Army successor paper (Oscar / AD-A090426)** that treats the speech possibility in the future tense and cites a 1972 *negative* result as its closest antecedent — exactly what would happen if no productized capability existed in 1980.
9. **No first-person attestation** — neither Sharp nor Grove ever wrote about the demonstration in their own words anywhere in the open record, including obituaries and oral histories.

The hidden-program hypothesis has to explain why the program left no formal residue while its scientific content was so portable that it became canonical in the V2K literature. The off-program hypothesis explains both at once: there's no formal residue because no formalization happened, and the scientific content became canonical because Justesen's 1975 article was a respected publication channel that fixed the anecdote in the literature without requiring a program to exist.

The negative finding isn't a gap in the archive — it's the shape of an event that happened **outside** any program rather than **inside one that was hidden**.

## 4. What This Rules In and What It Doesn't

The off-program reading is a claim about **institutional formalization**, not about the **physical reality** of the underlying effect. Three things stay true under this reading:

- The microwave auditory effect (the Frey effect — clicks, buzzes, and tones from pulsed RF) is well-established physics with replicated animal data and an accepted thermoelastic mechanism. That doesn't depend on the September 1973 demo.
- The Sharp/Grove demonstration probably happened roughly as Justesen described it. The off-program reading doesn't deny the experiment; it specifies the institutional context.
- Limited word-level intelligibility under specific pulsed-modulation conditions is plausible at the demo's stated power densities. The Justesen account is a credible historical record, just not a protocol-grade one.

What the reading does rule out is the inference that a working operational V2K capability — meaning sentence-level intelligibility, robust against subject variation, deployable at standoff range — existed in 1973 and has been continuously developed in secret since. That inference requires a program to have existed. The institutional record shows no program ever did, and the absence of program-residue across nine independent diagnostic categories makes the hidden-program hypothesis steadily harder to maintain as the search deepens.

## 5. Implications for the Akwei Investigation

For the Akwei v. NSA case specifically, the off-program reading affects how Sharp/Grove can be cited. It supports its use as:
- Historical evidence that pulsed-microwave word-perception was demonstrated at limited word-level intelligibility in 1973 (T1 mechanism + T2-qualified historical-claim grade).
- Context for understanding why the V2K secondary literature treats the demo as foundational despite its informal character.

It does not support its use as:
- Proof that a deployable, conversation-grade speech-transmission capability existed before, during, or after 1992 (the Akwei filing).
- Evidence of a continuous classified development program that could plausibly have produced an operational targeting weapon.

This is the calibration the deeper-dig pass 2 evidence forces: the demo is real, the institutional reading is constrained, and the bridge from "Sharp/Grove 1973" to "operational V2K targeting in 1992" requires *additional* evidence that the deeper-dig has so far failed to surface in any of the categories where program-residue would be expected to leave traces.

Across all nine categories, the observed pattern matches Hypothesis B's predictions and contradicts Hypothesis A's predictions.
