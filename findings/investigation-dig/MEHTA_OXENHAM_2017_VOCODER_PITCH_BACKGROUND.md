# Mehta & Oxenham (2017) — Background psychoacoustic support for vocoder / periodicity-pitch limits

**Date added:** 2026-05-11
**Classification (single, explicit):** **Background psychoacoustic support — modern auditory-science context for the AD747684 (Frey/Messenger/Eichert 1972) negative-method finding around vocoder technology and periodicity pitch.** This paper is **not** RF-hearing primary evidence, **not** V2K primary evidence, and **not** evidence of remote speech induction by any method. It is included only to provide modern, peer-reviewed psychoacoustic context for *why* the specific routes Frey's group reported as failures in 1972 (vocoder-style synthesis and periodicity-pitch acoustic analogs) face strong constraints for accurate complex spectral pitch — constraints that have nothing to do with RF as a carrier.

## 2 · What the paper actually establishes (verbatim-anchored)

The paper reports five experiments in normal-hearing adults using noise-excited envelope vocoder simulations of cochlear-implant (CI) processing on a melody-discrimination paradigm (Oxenham et al. 2011 paradigm; one- or two-semitone scale steps). The key quantitative findings (paraphrased from the JARO body, pp. 789–800):

1. **Experiment 1 (no spectral overlap between channels).** A minimum of **32 channels** is required for above-chance complex spectral pitch discrimination; 8- and 16-channel conditions did not differ from chance for complex tones (paper §"Experiment 1," Fig. 2a, p. 793).
2. **Experiment 2 (channel × slope interactions).** With spectral overlap introduced via Butterworth filter slopes of **24, 48, and 72 dB/oct**, only the 72 dB/oct × 64-channel condition produced above-chance complex-tone performance; the shallower-slope conditions did not (Fig. 2b, pp. 793–794).
3. **Experiment 3 (steep slopes at 64 channels).** With slopes of **72, 96, 120, and 144 dB/oct** at 64 channels, performance improved gradually with steepness but **even 144 dB/oct was substantially poorer than the no-overlap conditions** (Fig. 2c, p. 795).
4. **Experiment 4 (asymmetric filter slopes).** A single very steep edge (360 dB/oct) on either the low or high side, combined with a shallower opposite edge, produced equivalent melody-discrimination performance regardless of which side was steep — but pitch-matching (Experiment 5) showed pitch was tied to the **peak** of the vocoder filter rather than the spectral centroid.
5. **General Discussion (pp. 799–800).** The authors state explicitly that *"the minimum number of channels required for accurate complex spectral pitch appears to be at least 32, with filter slopes exceeding 72 dB/octave,"* and that *"it seems unlikely that current CI devices will meet these constraints, even with significant further technical innovations."* Current CIs have ~12–24 active channels with electrical-current-spread-equivalent slopes of 8–24 dB/oct.

## 3 · Why this is psychoacoustic support, not RF-hearing or V2K evidence

The paper studies **acoustic vocoder simulations played to normal-hearing listeners through headphones**. The participants are normal-hearing adults; the stimuli are noise-vocoded sounds delivered as ordinary sound via Sennheiser HD 650 headphones at 65 dB SPL in a sound-attenuating booth, in background threshold-equalising noise (TEN) at 55 dB SPL/ERB. There is no radio-frequency carrier, no thermoelastic mechanism, no microwave-auditory route, no implant, and no remote delivery. Mehta & Oxenham (2017) is **modern psychoacoustics**: it characterises the spectral-resolution constraints on extracting complex spectral pitch from a vocoder representation, full stop.

The link to the V2K / RF-hearing repository chain is **mechanistic / contextual only**:

- AD747684 (Frey/Messenger/Eichert 1972) reports two specific negative method findings: *"aspects of vocoder technology could not be used to generate speech"* and *"perceived rf sound can not be generated using the rules of the periodicity pitch phenomenon."* See [`AD_A090426_REFERENCE_CHAIN_EVIDENCE.md`](AD_A090426_REFERENCE_CHAIN_EVIDENCE.md) §1.1 and [`fy74_wrair_speech_demo_hunt/DEEPER_DIG_9_ADDENDUM.md`](fy74_wrair_speech_demo_hunt/DEEPER_DIG_9_ADDENDUM.md) §2 for the verbatim recovery of those abstract and conclusion lines from the Internet Archive DTIC mirror of AD747684.
- Mehta & Oxenham (2017) supplies modern psychoacoustic context for *why* those specific synthesis routes face hard constraints: extracting a reliable complex spectral pitch (the kind of pitch that carries linguistic prosody, intonation, and speaker identity in speech) requires substantially more spectral resolution than current CI hardware provides, and substantially more than a low-channel vocoder route can deliver even in the absence of any electrical-spread limitation. It also distinguishes simple speech intelligibility (which the literature it cites — Shannon et al. 1995, Loizou/Dorman/Tu 1999, Friesen et al. 2001 — shows is achievable with as few as four spectral channels) from **pitch-bearing aspects of speech** (prosody, speaker identification, F0 in noise/competing-talker conditions), which require much higher spectral resolution.

This is a **plausibility / mechanism note for one bounded sentence in AD747684's abstract**, not a claim about RF-hearing, thermoelastic acoustic generation, the Sharp/Grove 1973 demonstration, the Oscar 1980 "intelligible speech may be created" sentence, or any operational protocol.

## 4 · Explicit limitations and what this paper does NOT support

The paper does **not**:

- claim that intelligible speech cannot be generated by any RF method — it does not address RF at all.
- claim that the Sharp/Grove 28 September 1973 zero-crossing pulse-trigger method would fail — that method bypasses vocoder synthesis and periodicity-pitch acoustic analogs entirely, and Mehta & Oxenham say nothing about thermoelastic acoustic-pulse mechanisms in the head.
- claim that cochlear-implant users cannot understand speech — they can; the paper carefully distinguishes simple speech intelligibility from pitch-bearing speech cues.
- speak to V2K, remote speech induction, microwave auditory effect, or any "voice to skull" mechanism.

The relevance is therefore **narrow and one-directional**: it strengthens the plausibility of the specific 1972 negative method finding in AD747684 (vocoder-style synthesis and periodicity-pitch acoustic analogs face hard pitch-resolution constraints), and nothing more. It does **not** speak to whether other methods (Sharp/Grove's zero-crossing pulse trigger from prerecorded voice waveforms, or any successor) might or might not work.
