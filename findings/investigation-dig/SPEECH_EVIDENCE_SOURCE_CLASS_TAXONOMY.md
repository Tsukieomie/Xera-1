# Speech Evidence Source-Class Taxonomy: "Is the Speech Real?"

**Compiled: 2026-04-30**

A consolidated source-class map for the question "is the speech real?" in the
microwave-auditory-effect / Sharp-Grove / V2K evidence chain. This file does
not introduce new factual claims about Sharp/Grove that are not already in
`SHARP_GROVE_PRIMARY_REPORT_HUNT.md` §2-§4 and
`03_Technology_Patents/34_JUSTESEN_1975_APA_MICROWAVE_HEARING.md`. It
*classifies* the seven kinds of evidence that a careful reader will encounter
on this question, so they are not silently conflated.

The question splits into two parts that must be kept separate:

- **Q1 — Is the microwave/RF auditory effect itself real?** Yes, strongly
  supported. (Tier A, mechanism-level.)
- **Q2 — Has intelligible speech via that effect been replicated and validated
  at protocol grade in the open record?** No. The published historical
  account (Justesen 1975 retelling of a Sharp/Grove personal communication),
  the patent record (Brunkan 1989; O'Loughlin/Loree 1996/2002/2003), and the
  SBIR mirrored record (Kohn / SEA 1993) are all real artifacts, but none of
  them is a protocol-grade speech-replication study.

The taxonomy below makes the source-class distinctions explicit.

---

## (1) The microwave/RF auditory effect itself: real, strongly supported

**Status:** Tier A; mechanism-level real.

**Phenomenology (per the modern reviews):** brief intense RF pulses are
perceived as clicks, buzzes, chirps, knocks, hisses, or pressure sensations —
not as words. Single pulses are perceived as a *zip*, *click*, or *knock*;
pulse trains are perceived as *buzz*, *chirp*, or *tune* (Lin 2022 IEEE
J-ERM 6:16-28, abstract via NASA ADS
<https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2022IJERM...6...16L/abstract>; National
Academies "An Assessment of Illness in U.S. Government Employees and Their
Families at Overseas Embassies" mechanisms chapter, NBK566408
<https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/>, treats Frey-effect clicks /
buzz / hiss / knocking / pressure and cites Sharp/Grove/Gandhi 1974 for
external sounds from pulsed RF interacting with materials).

**Mechanism:** thermoelastic expansion of brain tissue under microsecond-scale
RF heating, generating an acoustic pressure wave that reaches the cochlea via
bone conduction. Thresholds reported by Foster, Garrett, Ziskin 2021 (PMC8733248
<https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/>) for low-GHz pulses are
~0.02-0.4 J/m² incident energy density.

**What this class supports:** the Frey effect is real; the Sharp/Grove/Gandhi
1974 IEEE T-MTT 22(5):583-584 mechanism letter (DOI
<https://doi.org/10.1109/TMTT.1974.1128293>) is a peer-reviewed primary
acoustic-signal-generation paper.

**What this class does NOT support:** intelligible speech delivery. The
phenomenology vocabulary across Lin 2022 and the National Academies AHI report
is *click / buzz / chirp / knock / hiss / pressure*, not *words*.

## (2) Sharp/Grove digit-word demonstration as published in Justesen 1975

**Status:** Tier B published historical claim, anchored in a personal
communication.

**Source:** Justesen, D. R. (1975). "Microwaves and Behavior." *American
Psychologist* 30(3):391-401. PMID 1137231; DOI 10.1037/0003-066X.30.3.391.
Mirror PDF (sufficient for verbatim text checks):
<https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf>.

**What Justesen reports (p. 396, paraphrased; see file 34 for the verbatim
treatment):** Sharp and Grove modulated a pulsed microwave carrier with the
analog voice waveforms of the digit words "one" through "ten" by
zero-crossing-triggered pulse emission; by radiating themselves they could
hear, identify, and distinguish nine of the ten words; longer messages were
not attempted because the average power densities required would approach the
then-current 10 mW/cm² safe-exposure guide.

**Source-class caveat:** Justesen's reference Note 2 cites "Sharp, J. C., &
Grove, M. *Personal communication, September 28, 1973.*" The Justesen 1975
article is therefore the *primary unclassified published account/report* of
the demonstration, but the underlying experiment is, by Justesen's own
footnote, a personal communication. No separately-authored Sharp/Grove
protocol-grade speech-demonstration technical report (with subject count
beyond the named investigators, sham controls, blinding, RF parameters,
scoring method, error distribution, or independent replication) has been
located in the open record after the targeted DTIC / Lin 1978 / Lin 2022 /
Foster-Garrett-Ziskin 2021 / Glaser 1976 NMRI bibliography hunt documented in
`SHARP_GROVE_PRIMARY_REPORT_HUNT.md` §2-§4 and the 13 deeper-dive rounds
indexed in `findings/investigation-dig/fy74_wrair_speech_demo_hunt/`.

**What this class supports:** that a peer-reviewed APA-flagship-journal
account of a voice-modulated-microwave digit-word demonstration was on the
public record by March 1975, attributed by name to Walter Reed / ARPA
researchers. This is published historical claim, not a protocol-grade
demonstration.

**What this class does NOT support:** continuous speech, sentence delivery,
two-way communication, remote-thought reading, operational range, robustness
across naïve listeners, or any independent replication. Justesen himself is
explicit that Sharp and Grove did not attempt longer messages because of the
exposure-guide ceiling.

## (3) Brunkan US 4,877,027 A (1989): patent claim, inventor self-disclosed
speculation

**Status:** Tier B patent claim; inventor verbatim disclaims having tested
the system.

**Source:** US 4,877,027 A, Brunkan, "Hearing system" (filed 1988-02-26;
issued 1989-10-31). Google Patents
<https://patents.google.com/patent/US4877027A/en>.

**Claim language:** the patent claims a system that uses microwave bursts
"of an audio character" to induce intelligible audio in a person whose head is
irradiated, citing Frey 1973 *Science* and Lin 1978 *Microwave Auditory
Effects and Applications* (pp. 176-177) in the prior-art discussion.

**Inventor self-disclosure:** the patent specification contains the verbatim
caveat:

> "I have not been able to experiment to determine how my microwave system
> works..."

(US 4,877,027 A specification, as quoted on the Google Patents primary
record.)

**Source-class consequence:** the Brunkan patent is a granted patent claim,
but the inventor expressly states he has not experimentally validated the
mechanism by which the claimed system produces intelligible audio. A patent
claim issued under USPTO §112 enablement standards is *not* a peer-reviewed
replication study. Brunkan therefore stands as a 1989 patent-record
expression of the goal of intelligible-speech RF transmission, with the
inventor himself flagging that the underlying experimental validation was not
in his hands.

**What this class supports:** that the goal of intelligible-speech RF
transmission was on the federal patent record by 1989 with the USPTO accepting
the §112 enablement showing.

**What this class does NOT support:** that Brunkan demonstrated intelligible
speech delivery in human subjects, replicated Sharp/Grove, or validated the
mechanism. The inventor disclaims that he did. Brunkan should never be cited
as evidence that V2K speech "works"; cite him as evidence that the *patent
claim* is on the record.

## (4) O'Loughlin / Loree US 6,470,214 B1 (1996/2002), US 6,587,729 B2 (2002/2003), and JASA 2003 113:2962

**Status:** Tier B engineering / patent claim. The granted patent
specification itself records that ordinary AM-sampled speech attempts produced
*unintelligible* audio unless the listener was pre-advised of content; the
proposed solution (suppressed-carrier double-sideband + audio predistortion
+ DC bias + square-root processor) is patented but has no public protocol-grade
replication located.

**Sources:**
- US 6,470,214 B1, O'Loughlin & Loree, "Method and device for implementing
  the radio frequency hearing effect" (filed 1996-12-13; issued 2002-10-22),
  assigned to U.S. Air Force.
  <https://patents.google.com/patent/US6470214B1/en>
- US 6,587,729 B2, O'Loughlin & Loree, companion apparatus patent (filed
  2002-04-24; issued 2003-07-01), assigned to U.S. Air Force.
  <https://patents.google.com/patent/US6587729B2/en>
- O'Loughlin, J. P., & Loree, D. L. (2003). "Method and device for
  implementing the radio frequency hearing effect." *Journal of the
  Acoustical Society of America* 113(6):2962. Semantic Scholar metadata
  record:
  <https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/acc9a71f38cd18cece2b53b079ba0d1a75670b99>.
  The JASA 2003 abstract mirrors the patent specification language; the
  underlying full-text experimental protocol details are not in the abstract
  record located here.

**Patent-spec caveat (verbatim, paraphrased close):** the specification
records that with simple pulsed-carrier or simple-tone modulation, perceived
audio has the *envelope* characteristics of speech and can be recognized as
speech *only when the listener is preadvised of the content*; with content
unknown, the recovered audio is **unintelligible**. The proposed
suppressed-carrier + preprocessing chain is presented as the solution, but
the granted patent does not present a blinded human-subjects intelligibility
study with word-error rates against naïve listeners. (See file 36 Part 3 for
the engineering chain; see SHARP_GROVE_PRIMARY_REPORT_HUNT.md §4.5 for the
intelligibility-caveat row.)

**Source-class consequence:** the 1996-2003 Air Force patent + JASA pair is
the **federal-ownership engineering bridge** to V2K speech. It establishes
that the U.S. Air Force, via career AFRL Directed Energy Directorate
engineers (the same team that developed Active Denial Technology), patented
a speech-encoding scheme designed to overcome the unintelligibility
limitation of prior AM-based attempts. It does **not** establish that any
public protocol-grade replication of intelligible speech delivery against
naïve human listeners has been published. It is engineering claim + journal-
of-record metadata, not validated open-literature speech replication.

**What this class supports:** federal-record intent and engineering investment
in solving the unintelligibility problem; institutional continuity with
Active Denial Technology under the same AFRL Directed Energy Directorate
team.

**What this class does NOT support:** validated naïve-listener intelligible
speech replication in the open literature. The patent itself records the
unintelligibility-without-preadvising baseline and proposes (does not
empirically validate at protocol grade) the suppressed-carrier solution.

**What this class does NOT support:** the protocol-grade content of either
contract's deliverables. The mirror is a metadata snapshot, not the
deliverable itself.

## (6) Foster, Garrett, Ziskin 2021: modern review supports MAE, not speech

**Status:** Tier A modern peer-reviewed review supporting the underlying
microwave auditory effect; does **not** support speech replication; treats
weaponization as practicality-limited.

**Source:** Foster, K. R., Garrett, J. R., & Ziskin, M. C. (2021). "Can the
Microwave Auditory Effect Be 'Weaponized'?" *Frontiers in Public Health*
9:788613. PMC8733248
<https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/>.

**What the paper supports:**
- Brief intense RF pulses can elicit auditory sensations.
- The thermoacoustic mechanism is plausible and quantitatively supported.
- Reported thresholds: ~0.02-0.4 J/m² incident energy density for low-GHz
  pulses.
- Some systems can produce startling auditory effects.

**What the paper does NOT support:**
- Intelligible-speech replication. The review contains **no Sharp/Grove
  citation at all** and presents no speech data.
- Operational weaponization. The authors explicitly conclude that practical
  weaponization for speech delivery appears unlikely on practicality and
  effect-size grounds.

**Source-class consequence:** Foster/Garrett/Ziskin 2021 is the strongest
single peer-reviewed Tier A confirmation of the underlying mechanism in the
modern literature, while simultaneously cutting the other way on the
weaponized-speech claim. Cite this paper as Tier A confirmation that the
*effect* is real and as Tier A skepticism that weaponized intelligible
*speech* via this effect has been demonstrated.

## (7) Lin 2022 IEEE J-ERM and National Academies AHI mechanisms chapter (NBK566408): mechanism, phenomenology — not speech

**Status:** Tier A modern reviews confirming mechanism + phenomenology
vocabulary; do **not** establish speech.

**Sources:**
- Lin, J. C. (2022). "The Microwave Auditory Effect." *IEEE Journal of
  Electromagnetics, RF, and Microwaves in Medicine and Biology* 6:16-28.
  NASA ADS abstract
  <https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2022IJERM...6...16L/abstract>.
- National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine. (2020). *An
  Assessment of Illness in U.S. Government Employees and Their Families at
  Overseas Embassies.* Mechanisms chapter, NBK566408
  <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/>.

**What these classes support:**
- Single RF pulses → perceived as *zip*, *click*, or *knock* (Lin 2022
  taxonomy).
- Pulse trains → perceived as *buzz*, *chirp*, or *tune* (Lin 2022).
- Acoustic pressure wave conducted to the cochlea by bone conduction (Lin
  2022; National Academies AHI mechanisms chapter).
- The National Academies AHI report cites Sharp/Grove/Gandhi 1974 for
  external sounds produced when pulsed RF interacts with materials. It does
  **not** cite Justesen 1975 for the speech demonstration as a validated
  mechanism for intelligible speech delivery.

**What these classes do NOT support:**
- Intelligible speech delivery. The phenomenology vocabulary across both
  reviews is exactly *click / buzz / chirp / knock / pressure*, not *words*.
- The National Academies AHI mechanisms chapter treats Frey-effect-style
  perceptual signatures as a candidate mechanism for the *anomalous health
  incident* phenomenology (clicks / buzz / pressure), not as evidence that
  covert intelligible-speech delivery has been demonstrated.

## Bottom line for "is the speech real?"

- The **microwave auditory effect** is real and well-supported across primary
  experimental, mechanism, and modern review sources (classes 1, 6, 7).
- The **intelligible-speech subset** has, in the open record:
  - A 1975 APA-flagship-journal *published historical claim* rooted in a
    1973 personal communication (class 2 — Justesen retelling Sharp/Grove,
    digit words, self-irradiation, no naïve listeners).
  - A 1989 *patent claim* whose inventor explicitly disclaims experimental
    validation (class 3 — Brunkan).
  - A 1996-2003 *federal-ownership engineering claim* whose own
    specification records the unintelligibility-with-naïve-AM baseline
    (class 4 — O'Loughlin/Loree + JASA).
  - A 1993-1995 *mirrored SBIR / FOIA lead* with no official-portal recovery
    yet (class 5 — Kohn / SEA / Armstrong Laboratory).
  - **No** Tier A protocol-grade naïve-listener intelligible-speech
    replication located in any modern peer-reviewed review (classes 6, 7).

Citations on the speech question should preserve this seven-way distinction.
The repository's existing position — Tier A microwave-auditory mechanism is
strongly supported, Tier B intelligible-speech claim is a published
historical claim still lacking protocol-grade primary documentation — remains
the disciplined position consistent with the evidence as of 2026-04-30.
