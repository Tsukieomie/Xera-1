## 1) Executive conclusion

- The **Patent Office form scans (E-016)** confirm order existence, serial 756,124, applicant Gillis P. Flanagan, title "METHOD AND SYSTEM FOR SIMPLIFYING SPEECH WAVEFORMS," statutory basis (35 U.S.C. (1952) §§ 181-188), and the Apr 25 1969 order date. They do **not** name DIA, NSA, ASPAB, or any specific DoD component.
- **DIA attribution remains unproven.** It originates from later Flanagan / Begich / NEXUS-tradition retellings (E-013, E-014, E-025). No official government record found to date names DIA as the sponsoring or causing agency for serial 756,124.
- **Period-correct DoD patent-secrecy authority points away from DIA as the formal statutory sponsor.** The 1964 Federal Register Air Force § 850.12 codification, the 1966 Federal Register delegation notice, and the 1972 Federal Register / DoD Part 159 § 159.206-1 codification all describe **DoD Directive 5535.2** as delegating 35 U.S.C. § 181 / 182 / 184 authority to the **Secretaries of the Army, Navy, and Air Force**. DIA held Top Secret classification authority during this period, but that is **not** the same as formal patent-secrecy sponsorship under § 181.
- **NSA remains technically plausible as a reviewer / driver.** PSCRL Group IX / 6A (Jan 1971) covers NSA-cryptology / communications-security topics including analog and digitized voice privacy / speech secrecy, pseudo-random digit / sequence generation, and low-detectability / spread-spectrum communications, all of which fit the "secure speech information" subject matter recited in the issued patent's abstract.
- **Historical NSA evidence** (NSA Regulation 80-2; "NSA Comes Out of the Closet"; Reason's Phasorphone reporting) supports the pattern that NSA could review cryptologic patent applications, recommend secrecy, and be the responsible technical reviewer **while paperwork was outwardly routed through DoD / Army / ASPAB-like channels**.
- **Best concise reading on the present record:** if the order was DoD-caused, the official paperwork most likely identifies **ASPAB**, an **Army / Navy / Air Force** Service Secretary, or **Army OJAG** executive-agent channels even where a technical / intelligence reviewer (NSA, possibly DIA) supplied the underlying assessment. The present public record does not identify that reviewer. Based on subject-matter fit and period-correct delegation authority, **NSA / ASPAB / Service-channel attribution is currently better supported than DIA-as-formal-sponsor**.

## 3) Why DIA is not yet proven

1. **No statutory delegation found.** The 1964 Air Force § 850.12 codification (E-A3), the 1966 Federal Register delegation notice (E-A2), and the 1972 DoD Part 159 § 159.206-1 codification (E-A1) all describe DoD Directive 5535.2 as delegating 35 U.S.C. § 181 / 182 / 184 authority to the **Secretaries of the Army, Navy, and Air Force**. None of those public delegation texts names DIA as a designated defense agency for purposes of § 181 referral.
2. **Top Secret classification authority ≠ § 181 sponsorship.** The 1972 FR Part 159 Appendix A lists DIA among Top Secret classification authorities (E-A1). Classification authority governs the marking of national-security information; § 181 sponsorship is the separate authority to ask the Commissioner of Patents to withhold a patent. Conflating the two would treat any DoD intelligence component with Top Secret authority as also a § 181 sponsor, which the period regulations do not support.
3. **DIA's own pattern when canvassed.** In the 1984 CIA secrecy-order coordination packets (E-A6), DIA's response on the applications then under review stated they were **outside DIA's purview**. That is consistent with DIA's general posture as a foreign-military-intelligence consumer rather than a patent-secrecy sponsoring agency, and it weakens the prior assumption that DIA would routinely act as a § 181 referrer for cryptology / COMSEC content.
4. **Subject-matter mismatch.** Cryptology / COMSEC / voice-privacy / spread-spectrum content sits in PSCRL Group IX / 6A (E-022), the canonical NSA-cryptology / COMSEC review category. PSCRL routing for such content runs to NSA, not DIA. A DIA-driven secrecy order on a "simplifying speech waveforms" / "transmitting secure speech information" application would be a domain mismatch.
5. **Source-tradition only.** The DIA attribution is carried by Begich (E-014) and the NEXUS-derived "Three papers" page 5 transcription (E-013), and reproduced in compilations (E-025). These are tertiary nodes within a single Flanagan/Begich source tradition; they corroborate the existence of a DIA-attributing narrative, not the underlying attribution claim.

## 4) Why NSA remains plausible

1. **Subject-matter fit.** The patent's title and abstract (E-016, E-023) and CPC classification (H04K "Secret communication") sit directly inside PSCRL Group IX / 6A's NSA-cryptology / COMSEC category, including voice privacy / speech secrecy (analog and digitized), pseudo-random digit / sequence generation, and low-detectability / spread-spectrum communications (E-022).
2. **NSA had its own internal patent-review mechanism.** NSA Regulation No. 80-2 (1953) tasks the Assistant Director, Research and Development, with investigating / coordinating / reviewing patent applications submitted to NSA, including coordination with Production, COMSEC, and other interested divisions, and recommending secrecy where cryptologic principles could be detrimental (E-A4). The mechanism existed and was used for cryptologic content.
3. **NSA was a routine cryptologic-patent reviewer.** The 1996 government report "NSA Comes Out of the Closet" (E-A5) documents that cryptologic inventions were routinely passed to NSA, that NSA could recommend secrecy, and that this happened in cases such as Davida and Nicolai. It also documents that DIRNSA (Inman) at one point chose to err on the side of national security in such recommendations.
4. **Outward routing through Army / DoD does not exclude NSA.** Reason (1981) reported on the Phasorphone speech-scrambler case (E-A7): the Patent Office initially pointed inventors to Army, but the underlying files showed the Army representative advised **against** secrecy and that NSA was the **responsible agency**. The Flanagan case displays the same outward shape (Patent Office instruments, no agency named on the order), and "Army / ASPAB outward routing" therefore does not rule out NSA technical sponsorship.
5. **Flanagan first-person tradition.** Distinct from the Begich/NEXUS DIA tradition, Flanagan's first-person narrative tradition (preserved in E-004 and E-025) attributes the action to NSA. Where two source traditions diverge, period-correct delegation authority and PSCRL routing favor the NSA reading.

## 5) Why Army / ASPAB may appear in paperwork even if NSA drove technical review

1. **§ 181 delegation runs to Service Secretaries.** 1964 / 1966 / 1972 FR texts (E-A1, E-A2, E-A3) all delegate § 181 / 182 / 184 authority to the Secretaries of the Army, Navy, and Air Force under 5535.2. Outward paperwork from a DoD-caused order would therefore most naturally bear a Service Secretary or a delegate (ASPAB) as the recommending entity, even when the underlying technical assessment came from NSA.
2. **ASPAB is the unified DoD recommendation channel.** FAS (E-017, E-018) describes ASPAB as the sole DoD authority for transmitting secrecy-order recommendations to the Commissioner of Patents; defense-agency concurrences (DIA, NSA, Service intelligence, OSD) flow into ASPAB and out as a unified DoD position.
3. **Army OJAG executive-agent role.** The 1984 CIA packets (E-A6, E-A8) show Army OJAG / DAJA-IP (John Raubitschek) acting as the operating coordination point for IC canvassing on patent secrecy. This is a documented pattern of Army-executive-agent paperwork carrying technical input from other agencies.
4. **Phasorphone analog.** Reason's Phasorphone reporting (E-A7) directly documents the divergence between outward Army routing and underlying NSA responsibility. The Flanagan case cannot be presumed to be otherwise on the present record.
5. **Implication.** Identifying "Army," "Navy," "Air Force," "ASPAB," or "OJAG / DAJA-IP" on the certified file-wrapper paperwork for serial 756,124 would be **consistent with NSA technical sponsorship** as well as with Service-level sponsorship; it would not, by itself, refute either reading.

## 7) Best concise reading

If the order on serial 756,124 was DoD-caused — which the period-correct § 181 delegation framework supports as the formal channel — the official paperwork is most likely to identify **ASPAB**, an **Army / Navy / Air Force** Service Secretary, or **Army OJAG executive-agent** channels (e.g., DAJA-IP), even where the underlying technical or intelligence reviewer (NSA, possibly DIA) supplied the assessment.

The present public record:

- **Does** support order existence, serial, applicant, title, statute, and order date (E-016).
- **Does not** support a specific § 181 sponsor or recommending agency.
- **Does not** support DIA as the formal sponsor.
- **Is consistent with** NSA as the technical reviewer, with outward routing through ASPAB or a Service Secretary.

Based on subject-matter fit and period-correct delegation authority, **NSA / ASPAB / Service-channel attribution is currently better supported than DIA-as-formal-sponsor** on the public record, while DIA-as-formal-sponsor remains a source-tradition narrative claim from Begich / NEXUS without official corroboration.

## 12) Third-layer addendum (2026-05-01): form-face omission, successor-retention, negative agency-side search, CIA process lead

This addendum re-weights three readings from new ledger work without changing the section 1 / 7 conclusions. It adds rows **E-A14 through E-A17**.

### 12.1 Form-face sponsor omission is normal form behavior

Comparison of declassified secrecy-order forms in the NSA Friedman Collection (1941-1947 Patent Office secrecy-order forms held in NSA's patent-equipment folders) and a CIA-released 1956 Patent Office secrecy-order file shows that the generic Patent Office / Department of Commerce secrecy-order form historically does **not** name the sponsoring or recommending agency on the order face. The order face routinely carries only the Commissioner's signature line and the statutory recital.

Implication for E-016: the absence of NSA / DIA / DoD / ASPAB on the Flanagan order face is **normal form behavior**, not evidence against an agency sponsor. The argument in section 3 ("Why DIA is not yet proven") therefore should not lean on form-face silence; it leans on period-correct § 181 delegation, subject-matter routing, and the Begich/NEXUS source-tradition character of the DIA claim.

Sponsor identity for serial 756,124 is more likely to be recorded in:

- **D-1** (USPTO national-security patent application file; E-026 / NC1-241-76-01)
- **AC-AK** (USPTO Group 2200 reviewer / agency / recommendation folders; E-A11 / N1-241-96-003)
- **Group 2200** application correspondence retained inside the certified file wrapper (E-027 / E-028)
- **ASPAB** referral / concurrence correspondence (E-017, E-018, E-A9 / FN 406-02)
- **Defense-agency concurrence** correspondence routed through ASPAB (NSA, DIA, military departments, OSD)
- **File-wrapper correspondence** (Licensing & Review / former Group 220 letters retained in the wrapper)

This refines, but does not contradict, section 6's ranking of likely-surviving smoking-gun record classes.

### 12.2 Successor-retention refinement (N1-241-96-003 item 37 → N1-241-10-001 permanent transfer to NARA)

NARA SF-115 **N1-241-96-003** item 37 describes Group 2200 applications selected for security review by defense agencies and DOE, including secrecy orders issued on those applications. NARA SF-115 **N1-241-10-001** crosswalks N1-241-96-003 item 37 a/b into a **permanent transfer-to-NARA** bucket after closure of the application.

Caveat: local OCR and source extraction disagree on the exact closure-window length (one extraction read 18 years; another read 40 years). The disagreement is in the closure-timing parameter, not in the permanent-transfer disposition. Until the SF-115 PDF is read by a human reviewer, this should be cited as **"permanent transfer to NARA after closure"**, citing the underlying PDF, and **without** committing to an exact closure interval.

Implication for survival odds:

- A **Group 2200 selected-application record** for serial 756,124 has materially better survival odds than a stand-alone D-1 or AC-AK or ASPAB folder, because the successor schedule promotes the selected-application bucket from temporary to permanent transfer to NARA.
- The other temporary classes recorded in section 6 (D-1 under NC1-241-76-01; AC-AK under N1-241-96-003 strict 3-year retention; FN 406-02 ASPAB review files under Army schedule, 20 years after rescission; Air Force Table 51-2 Item 4) **still** had short destruction schedules under their governing instruments unless the underlying papers were copied into the file wrapper or retained elsewhere.
- The certified USPTO/NARA file wrapper (E-027 / E-028) remains the single highest-probability surviving record. Group 2200 selected-application material crosswalked under N1-241-10-001 is the next most likely surviving channel and should be added to the closure-target queue.
