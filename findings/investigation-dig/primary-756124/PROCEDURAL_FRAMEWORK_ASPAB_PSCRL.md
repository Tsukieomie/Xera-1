# Procedural Framework for Application 756,124: Statute, USPTO Process, ASPAB, PSCRL, and Archival Path

Date: 2026-05-01
Owner: Investigation workflow
Scope: explain — conservatively — the statutory/procedural machinery that produced the Apr 25 1969 secrecy order on application **756,124** (issued as **US 3,647,970** on Mar 7 1972) and identify the document classes and custodians whose certified records are required to close the agency-attribution question (DIA vs NSA vs other defense entity).

This file is a **procedural framework only**. It does not assert which agency referred the secrecy action. The Patent Office form scans visible in the public PDF (E-016 in `index.md`) bear only Patent Office signatures. Agency attribution remains a Begich/NEXUS-tradition narrative claim, not official proof.

## 1) Statutory basis (35 U.S.C. §§ 181-188)

35 U.S.C. § 181 is the operative statute cited on the apparent Notice of Allowability for application 756,124 (E-016 page 3). Under § 181:

- Whenever publication or disclosure of an invention by granting a patent might, in the opinion of the **head (or his designate) of the interested government department or agency**, be detrimental to the national security, the **Commissioner of Patents**, upon being so notified, **orders that the invention be kept secret** and **withholds the grant of a patent** for such period as the national interest requires.
- For applications owned by parties other than the U.S. Government, the Commissioner makes the application available to the **Atomic Energy Commission**, the **Secretary of Defense**, and the chief officer of any other department or agency designated by the President as a defense agency of the United States.
- A defense agency receiving such an application provides the Commissioner with a written notice as to whether publication or disclosure would be detrimental, and the Commissioner enters acknowledgment of the receipt and disposition of the application in the application file.
- A secrecy order remains in effect for one year and may be renewed.

Public-source statute text used here:

- 35 U.S.C. § 181 (current codification): <https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title35-section181&num=0&edition=prelim>

The Apr 25 1969 form on E-016 page 2 cites the **1952** edition of Title 35, §§ 181-188 — the same statutory chapter, in the codification in force at the time of the order.

### Why this matters for 756,124

- The statute identifies **defense agencies** generally, not a specific agency, as the source of the "interested" recommendation.
- The Commissioner is the issuing authority on the order itself; this matches the E-016 page 2 form, which is signed by **Edwin L. Reynolds, First Assistant Commissioner**, on Patent Office form **POL-96 (7-66)**.
- Statutorily, "acknowledgment of the receipt and disposition" is **entered in the application file**. Under the statute, the file wrapper for serial 756,124 is therefore the canonical place where the **identity of the recommending defense agency** would have been recorded.

## 2) USPTO process (MPEP 120 / 37 CFR 5.2)

MPEP § 120 — "Secrecy Orders" — and the implementing regulation 37 CFR 5.2 describe the operational sequence:

1. The Commissioner issues a secrecy order **after notice** from the chief officer of a designated defense agency that publication or disclosure of the application would be detrimental to the national security.
2. The order prohibits publication or disclosure of the application and withholds it from issue.
3. The applicant may petition for **modification or rescission** of the order; petitions are coordinated with the **sponsoring agency**, which can be contacted in connection with the petition.
4. The order is reviewed annually for renewal; rescission is recorded in the application file when the national-security interest no longer requires withholding.

Public-source MPEP excerpt used here:

- MPEP § 120, "Secrecy Orders": <https://www.uspto.gov/web/offices/pac/mpep/s120.html>

### Why this matters for 756,124

- The MPEP makes explicit that the **sponsoring agency** is identifiable and contactable by the Commissioner. That identification ordinarily lives in the file wrapper / Licensing & Review correspondence.
- The Jan 11 1971 Notice of Allowability on E-016 page 3 ("withheld from issue during such period as the national interest requires" / unclassified correspondence stamp) is consistent with MPEP § 120 handling of an application held under an active order even after being adjudged allowable.
- A **petition to rescind** (or any defense-agency concurrence to lifting the order before Mar 7 1972) would be the document class most likely to name the sponsoring agency in plain text.

## 3) DoD coordination via ASPAB (FAS Secrecy Order Program / Administration)

The Federation of American Scientists (FAS) "Secrecy Order Program" and "Administration of the Invention Secrecy Act" pages summarize the Patent Office / DoD interface in force during the late 1960s and early 1970s:

- The Patent Office makes private (non-government-owned) applications available to defense agencies for review under § 181.
- DoD referrals are coordinated through the **Armed Services Patent Advisory Board (ASPAB)**.
- ASPAB is described as the **sole DoD authority** for making secrecy-order recommendations — i.e., for transmitting DoD's "interested agency" notice to the Commissioner.
- Operationally, applications flagged for review at the USPTO are routed via **Group 220 / Licensing & Review**, which physically holds the file at the PTO and forwards microfiche copies to the relevant defense agencies for their classified review.
- Defense agencies (including DIA, NSA, and the military departments) review the microfiche copies and return their concurrence (or non-concurrence) to ASPAB, which then issues a unified DoD recommendation to the Commissioner.

Public-source FAS pages used here:

- FAS, "Invention Secrecy Order Program": <https://sgp.fas.org/othergov/invention/program.html>
- FAS, "Administration of the Invention Secrecy Act": <https://sgp.fas.org/othergov/invention/admin.html>

### Why this matters for 756,124

- If the Apr 25 1969 order on serial 756,124 was a **DoD-channel** order, ASPAB is the **sole DoD authority** that would have transmitted the recommendation to the Commissioner. ASPAB records (and any DIA or NSA concurrence routed through ASPAB) are the document class that would resolve DIA vs NSA attribution.
- The **physical file wrapper** sits at PTO / Licensing & Review (then Group 220). The microfiche review copies sit (or sat) at the reviewing defense agencies. **Both** custody chains can in principle be requested via certified-copy and FOIA processes.
- The official Patent Office secrecy-order form (E-016 page 2) and Notice of Allowability (E-016 page 3) **never name an agency**, by design — agency identity lives in the ASPAB / Licensing & Review correspondence layer, not on the order form.

A modern, public-facing analog of this DoD review function is the Defense Technology Security Administration (DTSA) "Patent Security Reviews" page:

- DTSA, "Patent Security Reviews": <https://www.dtsa.mil/SitePages/assessing-and-managing-risk/patent-security-reviews.aspx>

DTSA is referenced **only as a modern analog** of the DoD-side review function. It is **not** the entity that handled application 756,124 in 1969-1972; ASPAB is.

## 4) Why the technology fits NSA-cryptology / communications-security review (PSCRL Group IX / 6A)

The **Patent Security Category Review List (PSCRL)** dated **January 1971** (the codified DoD review-screening list in force at the time the Jan 11 1971 Notice of Allowability was mailed for serial 756,124) identifies categories of inventions whose disclosure is presumptively detrimental to national security and which are routed for specific defense-agency review.

Within the PSCRL, **Group IX / 6A** covers, in summary:

- **NSA-cryptology / communications security** subject matter, including:
  - **voice privacy** and **speech secrecy** systems (analog and digitized)
  - **pseudo-random digit / sequence** generation for use in **digital messages** and **digitized voice**
  - **low-detectability** and **spread-spectrum** communications

Public-source PSCRL used here:

- PSCRL (Jan 1971), via FAS: <https://sgp.fas.org/othergov/invention/pscrl.pdf>

### Why this matters for 756,124

- The application's USPTO-public title is **"METHOD AND SYSTEM FOR SIMPLIFYING SPEECH WAVEFORMS"** (E-016 page 2), and the issued patent's **abstract** describes **"a system for transmitting secure speech information"** (E-016 page 4 and the Google Patents record below).
- This subject matter — **secure speech / digitized voice / waveform transformation** — is **directly inside PSCRL Group IX / 6A**'s NSA-cryptology / communications-security category.
- The PSCRL fit therefore explains **why a Patent Office reviewer in late 1968 / early 1969 would have referred the application out for defense-agency screening**, and **why an NSA-channel review (or ASPAB referral that included NSA concurrence) is procedurally plausible**.
- It does **not** prove NSA sponsored the secrecy order. The PSCRL only describes the **screening category**, not which agency ultimately concurred or recommended issuance of the order. ASPAB and other defense agencies (including DIA, the military departments, and the Office of the Secretary of Defense) could have concurred or recommended through the same channel.

The Begich/NEXUS narrative attribution (DIA) and the Flanagan first-person narrative attribution (NSA) are therefore **both compatible** with PSCRL Group IX / 6A screening, because PSCRL screening determines **review routing**, not **recommending agency**.

## 5) Archival custody and request paths (NARA / USPTO)

To close the agency-attribution question, the documents required are not on the order form itself but in the **file wrapper**, **Licensing & Review correspondence**, and **ASPAB / defense-agency referral or concurrence papers**.

### 5.1 NARA RG 241 — USPTO national-security patent application files

NARA records schedule **NC1-241-76-01** describes USPTO "national security patent application files." Per the schedule, such files **may include** material such as:

- the underlying patent application,
- the **request from the defense agency** for secrecy treatment,
- the **secrecy order** itself,
- internal USPTO and inter-agency **memoranda** and **concurrences**,
- petitions, renewals, and rescission correspondence.

The schedule also indicates these files **may be destroyed after a fixed retention** (commonly cited as 35 years from final action), so survival of any specific file is **not guaranteed** — survival must be confirmed by direct inquiry against the surviving NARA accession.

Public source:

- NARA SF-115 NC1-241-76-01: <https://www.archives.gov/files/records-mgmt/rcs/schedules/departments/department-of-commerce/rg-0241/nc1-241-76-01_sf115.pdf>

### 5.2 NARA RG 241 — broader USPTO patent case files schedule

NARA records schedule **N1-241-10-001** covers the broader USPTO patent case-file series (Record Group 241). The patent case file for **US 3,647,970** itself is the operational target for ordinary patent prosecution material; the **file wrapper** for application **756,124** is the operational target for secrecy-order correspondence retained in the prosecution file.

Public sources:

- NARA SF-115 N1-241-10-001: <https://www.archives.gov/files/records-mgmt/rcs/schedules/departments/department-of-commerce/rg-0241/n1-241-10-001_sf115.pdf>
- NARA "Records of the Patent and Trademark Office": <https://www.archives.gov/research/patents>

The relevant patent case-file accession band (already recorded as E-006 in `index.md`) is **`Patent Case Files (#3,647,069 - 3,651,016); ENTRY UD13W 12 (NAID 302050)`**, held at NARA Kansas City.

### 5.3 USPTO certified-copy and file-wrapper paths

USPTO maintains two operational channels for prosecution-record retrieval that are useful here:

- USPTO update on paper file-wrapper requests: <https://www.uspto.gov/about-us/news-updates/update-paper-patent-file-wrapper-requests>
- USPTO order page for certified copies: <https://www.uspto.gov/patents/apply/checking-application-status/order-certified-copies>
- USPTO Certified Copy Center FAQ: <https://www.uspto.gov/learning-and-resources/frequently-asked-questions-about-certified-copy-center>

A **certified copy of the file wrapper for application serial 756,124**, plus any **Licensing & Review correspondence** retained in the wrapper, is the single highest-value document class for the closure question, because that is where § 181 statutorily requires the Commissioner to "enter acknowledgment of the receipt and disposition" of the application by defense agencies.

### 5.4 NSA-side records (RG 457) — only if NSA-channel review

If — and only if — the secrecy order was reviewed via an NSA-channel (e.g., ASPAB referral with NSA concurrence, or direct § 181 notice from NSA), responsive NSA records may exist in **RG 457** under NSA's records schedule:

- NARA SF-115 N1-457-07-001 (NSA records schedule): <https://www.archives.gov/files/records-mgmt/rcs/schedules/departments/department-of-defense/defense-agencies/rg-0457/n1-457-07-001_sf115.pdf>

This is a **conditional** path: until certified file-wrapper / ASPAB records identify the recommending agency, an NSA RG 457 inquiry is speculative. The NSA route is recorded here as a possible custody chain to query, **not** as evidence of NSA sponsorship.

For historical context only (not Flanagan-specific): an NSA-declassified document on Patent Office Secrecy Orders dated 1948 (referencing the predecessor Army-Navy Patent Advisory Board / Signal Corps cryptology review function) is publicly available:

- NSA, "Patent Office Secrecy Orders" (1948, declassified): <https://www.nsa.gov/portals/75/documents/news-features/declassified-documents/friedman-documents/patent-equipment/folder_527/41771019081010.pdf>

This 1948 document is cited **only** to illustrate that Patent-Office secrecy-order review by communications-security / cryptology entities long predates 1969. It is **not** evidence about application 756,124 specifically.

## 6) What this framework does and does not establish

**Does establish (procedurally):**

- The order on application 756,124 — whose existence and dates are supported by the public PDF form scans (E-016) — sits inside a well-defined statutory and procedural framework: **35 U.S.C. §§ 181-188**, **MPEP § 120 / 37 CFR 5.2**, and (on the DoD side) **ASPAB** as the sole DoD authority for secrecy-order recommendations.
- The application's subject matter (secure speech / waveform simplification, with H04K secret-communications classifications) sits **inside PSCRL Group IX / 6A** (NSA-cryptology / communications-security category), which makes routing through a communications-security / cryptology review **procedurally plausible**.
- The custody chain for the documents that would identify the sponsoring agency runs through the **PTO file wrapper** (Licensing & Review / former Group 220), **NARA RG 241** (USPTO national-security patent application files under NC1-241-76-01 and the broader patent case files under N1-241-10-001), **ASPAB referral correspondence**, and — only if NSA-channel review occurred — **NARA RG 457**.

**Does not establish:**

- Which specific agency (DIA, NSA, OSD, or other) referred or recommended the secrecy order on serial 756,124.
- Whether the Begich/NEXUS DIA-attribution tradition or the Flanagan first-person NSA-attribution tradition is correct.
- That a sponsoring-agency referral memo for 756,124 has survived in any specific NARA accession.

Closure of the agency-attribution question requires both:

1. **A certified USPTO/NARA file wrapper** for application serial 756,124 (with Licensing & Review correspondence intact).
2. **Any surviving D-1 / Group 220 / Licensing & Review or ASPAB referral / concurrence papers** identifying which defense entity referred the secrecy action to the Commissioner.

Until both are obtained, agency attribution remains a **source-tradition claim**, not official proof.
