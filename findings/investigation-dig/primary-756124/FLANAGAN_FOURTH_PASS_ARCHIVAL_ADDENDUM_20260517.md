The secrecy-order claim for Flanagan’s later speech-waveform patent is now better supported than before, but it is still not fully primary-validated. A hosted PDF titled “PAT FLANAGAN High Security Speech Scrambler” contains a reproduced Department of Commerce Patent Office secrecy-order text block for **Serial No. 756,124**, filed **Aug. 29, 1968**, applicant **Gillis P. Flanagan**, title **Method and System for Simplifying Speech Waveforms** ([hosted PDF](https://img1.wsimg.com/blobby/go/a56ed7e4-a072-4dc8-b939-26ab140309de/downloads/Patrick%20Flanagan%20High%20Security%20Speech%20Scramble.pdf?ver=1685486536436)). The clinical trail is now more balanced. PubMed confirms the original Puharich/Lawrence 1969 article as a clinical trial titled “Hearing rehabilitation by means of transdermal electrotherapy in human hearing loss of sensorineural origin. Preliminary report,” published in **Acta Otolaryngologica 67(1):69-83**, with PMID **4906301** and DOI **10.3109/00016486909124370** ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4906301/)). JAMA’s 1974 “Transdermal Electrostimulation in Sensory Hearing Loss” reported placebo-controlled improvements of **16% to 25%** in W-22 scores for **35% to 40%** of subjects’ better ears, while a same-issue double-blind JAMA study by Glattke and Simmons found **no discernible treatment effects** and no significant differences between treated and control groups ([JAMA positive study](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamaotolaryngology/article-abstract/605317), [JAMA negative study](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamaotolaryngology/fullarticle/605313)). A 1975 Brooks/Harrison clinical trial exists on PubMed, but PubMed provides no abstract, so it is a bibliographic lead rather than usable outcome evidence at this stage ([PubMed Brooks/Harrison](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1092039/)).

## Evidence lane: Biolectron assignment cluster

Google Patents lists **Biolectron Inc.** as a current assignee for **US3393279A**, with Listening Inc. as original assignee, and its legal-events table records a **1984-06-04** assignment to Biolectron, Inc., 200 Holt St., Hackensack, NJ, with free-format text “ASSIGNMENT OF ASSIGNORS INTEREST.; ASSIGNOR: CHEN, C.Y.; REEL/FRAME:004271/0859” and effective date **19840530** ([US3393279A](https://patents.google.com/patent/US3393279A/en)). The same reel/frame, assignor, assignee, and effective date appear on Puharich/Lawrence **US3563246A**, where Google Patents lists Henry K. Puharich and Joseph L. Lawrence as inventors and Intelectron Corp. as original assignee ([US3563246A](https://patents.google.com/patent/US3563246A/en)). The same assignment also appears on **US3629521A**, which is another Puharich/Lawrence hearing-system patent in the same Intelectron/Biolectron orbit ([US3629521A](https://patents.google.com/patent/US3629521A/en)).

This does not prove that Chen was the original inventor, nor that Biolectron owned the rights continuously from issuance. It does show that, as of a 1984-recorded assignment event, Flanagan’s **US3393279A** and at least two Puharich/Lawrence patents were bundled under the same assignment reel/frame into Biolectron. The legal significance is narrower but important: it supports a later portfolio-consolidation path linking Listening/Intelectron assets, Puharich/Lawrence electrotherapy patents, and Flanagan’s Neurophone patent through Biolectron rather than proving the original 1960s ownership chain.

## Evidence lane: speech-waveform secrecy order

The later Flanagan speech-waveform patent is distinct from the original high-voltage/radio-frequency Neurophone patent. Google Patents identifies **US3647970A** as “Method and system for simplifying speech waveforms,” with inventor **Gillis P. Flanagan**, application **US756124A**, and filing date **Aug. 29, 1968** ([US3647970A](https://patents.google.com/patent/US3647970A/en)). The hosted “High Security Speech Scrambler” PDF reproduces a Patent Office secrecy-order text block with the same serial, applicant, filing date, and title, and its notice states that the application was found to contain subject matter whose unauthorized disclosure might be detrimental to national security ([hosted PDF](https://img1.wsimg.com/blobby/go/a56ed7e4-a072-4dc8-b939-26ab140309de/downloads/Patrick%20Flanagan%20High%20Security%20Speech%20Scramble.pdf?ver=1685486536436)).

That combination is materially stronger than a generic memoir claim because the reproduced order’s metadata aligns with an issued patent record. However, the PDF is still a secondary hosted copy, not an image recovered directly from USPTO, NARA, or a Federal Register file. The correct evidentiary label is therefore: **credible secondary document image aligned to official patent metadata; primary record not yet recovered**.

The legal mechanics are plausible under the Invention Secrecy Act framework. USPTO’s MPEP states that when a defense-agency chief notifies the Commissioner that patent publication or disclosure would be detrimental to national security, a secrecy order is issued by the Commissioner for Patents ([USPTO MPEP 120](https://www.uspto.gov/web/offices/pac/mpep/s120.html)). The statutory text of 35 U.S.C. Chapter 17 allows such orders, limits them to renewable one-year periods outside wartime or national emergency contexts, and allows rescission when the relevant agencies no longer deem publication detrimental to national security ([35 U.S.C. Chapter 17 mirror](https://sgp.fas.org/othergov/invention/35usc17.html)). FAS’s invention-secrecy archive explains that secrecy orders can apply even to privately generated applications without government sponsorship, which fits the form of the claim but does not independently validate Flanagan’s specific order ([FAS invention secrecy archive](https://sgp.fas.org/othergov/invention/index.html)).

The FAS spreadsheet of secrecy orders rescinded with patents issued for **1982-1988** does not include application **756124** or patent **3647970**, which is unsurprising if the Flanagan order was rescinded around 1972 and the patent issued in March 1972, outside that spreadsheet’s coverage window ([FAS rescind spreadsheet](https://sgp.fas.org/othergov/invention/rescind.xls), [US3647970A](https://patents.google.com/patent/US3647970A/en)). The absence from that later spreadsheet should not be treated as disproof; it only means the fourth pass did not find an official rescinded-order list covering the claimed 1968-1972 period.

## Evidence lane: clinical hearing-therapy literature

The Puharich/Lawrence clinical claim has a verified peer-reviewed bibliographic anchor. PubMed lists the 1969 paper as a clinical trial and controlled clinical trial, authored by **H.K. Puharich** and **J.L. Lawrence**, published in **Acta Otolaryngologica** in January 1969, with no abstract available in PubMed ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4906301/)). Because the abstract is unavailable, the fourth pass cannot independently evaluate the article’s sample size, protocol, endpoints, or claimed effect from PubMed alone.

The follow-up literature is mixed, which is analytically important. JAMA’s “Transdermal Electrostimulation in Sensory Hearing Loss” reports that a pilot study found W-22 gains in a socially relevant speech-reception range and that two placebo-controlled experiments produced statistically significant **16% to 25%** W-22 improvements for **35% to 40%** of subjects’ better ears, while pure-tone average was unchanged and improvements were usually lost after three to four months unless maintained by a home unit ([JAMA positive study](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamaotolaryngology/article-abstract/605317)). In contrast, Glattke and Simmons’ “Transdermal Therapy and Monosyllabic Word Discrimination” studied 31 hard-of-hearing subjects in a double-blind design and reported that no treatment effects were discernible and no significant differences could be found between control and treated groups ([JAMA negative study](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamaotolaryngology/fullarticle/605313)).

PubMed also confirms a 1975 Brooks/Harrison clinical trial titled “An investigation of the efficacy of transdermal therapy on sensori-neural hypacusis,” published in **Trans Sect Otolaryngol Am Acad Ophthalmol Otolaryngol** at **80(1):79-84**, with PMID **1092039**, but PubMed provides no abstract and therefore no directly usable outcome statement ([PubMed Brooks/Harrison](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1092039/)). The best current characterization is that transdermal/electrostimulation hearing therapy generated real clinical literature in the late 1960s and 1970s, including controlled trials, but the publicly accessible abstracts show inconsistent outcomes and do not establish durable hearing restoration.

## Evidence lane: LIFE article and press provenance

The LIFE article pathway is still partly archival rather than fully primary-text verified. The strongest easily accessible article transcript remains PhiSciences’ page identifying the LIFE Magazine feature as **“Whiz Kid, Hands Down,” September 14, 1962**, by **William Moeser**, with text describing Pat Flanagan as a 17-year-old Bellaire, Texas inventor whose machine might help deaf people hear and blind people “see” ([PhiSciences transcript/scan page](https://www.phisciences.com/pages/dr-patrick-flanagan-life-magazine-article)). Google Books confirms a LIFE issue record for September 1962 issue identifiers, but the fourth pass did not recover a stable page-image citation for the exact article spread from Google Books ([Google Books LIFE record](https://books.google.com/books/about/LIFE.html?hl=en&id=z00EAAAAMBAJ)).

The correct repo status should therefore remain conservative. The article likely exists and is widely mirrored with consistent bibliographic details, but the investigation should not yet cite a Google Books page image as if it had been recovered. The next target is a page-resolved Google Books image, Internet Archive scan, library database copy, or direct LIFE archive clipping that shows the page spread and masthead.
