## C. The Three-Stage Legal Kill Chain

Every federal TI case follows an identical procedural trajectory:

### Stage 1: IFP Screening (28 USC 1915(e)(2))

- 72% of TI plaintiffs file in forma pauperis (cannot afford filing fees)
- Filing IFP triggers mandatory judicial screening before the case proceeds
- The standard: dismiss if the complaint is "frivolous or malicious" or "fails to state a claim"
- Under *Denton v. Hernandez* (1992, SCOTUS), allegations that are "fantastic or delusional" can be dismissed without adversarial process
- Courts routinely classify directed energy weapons, V2K, and remote neural monitoring as "fantastic or delusional"
- The defendant is never served. No response is required. No discovery occurs.

**This is the mechanism that killed Akwei's case in 17 days.** Judge Sporkin applied 28 USC 1915(d) (the pre-1996 version) to dismiss without requiring the NSA to respond.

### Stage 2: Plausibility Screening (Twombly/Iqbal)

For cases that survive IFP screening (typically those filed by plaintiffs who can afford filing fees):

- *Bell Atlantic v. Twombly* (2007) and *Ashcroft v. Iqbal* (2009) require "factual allegations" that make a claim "plausible on its face"
- A plaintiff alleging classified surveillance cannot provide factual allegations about classified programs without access to classified information
- Courts distinguish between "factual allegations" (accepted as true) and "conclusory statements" (disregarded)
- Claims about V2K, RNM, and directed energy are treated as conclusory regardless of supporting patent citations or published research

### Stage 3: Immunity Barriers

For the rare case that survives both screenings:

- **Sovereign immunity:** Federal agencies cannot be sued without consent; the Federal Tort Claims Act excludes intentional torts by federal employees acting within scope
- **Qualified immunity:** Individual federal agents are immune unless they violated "clearly established" rights -- no court has "clearly established" a right against directed energy targeting
- **Post-*Egbert v. Boule* (2022):** Bivens actions (suing individual federal agents) are effectively closed in any national security context; "special factors" automatically counsel against new causes of action
- **State secrets privilege:** If discovery is somehow reached, the government can invoke state secrets to block disclosure of classified programs; post-*FBI v. Fazaga* (2022), even FISA does not override this privilege

**Result: Every legal pathway is procedurally blocked.**

- Cannot sue the agency (sovereign immunity)
- Cannot sue individual agents (qualified immunity + Egbert)
- Cannot compel disclosure of classified programs (state secrets)
- Cannot survive IFP screening (Denton "delusional" standard)
- Cannot meet plausibility standard without classified evidence (Twombly/Iqbal)
- Cannot access FISA Court proceedings (no standing for private citizens)
- Cannot benefit from HAVANA Act (civilian exclusion -- see Section G)

## D. Landmark Cases -- Detailed Analysis

### D1. Akwei v. NSA (1992) -- The Template Case

- **Case:** Civil Action 1:92-cv-00449-SS, U.S. District Court D.C.
- **Filed:** February 20, 1992
- **Dismissed:** March 9, 1992 (17 days)
- **Judge:** Stanley Sporkin (CIA General Counsel 1981-1986)
- **Standard:** 28 USC 1915(d) -- "unable to construe this complaint as stating any identifiable legal cause of action"
- **Process:** No service on NSA, no response required, no discovery, no hearing
- **Significance:** First federal case alleging remote neural monitoring and V2K by the NSA. Established the dismissal template replicated in every subsequent case. The 1996 Nexus Magazine dossier became the foundational reference document for all subsequent TI litigation (confirmed by Banner v. NSA, 2011, which attached the dossier as evidence).

### D2. Walbert v. Redford (2008) -- The Only Federal-Level Victory

- **Case:** 08DM8647, Sedgwick County District Court, Kansas
- **Date:** December 30, 2008
- **Judges:** Pullman and Beasley
- **Outcome:** Protection order granted banning "electronic means" of harassment

**Why this case succeeded (five necessary conditions):**

1. **Physical evidence:** TSCM expert William Taylor performed spectral analysis and detected a 1 GHz carrier signal with 10-nanosecond pulses directed at Walbert's location
2. **Medical evidence:** Dr. Hildegarde Staninger's toxicology reports identified RFID implants; MRI scans showed foreign bodies
3. **Political support:** Missouri State Representative Jim Guest (R-King City, former aerospace engineer) provided formal support letter
4. **Local court:** State court -- no sovereign immunity, no state secrets privilege, no IFP screening
5. **Private defendant:** Jeremiah Redford was a private individual, not a federal agency; he was absent from the hearing

**Aftermath:** Walbert sued the Wichita Police Department (Case 6:2010cv01234) for failure to enforce the protection order. That federal case was dismissed on June 21, 2011. The local court order succeeded; the federal follow-up failed -- confirming the jurisdictional pattern.

### D3. Watterson v. Aro (2014) -- The Partial Victory

- **Case:** CIVMS1400123, San Bernardino County Superior Court, Joshua Tree
- **Expert:** Levi McCann (RF spectrum analysis, tri-field meters)
- **Evidence:** Defendant's satellite dishes pointed at Watterson's house rather than at satellites (physically anomalous orientation)
- **Judge Gafkowski's statement:** Acknowledged there was "no doubt in my mind she has suffered physical ailment because of these microwaves"
- **Outcome:** Judge **denied** the permanent restraining order, finding negligence rather than intentional harassment

**Note:** The TI community widely cites this as a victory. It was not -- the permanent order was denied. However, the judge's on-record acknowledgment of microwave-caused physical harm is significant as judicial recognition that the phenomenon is real.

### D4. Starrett v. Lockheed Martin (2018, Fifth Circuit, 18-40567)

- **Original:** 3:17-cv-00988
- **Plaintiff:** William Henry Starrett Jr.
- **149-page complaint** naming Lockheed Martin, DARPA, Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, DOD, and Army Special Operations
- **Claims:** V2K, Remote Neural Monitoring, mind control conspiracy
- **Dismissed as:** "Fanciful, fantastic, or delusional"
- **Significance:** Affirmed on appeal by the Fifth Circuit. This opinion became a cited precedent in the Targeted Justice case, establishing that V2K/RNM claims are per se "delusional" in the Fifth Circuit.

### D5. Targeted Justice v. Garland (2023-2024) -- The Organized Effort

- **Cases:** 6:23-cv-00003 and 4:23-cv-01013, S.D. Texas
- **Appeal:** Fifth Circuit, 23-20342
- **Plaintiffs:** Targeted Justice Inc. and 18 individual plaintiffs
- **Defendants:** Merrick Garland, Christopher Wray, Alejandro Mayorkas, and federal agencies (FBI, DHS)
- **Claims:** Directed energy weapons, V2K, wrongful TSDB (Terrorist Screening Database) handling codes 3 and 4 inclusion, constitutional violations, APA violations
- **Sought:** Declaratory and injunctive relief, mandamus, damages
- **District court:** Dismissed constitutional and APA claims for lack of jurisdiction, failure to state a claim; denied preliminary injunction; stayed Privacy Act claims for administrative exhaustion
- **Fifth Circuit (March 8, 2024):** Affirmed, calling claims "wholly insubstantial, obviously frivolous"
- **SCOTUS:** Cert petition filed (No. 24-91) -- outcome pending as of March 2026

This was the most organized, best-resourced TI legal effort to date -- an incorporated organization with 18 co-plaintiffs suing named federal officials. It failed at every level through the Fifth Circuit. The SCOTUS petition is the last remaining avenue.

### D6. Lamb v. DOD and CIA (2024, D. Arizona, 2:24-cv-00345)

- **Plaintiff:** Andrew Joseph Lamb
- **Claims:** V2K mind control since childhood
- **Technical specificity:** Explicitly cited the Frey Effect (1962) and USAF patent US 6,587,729 (apparatus for RF hearing effect)
- **Personal detail:** Noted his adoption near China Lake Naval Research Base
- **Outcome:** Dismissed as frivolous

**Significance for this investigation:** A 2024 plaintiff citing the identical patent chain documented in Files 04, 06, and 17 of this investigation -- and receiving the identical result Akwei received in 1992. The legal system's response has not evolved in 32 years despite the technology evidence accumulating continuously.

### D7. Banner v. NSA (2011, N.D. Florida, 3:11-cv-00120)

- Pro se plaintiff alleged "brain zapping" and sought $7 billion in damages

- Dismissed for lack of jurisdiction
- **Significance:** Confirms that Akwei's dossier circulated as the foundational reference document for TI litigation. The document Sporkin dismissed in 17 days became the primary source cited by subsequent plaintiffs over the next two decades.

## F. State Legislation -- The Contradiction

Three states have enacted statutes criminalizing electronic weapons:

### Michigan MCL 750.200h (Public Act 257, 2003)

- Criminalizes "harmful electronic or electromagnetic devices"
- Penalties: up to 7 years imprisonment; life imprisonment for devices causing serious injury or death
- Most aggressive electronic weapons statute in the US

### Maine Title 17-A Section 1004 (Public Law 264, 2005)

- Covers "electronic weapons" producing a "disabling effect" via "wave or beam"
- Penalties: fines and imprisonment
- Specific to directed energy weapons

### Massachusetts MGL Chapter 265 Section 43A (2010)

- General criminal harassment statute
- Does NOT specifically address electronic weapons despite TI community claims
- Broader application but lacks the specificity of Michigan and Maine statutes

**The contradiction:** State legislatures have acknowledged directed energy weapons as sufficiently real to criminalize their use. Federal courts simultaneously classify claims about the same technology as "fantastic or delusional" and dismiss them without hearing. These two positions cannot both be correct. No court has addressed this internal contradiction.

## I. The Legal Architecture as a Classification Protection System

Reading the Bales timeline in its entirety reveals that the legal system functions as an automated classification protection mechanism:

1. **The technology is classified** (NSA, DOD programs)
2. **Plaintiffs cannot cite classified evidence** in complaints
3. **Without classified evidence, claims appear speculative** to judges
4. **Speculative claims trigger "frivolous" dismissal** under 1915(e)(2) or 12(b)(6)
5. **Dismissal occurs before discovery** can be requested
6. **State secrets privilege never needs to be invoked** because the case never reaches that stage
7. **The classified program is never examined by any court**
8. **The dismissal itself becomes precedent** (e.g., Starrett cited in Targeted Justice)
9. **Accumulating dismissals reinforce the "delusional" classification**
10. **Return to step 1**

This is not a conspiracy -- it is a structural feature of the interaction between classification law and civil procedure. Each component (IFP screening, Twombly/Iqbal, sovereign immunity, qualified immunity, state secrets) was designed for legitimate purposes. But in combination, applied to claims about classified technology, they create an impenetrable procedural barrier that protects classified programs from judicial review without any actor in the system intending that specific outcome.

Judge Sporkin may have intended to protect the NSA in 1992. But the 120+ judges who dismissed subsequent cases did not need to have any intent -- the procedural machinery did the work automatically.

## L. Source Limitations

The Bales timeline has specific limitations noted by the author:

- Many placeholder cases (e.g., "TI Plaintiff v. Federal Agency #1-#113") lack case numbers and cannot be independently verified
- The total count (177-201) is an estimate based on Targeted Justice's archive, which may include overlapping or misreported cases
- Masters v. Runnels ($6.5M jury award) is mischaracterized -- it was an excessive force case involving a Taser, not a DEW/V2K case
- Massachusetts MGL 265 Section 43A is described as an electronic harassment statute but is actually a general criminal harassment statute
- The author (Paul Michael Bales) is an independent researcher who self-identifies as a Targeted Individual; his work is not peer-reviewed

These limitations affect the precision of the case count but do not affect the core analytical finding: the near-100% federal dismissal rate for electronic harassment claims is documented across independently verifiable named cases regardless of the placeholder count.
