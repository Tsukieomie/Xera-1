## A2. THERMAL VS NON-THERMAL HYPOTHESES (CITATION HARMONIZATION)

This file cites both **window / modulation-sensitive** laboratory work and **high-exposure animal oncology** programs. Those lines of evidence answer different questions and are debated on **different mechanism axes**. The labels below keep citations aligned with how mainstream biophysics and regulatory reviews usually partition claims.

**Thermal hypothesis (heating pathway).** Absorbed RF power raises tissue temperature. Injury and carcinogenesis in animal models at high whole-body or local SAR are often discussed in terms of **chronic tissue heating**, stress responses, and thermal confounders. Many safety limits (e.g., IEEE/ICNIRP-style SAR caps) are explicitly grounded in **avoiding hazardous temperature rise**, not in ruling out other mechanisms.

**Non-thermal hypothesis (field-coupling pathway).** Reported bioeffects occur at **exposure metrics** where bulk temperature rise is absent, clamped, or argued to be insufficient to explain the **magnitude or specificity** of the endpoint (e.g., narrow-band modulation windows, altered ion flux in isolated preparations). These claims are **contested**: independent replication, exposure dosimetry, and blinded protocols vary sharply across studies.

**Mixed / unresolved (common in genotoxicity and cancer bioassays).** Studies such as NTP/Ramazzini report **oncologic endpoints** under defined exposure regimes. Open literature disagrees whether observed DNA damage or tumors are best attributed to **thermal stress**, **oxidative stress with or without dominating heating**, measurement artifacts, or **field-specific non-thermal pathways**. Regulatory bodies sometimes accept the **endpoint** findings while rejecting a single canonical non-thermal mechanism.
