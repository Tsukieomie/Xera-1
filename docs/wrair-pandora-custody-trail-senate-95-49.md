# Xera-1 WRAIR/PANDORA Custody-Trail Investigation

## Executive assessment

The image text is substantially supported as a custody-trail claim, not as proof that a standalone Sharp/Grove speech-transmission report has been located. The public record supports that Project PANDORA existed, was classified, involved WRAIR, generated meeting minutes and technical reports, and had assets transferred to WRAIR after DARPA terminated the project in March 1970 ([DARPA letter via National Security Archive](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/sites/default/files/documents/rhwvuj-tzy4b/14.pdf); [Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). The public record also supports that the Senate committee was specifically pursuing whether PANDORA raw data had been destroyed or still existed, why destruction may have occurred, and what routine destruction procedures applied to such data ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)).

Direct review of Senate Serial No. 95-49 materially strengthens the custody-trail side of the Xera-1 theory. The hearing appendix contains “Unclassified documents pertaining to Project Pandora,” including committee questions to DARPA about raw data, classification, destruction, and current Walter Reed facilities, followed by DARPA’s answer that it had “Minutes of Pandora Meetings” and several technical reports that had been declassified, while lacking knowledge about whether data collected at Walter Reed had been destroyed ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). The same appendix says Project PANDORA was terminated by DARPA in March 1970 and that all assets were transferred to Walter Reed Army Institute for Research for disposition, directly confirming the WRAIR custody node that the image emphasized ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)).

The Sharp/Grove speech claim remains publicly traceable to Don R. Justesen’s 1975 article, where the underlying Sharp/Grove source is listed as “Personal communication, September 28, 1973,” rather than a published Sharp/Grove protocol or WRAIR report ([Justesen, “Microwaves and Behavior”](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)). Senate Serial No. 95-49 does include Joseph C. Sharp and H. Mark Grove in the PANDORA record, but the relevant passages tie them to PANDORA primate-behavior analysis, WRAIR special-signal work, facility briefings, and physical-measurement discussions rather than to the voice-modulated speech demonstration described by Justesen ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)).

## Direct Senate findings

Senate Serial No. 95-49 adds the strongest primary-source confirmation so far for the “record-custody trail” interpretation. The table of contents lists “Unclassified documents pertaining to Project Pandora” at the end of the hearing volume, and that section contains the DARPA question-and-answer sequence and reproduced PANDORA meeting materials that the later Senate report had pointed to ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). This matters because it moves the Serial No. 95-49 item from an inferred target to a reviewed primary source.

The appendix’s committee questions are unusually direct. They ask why Project PANDORA was classified, what it cost, what happened to raw data, whether relevant data had been destroyed or still existed, whether surviving data remained classified, why destruction may have occurred, and how often documents containing data such as PANDORA’s were routinely destroyed ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). DARPA answered that Project PANDORA cost $4,615,000, that technical researchers amassed the raw data, that only technical progress reports reflected what disposition was made of the raw data, and that DARPA had no knowledge whether the data collected at Walter Reed had been destroyed ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)).

The hearing directly confirms that there were declassified PANDORA meeting minutes and technical reports, but it does not disclose an itemized destruction inventory. DARPA stated that it had documents identified as “Minutes of Pandora Meetings” and several technical reports, and that those documents had been declassified; in the same answer, DARPA said PANDORA was terminated in March 1970 and that all assets were transferred to WRAIR for disposition ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). That combination is important: it confirms both the existence of PANDORA documentary products and the institutional transfer path, while preserving the unresolved gap around raw data and any itemized destruction records.

The reproduced PANDORA materials also confirm that Joseph C. Sharp, James McIlwain, Joseph Kubis, and H. Mark Grove are real participants or referenced personnel inside the PANDORA record. The hearing says preliminary analyses presented by Dr. Joseph Sharp of WRAIR in 1967 encouraged belief that the special signal altered primate behavior, that the PANDORA Scientific Advisory Committee requested fuller presentation of WRAIR data, that Dr. McIlwain later analyzed the material, and that Dr. Kubis evaluated procedures, protocols, data, and assumptions behind Sharp and McIlwain’s competing interpretations ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). The same materials list Mark Grove of Wright-Patterson AFB/AF Avionics Lab as a briefer or attendee in PANDORA/BIZARRE meetings, including facility and physical-measurement discussions ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)).

The strongest destruction-related language in the direct hearing review is narrower than the later 1979 Senate report’s destruction-record summary. In Serial No. 95-49, Dr. Pollack testified that actual data reviewed by the committee were not available and that he did not know what happened to them, while appendix correspondence states that data books had been destroyed and that major reports that may have been produced apparently reposed at WRAIR and had been destroyed ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). The later Senate report remains the stronger source for the specific claim that classified documents were destroyed in September 1973 and that records of what had been destroyed were provided to the committee in June 1978 ([Senate report via National Security Archive](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/sites/default/files/documents/rhwvuj-tzy4b/15.pdf)).

That structure matters because it prevents overclaiming: the repo does not treat a missing web article as the central problem; it treats the public Justesen citation, WRAIR/PANDORA custody nodes, Senate committee records, and destruction-record references as separate evidentiary strands.

The repository’s strongest conclusion is that the surrounding PANDORA/WRAIR record trail is real and documentable, while the Sharp/Grove speech-demonstration evidence remains weaker and citation-chain dependent. That framing now matches both the earlier external public documents and the directly reviewed Senate hearing: WRAIR’s role, the PANDORA asset transfer, and declassified meeting minutes are supported by primary records, while the speech claim is still directly supported only as a Justesen-published account based on a personal communication ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf); [DARPA letter via National Security Archive](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/sites/default/files/documents/rhwvuj-tzy4b/14.pdf); [Justesen, “Microwaves and Behavior”](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)).

## Key limitations

The direct Senate review does not locate the standalone Sharp/Grove speech-demonstration report. The hearing contains RF-hearing/Frey-effect and auditory-effect discussion elsewhere, and it contains PANDORA references to Sharp and Grove, but searches for “speech,” “receiverless,” “wireless,” and related speech-demonstration terms did not surface the Justesen-style Sharp/Grove speech claim in Serial No. 95-49 ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf); [Justesen, “Microwaves and Behavior”](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)). The hearing therefore strengthens the people-and-custody trail around Sharp and Grove but does not convert the speech claim from “personal communication” into a located technical report.

The direct Senate review also does not provide the itemized 1973 destruction inventory. Serial No. 95-49 shows the committee asking exactly the right questions and records DARPA’s lack of knowledge about whether Walter Reed data were destroyed, but the later Senate report remains the source that says classified PANDORA documents were destroyed in September 1973 and that remaining classified documents or records of destroyed documents were provided to the committee on June 29, 1978 ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf); [Senate report via National Security Archive](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/sites/default/files/documents/rhwvuj-tzy4b/15.pdf)). That makes the destruction-inventory problem more concrete but still unresolved.

WRAIR archive holdings remain a target, not a conclusion. WRAIR’s official archive page confirms a real archival repository and describes categories of historical and research material, but it does not by itself prove that the specific Sharp/Grove speech materials survive in accessible folders ([WRAIR Gorgas Memorial Library](https://wrair.health.mil/Library-Archival-Collection/)). The National Security Archive also describes the broader Moscow Signal/PANDORA record as incomplete, with major intelligence records still classified or unavailable and additional FOIA/declassification work ongoing ([National Security Archive](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/briefing-book/intelligence-russia-programs/2022-09-13/moscow-signals-declassified-microwave); [National Security Archive](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/briefing-book/intelligence-russia-programs/2022-09-15/moscow-signals-declassified-microwave)).

## Recommended next records

The highest-yield next step is now narrower than “review Senate Serial No. 95-49.” The specific follow-up should be a request for the underlying PANDORA raw data disposition records, the technical progress reports referenced by DARPA, and the WRAIR-held major reports/data books described in the appendix correspondence, because Serial No. 95-49 confirms those were the records whose custody or destruction was unclear ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). The WRAIR/Gorgas archive request should focus on Project PANDORA, Microwave Research Department, Microwave Research Program Review, Joseph C. Sharp, James McIlwain, H. Mark Grove, PANDORA SAC minutes, technical progress reports, and any 1973–1978 command or reprint files, because WRAIR is confirmed as the disposition point for transferred PANDORA assets and the archive is the official WRAIR historical repository ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf); [WRAIR Gorgas Memorial Library](https://wrair.health.mil/Library-Archival-Collection/)).

The second priority is a targeted FOIA or mandatory declassification request for the 1973–1974 destruction inventory and any records of destroyed PANDORA documents provided to the Senate committee on June 29, 1978. The request should cite both the direct Serial No. 95-49 questions about destruction and the later Senate report’s statement that records of destroyed documents were provided to the committee, because those two sources together show that the issue was contemporaneously documented and later narrowed to a specific committee-transfer event ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf); [Senate report via National Security Archive](https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/sites/default/files/documents/rhwvuj-tzy4b/15.pdf)).

The third priority is a DARPA/DTIC/WHS request for declassified “Minutes of Pandora Meetings,” PANDORA technical reports, technical progress reports, and records reflecting disposition of raw data. This request should quote DARPA’s own Serial No. 95-49 answer that it possessed minutes and technical reports and that raw data disposition was reflected only through technical progress reports, because that language identifies likely record series rather than relying on broad subject keywords ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)).

## Bottom line

The image’s core framing is right: the best current path is a records-custody investigation, not a hunt for a single vanished web page. Direct review of Senate Serial No. 95-49 confirms that the Senate record itself contains PANDORA questions, DARPA answers, declassified PANDORA meeting materials, WRAIR custody language, Sharp/McIlwain/Kubis analysis of WRAIR special-signal data, and Grove’s presence in PANDORA/BIZARRE facility and measurement discussions ([Senate Serial No. 95-49 via GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-95shrg96475/pdf/CHRG-95shrg96475.pdf)). The Sharp/Grove speech claim should still remain labeled “publicly attested by Justesen, sourced to a 1973 personal communication” until WRAIR archive folders, destruction records, Justesen correspondence, or a Sharp/Grove-authored technical report produce stronger primary evidence ([Justesen, “Microwaves and Behavior”](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)).
