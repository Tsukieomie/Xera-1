# Archival Research Notes: WRAIR WU 057, NSA Memorial, NorthWood Yearbooks, and 1980 Brain Research Leads

## Executive summary

The strongest confirmed lead is the WRAIR Work Unit 057 section in DTIC accession ADA009338, an annual progress report covering 1 July 1973 through 30 June 1974, where WU 057 is titled “Biological effects and hazards of microwave radiation” under Project 3A762758A824, Radiation Injury and Protection ([Internet Archive DTIC ADA009338](https://archive.org/details/DTIC_ADA009338)). The available evidence does not identify this as a “Behavioral Biology” report by title, although the WU 057 section explicitly discusses behavioral and biological microwave exposure effects and refers back to FY73 rat work ([DTIC ADA009338 full text](https://archive.org/stream/DTIC_ADA009338/DTIC_ADA009338_djvu.txt)).

The NSA National Cryptologic Memorial page was checked for “Marty Engels,” “Martin Engels,” and close variants, and the memorial list did not verify any Marty or Martin Engel(s) nexus ([NSA National Cryptologic Memorial](https://www.nsa.gov/History/National-Cryptologic-Memorial/)). The yearbook trail is also unresolved as stated because “Northwand High School” was not confirmed as a school name, while likely adjacent “NorthWood High School” yearbooks in Nappanee, Indiana were found for 1978 through 1981 on Internet Archive ([NorthWood 1978 yearbook](https://archive.org/details/0036_20240801), [NorthWood 1979 yearbook](https://archive.org/details/0008_20240801_202408), [NorthWood 1980 yearbook](https://archive.org/details/NorthwoodHSYearbook1980Images), [NorthWood 1981 yearbook](https://archive.org/details/NorthWoodHSYearbook1981Images_201507)).

The “Oscar 1980 follow-up studies” request remains ambiguous and no confirmed Brain Research peer-reviewed follow-up matching “Oscar 1980” was found in the available searches. Related microwave bioeffects leads were identified outside Brain Research, including James C. Lin work on microwave and blood-brain barrier interaction in Bioelectromagnetics and a 1980 microwave auditory phenomenon paper in Proceedings of the IEEE ([Wiley Bioelectromagnetics DOI](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/bem.2250010307), [IEEE microwave auditory phenomenon](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1455852/)).

## WRAIR WU 057 extracted report lead

DTIC ADA009338 is titled “Research in Biological and Medical Sciences Including Biochemistry, Communicable Disease and Immunology, Internal Medicine, Nuclear Medicine, Physiology, Psychiatry, Surgery, and Veterinary Medicine. Volume 2,” and it is an annual progress report for Walter Reed Army Institute of Research covering 1 July 1973 through 30 June 1974 ([Internet Archive DTIC ADA009338](https://archive.org/details/DTIC_ADA009338)). Within that volume, Work Unit 057 appears under Project 3A762758A824, Task 01, Radiation Injury and Protection, with the work-unit title “Biological effects and hazards of microwave radiation” ([DTIC ADA009338 full text](https://archive.org/stream/DTIC_ADA009338/DTIC_ADA009338_djvu.txt)).

The extracted section identifies Joseph C. Sharp, Ph.D. as principal investigator and lists H. Mark Grove, Sandra H. Githens, T. Daryl Hawkins, MAJ Stuart E. Hirsch, John H. Jacobi, John F. Schrot, and Peter Brown as associates ([DTIC ADA009338 full text](https://archive.org/stream/DTIC_ADA009338/DTIC_ADA009338_djvu.txt)). The section reports that FY74 work “systematize[d] the behavioral and biological effects of microwave irradiation” and corroborated earlier frequency-dependent behavioral effects, so it plausibly overlaps the user’s “behavioral biology” phrasing even though the official work-unit title is not “Behavioral Biology” ([DTIC ADA009338 full text](https://archive.org/stream/DTIC_ADA009338/DTIC_ADA009338_djvu.txt)).

The key experimental findings in the extracted section include mouse lethality studies at 2.45 GHz across 100, 150, 200, 300, and 400 mW/cm2, rat comparisons between 1.7 GHz and 2.45 GHz at 100 mW/cm2, and E/H vector orientation studies showing greater lethality when the E vector was parallel to the animal’s long body axis ([DTIC ADA009338 full text](https://archive.org/stream/DTIC_ADA009338/DTIC_ADA009338_djvu.txt)). The section also discusses pulsed microwave “hearing effect” work, blood-brain barrier observations that were investigated but not replicated to date, tissue dielectric measurements, current-awareness digests on nonionizing radiation bioeffects, and ocular studies in exposed Army personnel and rabbits ([DTIC ADA009338 full text](https://archive.org/stream/DTIC_ADA009338/DTIC_ADA009338_djvu.txt)).

The modern WRAIR Behavioral Biology page places Behavioral Biology under the Center for Military Psychiatry and Neuroscience and describes current research areas such as sleep, performance, stimulants, recuperative sleep, and acute stress interventions, but it does not list the historical WU 057 report ([WRAIR Behavioral Biology](https://wrair.health.mil/Biomedical-Research/Center-for-Military-Psychiatry-and-Neuroscience/Behavioral-Biology/)). The WRAIR finding aid identifies the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research Archives at the Gorgas Memorial Library and includes Series III Publications and Reports, including Research and Technology Work Unit Summaries dated 1978, but it does not explicitly list FY73, WU 057, or the “Biological effects and hazards of microwave radiation” section ([WRAIR Finding Aid](https://wrair.health.mil/Portals/87/Documents/WRAIR%20Finding%20Aid.pdf?ver=GlZOh4AUYb5Jxl-qVWCtUA%3D%3D)).

## NSA National Cryptologic Memorial check

The NSA National Cryptologic Memorial page states that the memorial honors personnel who gave their lives “serving in silence” and identifies the memorial as being located at the NSA headquarters complex ([NSA National Cryptologic Memorial](https://www.nsa.gov/History/National-Cryptologic-Memorial/)). A direct check of the memorial page did not verify “Marty Engels,” “Martin Engels,” “Marty Engel,” or close spelling variants as memorial entries ([NSA National Cryptologic Memorial](https://www.nsa.gov/History/National-Cryptologic-Memorial/)).

This is a negative verification, not a proof that no such person existed or had an intelligence-community connection. It only means the cited NSA memorial page does not support the claimed Marty Engels institutional nexus under the searched spellings ([NSA National Cryptologic Memorial](https://www.nsa.gov/History/National-Cryptologic-Memorial/)).

## Oscar 1980 Brain Research follow-up hunt

Searches for “Oscar 1980 Brain Research” did not identify a confirmed peer-reviewed follow-up study matching that phrasing. The most direct Brain Research lead returned was an Elsevier “Cumulative contents brain research reviews 1980” entry rather than a specific Oscar-related paper ([Elsevier Brain Research contents DOI](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/0006899380908598)).

Searches around microwave bioeffects, blood-brain barrier, and microwave auditory phenomenon produced adjacent peer-reviewed leads that align with topics mentioned in the WRAIR WU 057 extract, but they are not Brain Research articles and do not verify an “Oscar 1980” paper. The adjacent leads include “Studies on microwave and blood-brain barrier interaction” in Bioelectromagnetics and “The microwave auditory phenomenon” in Proceedings of the IEEE ([Wiley Bioelectromagnetics DOI](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/bem.2250010307), [IEEE microwave auditory phenomenon](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1455852/)).

The WU 057 publication trail also points to earlier microwave-acoustics and microwave applicator work by Sharp, Grove, Gandhi, Lenox, Meyerhoff, and Brown, but those leads are IEEE microwave engineering publications rather than Brain Research follow-up studies ([IEEE acoustic signals by pulsed microwave energy](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1128293/), [IEEE microwave applicator paper](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1128770/)). A separate Oscar Marin historical lead was not relevant to a 1980 Brain Research follow-up, because the accessible result concerns a 2015 historical paper rather than a 1980 Brain Research study ([PubMed Oscar Marin historical article](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26413738/)).

## Open gaps and recommended next actions

The WRAIR gap is best addressed by requesting the underlying WRAIR archives holdings rather than relying only on digitized DTIC text. The WRAIR finding aid points to the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research Archives at Gorgas Memorial Library and shows that Series III contains Publications and Reports, making it the most likely institutional route for FY73 predecessor or continuation files ([WRAIR Finding Aid](https://wrair.health.mil/Portals/87/Documents/WRAIR%20Finding%20Aid.pdf?ver=GlZOh4AUYb5Jxl-qVWCtUA%3D%3D)).

The yearbook gap requires either confirming the intended “Northwand” school identity or OCR-processing the 1978 and 1979 NorthWood image volumes. The current negative OCR result only covers the 1980 and 1981 NorthWood text that was available in OCR form ([NorthWood 1980 yearbook](https://archive.org/details/NorthwoodHSYearbook1980Images), [NorthWood 1981 yearbook](https://archive.org/details/NorthWoodHSYearbook1981Images_201507)).

The Oscar gap requires a disambiguator such as a full name, acronym expansion, paper title, lab name, DOI, or cited sentence from the source that mentioned “Oscar.” Without that disambiguator, the available searches do not support the existence of a specific “Oscar 1980” Brain Research follow-up study, although related microwave auditory and blood-brain barrier literature exists outside Brain Research ([Wiley Bioelectromagnetics DOI](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/bem.2250010307), [IEEE microwave auditory phenomenon](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1455852/)).
